{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Build sentences toward cosmic grandeur or philosophical profundity, then undercut them with the mundane, the bureaucratic, or the irritatingly trivial — this bathetic pivot is the foundational Adams move. Construct long, accumulating sentences that stack subordinate clauses, parenthetical asides, mock-scholarly footnote-like intrusions, and pseudo-technical explanations that spiral away from the original point before landing on an anticlimax the reader didn't see coming; alternate these with brisk declarative setups so the short sentence delivers the killing blow. Narrate with omniscient, editorializing authority — the narrator is a wry, slightly exhausted tour guide to an incomprehensible universe, freely offering sweeping generalizations that suddenly zoom into a petty human inconvenience. Treat the universe as fundamentally indifferent and bureaucratic; comedy emerges from intelligent beings confronting systems that are vast, stupid, and procedurally obsessed. Embrace parentheses, em-dashes, and lists that grow increasingly ridiculous, and use precise, overformal diction for ridiculous subjects — but never write pure whimsy, random zaniness, or sentimental warmth; the humor must feel intelligent, structurally controlled, and exact in its phrasing.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Characters speak in recognizably British registers — weary middle-class reasonableness (Arthur Dent), manic breezy confidence (Zaphod), prim passive-aggressive superiority (Marvin) — but all share a tendency to argue about the wrong thing at the wrong time with calm, grammatically composed insistence. Dialogue is frequently structured as two people having adjacent but non-intersecting conversations, each pursuing their own logical thread while missing the other's point entirely; characters articulate complaints with extraordinary precision and eloquence, especially about trivial matters, delivering three-sentence treatises on exactly how and why something is bad. Pepper speech with qualified hedges ("I suppose," "as it were," "one imagines") and sudden bursts of pedantic exactitude about irrelevant details; every line should feel as though the speaker is simultaneously apologizing for and marveling at the absurdity they are describing. Even aliens and robots speak with the cadence of someone who attended a mid-tier British university, mixing tea-and-crumpets formality with casual mentions of quantum mechanics or existential despair. Characters deflect sincerity through pedantry, sarcasm, or baffled understatement — never through emotionally raw vulnerability; their seriousness about absurd premises generates the comedy, not punchline-chasing or winking at the audience.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Characters sputter, repeat themselves, and restart sentences specifically when confronted with something so unreasonable that language temporarily fails them — Arthur Dent's "But— but— but—" when his house or planet is demolished is the archetypal pattern; this disfluency is the sound of common sense short-circuiting against an irrational universe. Use interruption sparingly but strategically: one clean fracture in a sentence is funnier than five — favor polite fumbling ("Well, no, that is to say—") over heavy phonetic stammering. Characters often begin rationally and then trail into bafflement as if language has declined to participate, or they keep talking past the point where they've lost their own thread, arriving at conclusions that surprise even themselves. Allow sudden parenthetical tangents to swallow the original point mid-sentence, and let characters realize what they are saying is ridiculous yet continue anyway with doomed momentum — the gap between thought and speech is filled with cosmic confusion rather than mere nervousness.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Accompany speech with physical details that emphasize helplessness and indignity: characters clutch dressing gowns, blink repeatedly at incomprehensible panels, pat pockets for impossible solutions, sag against walls, or stare with the expression of someone told something they were not remotely prepared to hear. The body must contradict or undercut the words — a character saying "Everything is fine" while physically trying to climb inside a cupboard, or making tea during an apocalypse, or straightening themselves with doomed dignity after teleportation. Use small, exact, economical gestures that are English in their restraint: tiny apologetic hand movements, polite nods that devolve into head-banging against walls, the slow polishing of non-existent spectacles, smiling with professional vacancy. Never write fluid, graceful, or melodramatic physical action — bodies in Adams's world are awkward, reluctant, slightly betrayed by gravity, and always doing something practical in precisely the wrong context.}}
