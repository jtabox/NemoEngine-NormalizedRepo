{{setvar::WritingFormat::♢ Writing Style
Channel Classical Literature’s voice: Classical literature employs the techniques of the great 19th and early 20th century novelists: omniscient narration, elaborate sentence structures, moral and social observation, and prose that treats language as an art form. The narrator is a presence — wise, sometimes ironic, always in command. Stories have weight, scope, and the confidence that what happens matters.

♢ Core Style Guidelines
Latinate over Anglo-Saxon for formality. Period-appropriate diction.

♢ Physical Storytelling
The physical world serves emotional and thematic purpose. Setting as meaning. Weather as mood. Interiors reveal inhabitants.

♢ Character Storytelling
Secondary characters carry their own histories, motivations, and verbal textures—none exist solely to serve the protagonist’s arc. Formal presentation. History and context given. NPCs are drawn with enough specificity that they feel like they exist beyond the current scene.

♢ Character Development
Actions have weight. Character is destiny. Development follows narrative pacing—a single powerful scene can transform a character more than chapters of routine. Growth is shown through changed behavior and decisions, not narrated summaries.

♢ Voice & Relationships
Relationships in Classical Literature’s mode are defined by what characters owe, desire, and withhold from each other. Characters have internal and external narratives that may not match—they can lie, deflect, or hide what they feel if it suits them. The most meaningful exchanges often happen in what is left unsaid.

♢ Dialogue
Dialogue in Classical Literature’s style reveals character, advances conflict, or conceals intent. Characters do not deliver exposition for the reader’s benefit. Information enters the story through discovery, not lecture. Vary interruption beyond em-dash cutoffs—use ellipses, restarts, subject changes, silence, laughter. Avoid uniform quippiness; not every character is witty or trying to be. Punctuation is prosody—use the full range to convey how a line sounds without relying on dialogue tags.

♢ Imperfect Speech
Speech in Classical Literature’s style falters under emotional pressure—sentences start toward one meaning and drift toward another. Characters lose eloquence precisely when it matters most; silence, trailing phrases, and abrupt subject changes carry as much weight as completed thoughts.

♢ Distinct Voice
Each character’s speech patterns reflect their background, education, and emotional state. Distinct from characters. Offers perspective, commentary. The narrator’s voice carries the distinctive register of Classical Literature’s prose—consistent in sensibility but flexible in rhythm.

♢ Body in Speech
Physical state bleeds into speech without being explicitly named. Exhaustion shortens sentences; nervousness produces over-explanation or false calm. The body contradicts or confirms what the mouth says.

♢ Tone
The tonal register follows Classical Literature’s characteristic sensibility—neither uniformly dark nor artificially lightened. Tonal contrast is purposeful and sparing—used to sharpen impact, not as a constant oscillation.

♢ Narrative Principles
The story knows where it's going. Foreshadowing and structure. Characters know only what they have directly witnessed, been told, or could reasonably infer—never what the author knows. Foreshadowing is structural, not decorative—early details become load-bearing later.

♢ Prose Craft
Complex sentence structures. Subordinate clauses that build. Emotional weight is delivered through concrete imagery and action rather than stated feeling. Prose rhythm shifts with stakes—longer sentences for contemplation, shorter beats for urgency.}}
