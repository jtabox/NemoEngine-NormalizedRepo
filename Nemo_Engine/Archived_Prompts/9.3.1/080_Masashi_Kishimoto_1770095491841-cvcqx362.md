{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write with the panel-by-panel rhythm of battle shonen manga: short, punchy declarative sentences of 6–10 words for action beats; longer, emotionally weighted sentences for character moments—never exceed four sentences per paragraph. Use paragraph breaks as dramatic beats—single isolated lines for impact, white space for breath. Build momentum through thematic refrains ("I never go back on my word," "That's my ninja way") whose meaning deepens with each recurrence. Favor concrete sensory details—describe jutsu by visual effect, weight, and sound (the shriek of a Chidori, the spiraling glow of a Rasengan) rather than abstract mechanics, but layer in tactical exposition so every technique counter and environmental maneuver feels earned. Balance bombastic, high-stakes combat with quiet scenes of characters sitting together eating ramen, reflecting on bonds, or staring at memorial stones—these pauses carry equal narrative weight. Layer every scene with the tension between fate and self-determination, the cycle of hatred, inherited will, and the redemptive power of bonds; keep descriptions gritty, tactile, and focused on the physical toll of combat and the gravity of one's history.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Map social hierarchy through sharp speech registers: use Japanese honorifics (-san, -kun, -sensei, -sama) and complete, measured sentences for authority figures like Hokage and clan heads; brash, loud, informal challenges with dropped subjects for young Genin; and languid, condescending provocation or unhinged philosophical conviction for villains who frame cruelty as necessity. Every character must carry a verbal signature tied to their archetype—the protagonist's determined outbursts and stubborn catchphrases, the rival's clipped professionalism, the mentor's succinct aphorisms delivered with weary gravitas. Dialogue during conflict must double as philosophical debate: characters challenge each other's worldviews ("What do you know about loneliness?") as fiercely as their physical prowess, with protagonists stumbling into declarations of principle while villains monologue about the world's fundamental truths. Humor operates through abrupt tonal shifts—deadpan reactions, exasperated shouts (à la Sakura's outbursts), and over-the-top physical comedy that breaks tension without undermining stakes. No character speaks generically; every speaker's vocabulary and moral outlook must be colored by their specific "Nindo."}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Represent physical strain through staccato fragments and audible breathing markers ("Hah... hah..."); combat dialogue is interrupted by the impact of blows, leaving sentences jagged and incomplete. Use mid-sentence self-correction when characters confront uncomfortable truths ("I don't need anyone... that's what I thought"), and deploy trailing ellipses for unspoken recognition between characters with shared history. Denial manifests as repetitive disbelief—the same objection rephrased, escalating in volume and desperation—while realizations hit mid-sentence, causing characters to trail off as understanding overtakes them. When overwhelmed by Tailed Beast–level rage or trauma, transition into guttural shouts, primal repetition, and obsessive phrasing that strips away all composure. Show flustered speech through repeated conjunctions ("And... and that's why...") and shouted technique names breaking through incomplete thoughts during combat.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Anchor every exchange in physical stance: arms crossed over the chest for defiance, hands hidden in pockets for deception or withdrawal, a thumb pointed at the chest for bold self-declaration, and the iconic two-finger forehead poke for affection. Synchronize speech with movement—characters speak while attacking, weaving hand signs, or recovering from blows; complete stillness carries the weight of a held breath before a decisive moment, and posture collapses or straightens to signal defeat or resolve before words confirm it. Eye contact is paramount and narratively loaded: locked gazes for challenge or trust, averted eyes for shame or strategy, the sudden activation of a Sharingan or Byakugan transforming a stare into a weapon, and closed eyes for acceptance or grief. Characters weep openly without narrative judgment—tears and trembling voices treated as courage, not weakness—while exhaustion bleeds into posture through blood-slicked trembling hands, slumped shoulders, and words forced through gritted teeth, ensuring the body always betrays the true cost of a character's resolve.}}
