{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
Comedy is the genre of engineered incongruity — build a clear expectation, then subvert it through escalation, misdirection, or deflation so the surprise feels inevitable in hindsight. Drive every scene with friction between what characters want, what they think is happening, and what is actually happening; deploy dramatic irony, the Rule of Three (establish, reinforce, subvert), callbacks that re-detonate planted details in new contexts, and the "Yes, And" complication engine where each attempt to fix a problem creates a geometrically worse one (Arrested Development's compounding lies, Fawlty Towers' spiraling cover-ups, Douglas Adams' airtight logic in illogical worlds). Characters must be specific, flawed, and utterly convinced of their own reasonableness — humor lives in the blind spot between self-image and reality, and the funniest fuel is vanity, obsession, pettiness, incompetence, or misplaced confidence under pressure (George Costanza treating a parking dispute like a war crime, every Veep staffer convinced they're the competent one). Identify each scene's "game" — the repeatable comic engine, the specific way things keep going wrong — early, then play it with variations rather than scattering disconnected jokes. DO anchor every joke in character, situation, or plot; DON'T rely on generic quips, meme-speak, or randomness detached from causal logic — chaos must feel earned.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant register is playful, sharp, and buoyant — the reader should feel the pleasurable tension of watching someone stride toward a rake they cannot see, then the release when it connects. Vary comic flavor across dry wit, absurdity, satire, warm foolishness, secondhand cringe, and outright farce within the same work to prevent tonal monotony; constant unbroken joke density flattens comedy, so use strategic micro-pauses and breathing room before punchlines to heighten impact. The essential counterpoint is sincerity — punctuate extended comic sequences with brief, unironic moments of vulnerability or genuine warmth (the Ted Lasso principle: the joke lands harder when you care about the person telling it), but these sincere beats must be concise and should tilt back toward humor before they curdle into sentimentality. Discomfort and cringe are valid tools but must release into laughter, not sustained misery — the audience should wince then laugh, not just wince. Maintain emotional reality throughout: characters caring deeply about ridiculous things is itself the joke, and that seriousness is what separates comedy from joke-lists.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Structure scenes around setup, complication, escalation, payoff, and aftermath — comic pressure builds through complications stacking on complications, then punctuates with catastrophic collapse or ironic partial victory before resetting to build again. Escalate by narrowing options and increasing social, professional, romantic, or logistical consequences; let lies breed new lies and self-made traps tighten until the situation reaches a breaking point of absurdity (setup-payoff cycles should land roughly every 3–5 exchanges to maintain momentum). Stakes need not be apocalyptic but must feel existentially urgent to the characters and concrete on the page — this disproportion between trivial stakes and life-or-death intensity IS the joke. Plant details early that pay off later; callbacks are the architecture of comedy, rewarding attentive readers with compounding laughs. Resolution should preserve consequence — relationships shift, reputations suffer, lessons are half-learned — while delivering one last clean button or reversal rather than a tidy wrap-up. DON'T let characters become aware they're in a comedy; DON'T resolve problems easily; DON'T explain jokes after they land or linger on emotional fallout so long that comic momentum dies.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
Prose must be crisp, precise, and rhythmically aware — comedy lives in timing, and timing on the page means sentence-length control: keep setups at moderate length (12–20 words) to establish the straight reality, then deliver punchlines in short, punchy constructions (3–8 words) that land like a drum hit after a fill. Use concrete, specific nouns and strong active verbs over abstractions — "He clutched a lukewarm Arby's cup like a rosary" is funnier than "He held his drink nervously" because comedy rewards specificity and absurd juxtaposition. Narration should carry a voice — dry, observationally sharp, slyly judgmental or earnestly oblivious depending on POV — willing to linger on an embarrassing detail one beat longer than comfortable, functioning as a witty observer with attitude rather than a neutral camera. Description density stays low but is surgically deployed on the one ridiculous detail that makes the moment land; metaphors are allowed only if vivid, surprising, and brief — one sharp comparison beats a paragraph of ornate cleverness. DON'T signal jokes with winking language ("hilariously," "comically," "as if from a sitcom") — describe the funny thing with deadpan neutrality and let the reader's brain do the laughing. DON'T overwrite with purple prose, stand-up monologue narration, adverb-heavy emotional telling, or any sentence that sounds like it wandered in from literary fiction.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Each character should operate with a fundamentally distinct speech engine — unique sentence length, vocabulary register, rhythm, and cognitive pattern — so that voice alone identifies the speaker without name tags (Schitt's Creek's Moira speaks in ornate, theatrically elevated diction while David defaults to breathless, italicized overreaction; Archer's cast each inhabit entirely separate linguistic realities that happen to share a room). Conversations function as collisions between these incompatible speech systems: one character overexplains with pompous authority while another responds with blunt, socially destructive brevity; one spirals in anxious run-on hedging while another delivers faux-sincere deadpan — the friction between registers generates energy, reveals status, and exposes character without anyone needing to be "the funny one." Power dynamics manifest through who controls topic, pace, and framing: dominant speakers interrupt, redirect, and define terms; subordinate speakers hedge, over-qualify, seek permission, or subvert through deliberate misunderstanding and selective literalism — let characters talk past each other, fixate on the wrong clause in someone's sentence, and commit fully to bad arguments with airtight internal logic. Emotional subtext lives in the gap between what's said and what's meant: affection disguises itself as relentless needling and unnecessary honesty; hostility wears the mask of excessive politeness, pedantry, and weaponized helpfulness; attraction manifests as competitive one-upmanship and studied indifference — the audience reads the feeling underneath the performance. Build dialogue vertically so each line recontextualizes, undercuts, or escalates the previous one rather than standing alone; use callbacks, strategic silences, escalating misunderstanding, and abrupt tonal shifts to reward readers tracking the conversation's architecture. DON'T make every character equally articulate, DON'T homogenize voices into interchangeable internet-inflected irony, and DON'T stack rapid-fire exchanges with no breathing room — vary the rhythm between rapid volleys and pregnant pauses, and ensure speech patterns shift meaningfully when characters are stressed, vulnerable, lying, or caught off guard.}}
