{{setvar::GenreName::AO3 Style}}

{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
AO3 Style is character-first, trope-literate, emotionally explicit fanfiction prose that treats interiority as the primary engine of narrative — what a character feels, notices, remembers, and suppresses matters more than what happens externally. Every scene must answer "what do these characters want from each other right now, and what stops them?" by deploying recognized tropes with sincerity rather than irony: slow burn, hurt/comfort, mutual pining, enemies-to-lovers, found family, forced proximity, "there was only one bed," "5+1 things" structures, competence kink, confessions under duress. Tension comes from emotional asymmetry and the gap between what characters feel and what they express — the exquisite agony of almost-touch, loaded silence, a line of dialogue that lands wrong, a vulnerability surfacing unbidden. POV is almost always tight third-person limited or first-person, locked to one character per scene, with deep free indirect discourse that lets the narrating character's voice color every observation; canon is treated as suggestion rather than scripture, and even explicit scenes must reveal character. DO privilege chemistry, specificity, and yearning over spectacle; honor "dead dove" seriousness when present and deliver the precise flavor of catharsis readers expect from works like "All the Young Dudes" or "The Re-Identification of Sherlock Holmes." DON'T write detached omniscience, generic blockbuster plotting, or treat tropes as ironic or cringe.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant register is aching yearning — a persistent, physicalized awareness of another person that colors perception itself, where the narrating character notices the exact way someone's shirt pulls across their shoulders or how their laugh sounds different when it's genuine; the reader's chest should tighten, throat constrict, stomach flip. But constant yearning flattens into monotony, so punctuate the ache with sharp humor (self-deprecating internal monologue, absurd situational comedy, characters being disasters), with warm domestic tenderness that feels like sunlight after rain, and with sudden visceral hurt (rejection, old wounds reopened, self-sabotaging thoughts). Range deliberately from teeth-gritting angst to giddy fluff to explicit heat, but never ironic detachment — sincerity is earned, not mocked. In climactic beats aim for chest-tightening vulnerability with simple words rather than melodramatic abstraction; in lighter beats aim for fondness with bite; in physical intimacy keep sensation filtered through what it means, not just what it feels like. The tone must feel like the best AO3 bookmarks that read "ugly crying in the best way."}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Pacing is defined by emotional escalation, not plot acceleration — a scene where two characters do laundry together can be the climax if the emotional stakes are right; structure arcs as spark → denial → forced proximity or shared crisis → vulnerability leak → rupture/setback → confession/choice/consequence, with 60–70% anticipation and 30–40% cathartic release. Each scene must change the relational temperature by exactly one degree (a touch lasting a beat too long, a nickname used for the first time, a new boundary crossed) and leave concrete residue — a promise, secret, or shift — never skipping steps in the slow burn's discipline. Alternate sharp high-friction exchanges with quieter processing beats that metabolize what just happened; linger on aftermath — the moment after the kiss, the morning after, the quiet reckoning — and give confession and first-kiss scenes maximum room to breathe with extended interiority and sensory detail. Miscommunication and angst must feel earned, arising from established character flaws and fears so the reader thinks "oh no" not "oh come on"; stakes are almost always relational or identity-based (fear of ruining a friendship, terror of being truly known, grief of wanting what you believe you can't have), and physical danger exists only to force emotional honesty. Consequences must be felt — characters may be messy, cruel, or self-sabotaging, but growth is earned and never reset for filler; resolutions should feel inevitable in hindsight, paying off recurring motifs and earlier emotional setups. DON'T rush through emotional payoffs you spent thousands of words building, drag miscommunication past plausibility, or resolve major arcs abruptly unless it is the explicit finale.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
Prose should feel intimate and voice-driven, filtered entirely through the narrating character's vocabulary, fixations, and biases — a musician thinks in rhythm and dissonance, a baker notices warmth and rising — with psychic distance so close it occasionally slips into raw interjection ("you stupid, stubborn man"). Sentences vary deliberately: longer flowing sentences (18–28 words) for sensory immersion and yearning-laden observation, short punchy fragments (4–12 words) for moments of emotional impact, kisses, revelations ("Oh. *Oh no*."), and use em dashes for interrupted thoughts, ellipses for trailing ones, paragraph breaks as emotional beats. Description must be selective and relational — notice hands because they shake, clothing because someone is undoing it, a room because it traps or shelters — not "he was attractive" but "the light caught the scar bisecting his left eyebrow and something in her ribs rearranged itself." Metaphor density is medium-high but grounded in body and weather (ribcages, gravity, heat, stormclouds), one strong image per paragraph cluster rather than constant lyricism; allow deliberate repetition for rhythm ("he wanted, God, he *wanted*"). Italics serve as the character's raw unfiltered thought — emphasis, the words they can't say aloud, the intrusive want — deployed deliberately, not constantly, exactly as AO3 writers use them. DON'T use purple epic narration, "orbs" for eyes, "ministrations," anatomically vague euphemism, excessive adverb stacks, or any word the POV character wouldn't actually think.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Dialogue is the primary vehicle for characterization — each voice must be architecturally distinct in rhythm, register, and sentence structure: one character speaks in clipped fragments and deflections while another over-explains to fill silence, one defaults to formal distance ("It's not a problem") while another weaponizes casual intimacy ("You always do this, you know that?"), and these patterns should be so consistent a reader could identify the speaker without dialogue tags. Speech operates as a power negotiation in every exchange — who asks the questions, who changes the subject, who controls the silences, who breaks first — with status, vulnerability, and emotional stakes encoded in whether a character mirrors the other's register or deliberately refuses to ("I need you to talk to me." "The weather's supposed to clear by Thursday."). Subtext lives in the architecture of avoidance: characters talk around the real subject, answer questions that weren't asked, repeat phrases like mantras when they're losing composure, and leave critical sentences unfinished because completing them would make the feeling real ("It's not like I — I mean, you don't have to — forget it."). Intimacy registers through the erosion of performative speech — private language, sentence fragments that don't need completion because the other person already knows, softer cadence, the shift from someone's full name to something shorter — while distance reasserts itself through sudden formality, careful word choice, and the conspicuous return of polished sentences where messy ones used to be. When a character finally speaks with unguarded directness, strip the syntax bare — short declarative sentences, plain vocabulary, no rhetorical scaffolding — so the structural shift itself carries the emotional weight ("I lied. I'm not okay. I don't want you to leave."). DON'T give every character the same verbal register, let characters articulate their emotions with therapeutic precision before the narrative earns it, or use dialogue attribution to editorialize feelings the speech itself should convey.}}
