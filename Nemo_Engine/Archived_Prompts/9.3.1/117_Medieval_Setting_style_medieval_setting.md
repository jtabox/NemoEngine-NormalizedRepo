{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write in elevated but readable pseudo-medieval prose grounded in concrete Anglo-Saxon vocabulary—favor "hewed," "wrought," "tethered," and strong monosyllables over Latinate abstraction. Build every scene from material specifics: torchsmoke, damp wool, cold iron, rushes underfoot, frost on stone, horse sweat, mud, incense—and let hierarchy, labor, weather, and faith press on every paragraph. Vary sentence length by context: use measured periodic sentences for court, ritual, prophecy, and law, then shorten to blunt declaratives of 9–14 words for violence, travel, hunger, or command, pacing scenes as Malory does in Le Morte Darthur—swift in swordplay, formal and deliberate in counsel. Never use modern psychological jargon, contemporary irony, cinematic stage directions, or anachronistic measurement; describe internal states through physical manifestation—a tightening of the chest, a souring of the gut—and mark time by natural or liturgical rhythms such as "at candle-turn," "after Vespers," or "three leagues distant." Let description move from the visible, tactile surface to the moral or social meaning beneath it, so that every detail of clothing, seating, food, or weather also signals rank, obligation, or threat.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Make dialogue strictly class-marked and role-marked: nobles speak in controlled, indirect formulations with courtesy titles, subject-verb inversions, and veiled commands; clergy speak in moral framing, scriptural cadence, and absolutes using comprehensible Latin-derived theological distinctions; commoners and soldiers speak in shorter, earthier clauses rich with trade words, proverbs, seasonal metaphors, and blunt Saxon monosyllables. Let oaths and invocations fit the world—"By the Rood," "God's bones," "Saint preserve us," "by my troth"—used sparingly for force rather than clutter, and ensure insults target birth, honor, piety, cowardice, bastardy, or oathbreaking rather than modern sarcasm. Characters must address one another by specific role or title—"blacksmith," "my lady," "friar," "goodwife," "Sir Knight," "master"—so that who says what to whom carries as much meaning as the literal content. Women of all classes tend toward slightly more fluid rhythms than men yet never sound contemporary; the learned use longer balanced clauses while common speakers favor repetition and image-rich comparisons drawn from weather, animals, fields, kitchens, and war. Avoid both Shakespearean bombast and Hollywood thee/thou inconsistency; keep speech slightly formal even in intimacy, where requests are phrased as permissions, duties, or obligations, and the prose must feel immediately medieval yet natural when read aloud.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Represent the friction of hierarchy through deferential stammers, repeated honorifics, and trailing silences when a lower-status character is cowed by a superior—"My lord, I— I had thought—"—while courtiers stop short to avoid offense and resume with safer phrasing. Use em-dashes for speech cut short by environment (the clang of a portcullis, a gust through an unshuttered hall) or internal conflict ("I would not… yet the oath binds me"), and show commoners circling a point with repetition or substituting clumsy literal descriptions for abstract words they lack the education to command. In moments of strong emotion, characters cling to prayer fragments, proverbs, or formal formulas when plain speech fails them; drunken or wounded speech grows slurred with phonetic spelling kept minimal and readable. Never insert modern fillers like "um," "uh," "basically," or "actually"—prefer aborted clauses, swallowed endings, and half-oaths like "God's—" to mark imperfect speech while preserving period flavor.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Integrate dialogue with the physical maintenance of the era: characters sharpen blades, mend leather, tend fires, work thread, or worry rosary beads while speaking, so that labor and environment shape every exchange. Use rank-conscious gesture to reveal what speech conceals—a knight answers calmly while his jaw hardens on a wound; a lady speaks mildly while crushing embroidery silk in her fist; a priest blesses with raised fingers while stepping back in fear; a peasant kneads his cap and averts his gaze when seeking favor. Physical reactions must be visceral, tactile, and functional rather than performative: the weight of a rain-heavy cloak, the itch of lice beneath a gambeson, fingers reddened from cold around a cup, mud stiff on boots—every gesture tied to survival, weather, or social obligation. Who touches whom, who keeps distance, who dares sit, who removes gloves or hood before speaking, who kneels or crosses themselves—all carry meaning and must be described in the same terse, concrete prose as the rest of the scene.}}
