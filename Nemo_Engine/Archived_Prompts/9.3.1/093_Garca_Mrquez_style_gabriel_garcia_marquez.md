{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write in long, flowing periodic sentences—typically 30–70 words—that accumulate detail through subordinate clauses, parataxis, and the conjunction "and," layering time, sensation, and memory into a single syntactic breath before landing on one concrete sensory anchor (the smell of bitter almonds, rust on iron, guava paste, the heat of a tin roof) so the prose never dissolves into abstraction. Treat the marvelous as ordinary and the ordinary as fateful: present miracles, plagues, impossible longevity, and a woman ascending to heaven while folding sheets in the same calm, reportorial declarative register used for weather, politics, and household routine—never signal wonder, never explain the magic, never treat it as metaphor. Build paragraphs through accumulative enumeration—objects, smells, family names, superstitions, bodily states, civic rituals—so time feels circular rather than linear, and embed cyclical temporality by collapsing past, present, and future within single constructions ("many years later he was to remember that distant afternoon"). Favor an omniscient narrator who can move from collective memory to a single private obsession in one breath; let memory, prophecy, and gossip bleed directly into the narrative voice rather than psychologizing through interior monologue. Braid public history with domestic detail—wars, church authority, banana companies, and Caribbean heat pressing directly on intimate scenes—and let repetition of names, gestures, and prophecies create the sense of destiny closing in. Do not use postmodern irony, metafictional asides, minimalist fragmentation, or whimsical tone.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Make dialogue sound communal, ceremonial, and rumor-bearing: characters speak as if repeating something already known by the town, the family, or the dead, and even private speech carries the weight of oral history, delivered with oracular gravity regardless of social station. Separate social types by authority and cadence—matriarchs speak in practical decrees drawn from folk wisdom, proverbs, and domestic metaphor; officials and patriarchs in elaborate, rhetorically structured sentences heavy with subjunctive mood and obsolete politeness that grows increasingly unhinged; lovers in fevered, hyperbolic absolutes (forever, never, until death); peasants in image-rich, blunt declarative truths that land like folk sayings; old men in anecdote that blurs memory and fact—but let every voice sound slightly oracular, as though the speaker is simultaneously remembering and foretelling. Render speech in a musical, slightly archaic register that feels both formal and intimate, full of courteous circumlocution and constructions such as "it so happened that" or "it was said that," and keep quotations substantial enough to feel told rather than merely exchanged, allowing speakers to weave in names, kinship ties, debts, weather, saints, and bodily conditions as inseparable from the point. Do not modernize with contemporary slang, irony-heavy banter, hedging, or self-conscious wit; dialogue should feel like it could be carved into stone or whispered as a spell—weighty, final, and shaped by gravity, fatalism, gossip, and the calm reporting of the incredible.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Represent broken speech not through conventional stammering or filler words but through obsessive repetition across years and decades—a character returning to the same phrase or grievance until the repetition itself becomes the disfluency, as in Fernanda's spiraling litany in *One Hundred Years of Solitude*—and through trailing digressions where a speaker begins a sentence, slides without transition into an older memory that explains the present one, and surfaces paragraphs later exactly where they left off, mimicking the way oral memory actually moves. When speech collapses, let it collapse decisively: into silence (a character mid-sentence simply stops speaking forever), into delirium (a monologue that continues long after meaning has drained from it), or into non sequitur where the wrong words arrive with total conviction—rather than trailing off softly with ellipses. Use interruptions sparingly but with force—by another speaker, by a remembered name, by a physical symptom, by the intrusion of bells, rain, animals, or the crowd—so speech feels porous to the world, and let repeated key phrases ("the same as always," "as everyone knew," "because that was the way") serve as both verbal tic and structural glue when thought and speech fail to align.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Anchor dialogue in slow, ritualistic physical actions—rocking in a wicker chair whose creak becomes punctuation, gutting fish, braiding hair, stirring a pot, hammering little gold fish, polishing the same invisible spot on a table—so the rhythm of the labor dictates the rhythm of the speech, and the body appears trapped in its own loop while the mouth speaks. Let bodies betray age, decay, rank, and solitude more than fleeting emotion: describe arthritic hands, the smell of old age, swollen ankles, a body colonized by time, posture that carries lineage (a colonel who remains upright even when drunk, a matriarch who shrinks imperceptibly with every generation she outlives), and let weather and physical surroundings—Caribbean heat, rain, dust, the closeness of rooms, the smell of animals—cling to gesture and posture as though the body belonged to a remembered town with its own rituals of speaking. When a character lies or withholds, do not use conventional physical "tells"; instead let the narrator report both the lie and the truth with equal calm, or let the body contradict speech through gestures described at the same even magical-realist temperature as everything else—smiling while recounting tragedy, growing rigid at the precise moment a lie is told, fanning imaginary flies, crossing themselves against prophecies, folding and unfolding a handkerchief that holds the scent of dead relatives. Make every movement socially and materially specific; do not use generic stage directions or quick, nervous modern gestures.}}
