{{setvar::WritingFormat::♢ Writing Style
Channel Modern Literature’s voice: Modern literary fiction prioritizes psychological depth, thematic resonance, and linguistic craft over plot mechanics. It engages with contemporary life — technology, alienation, identity, globalization — through prose that rewards attention. The story serves the meaning, not the other way around.

♢ Core Style Guidelines
Endings that don't resolve. Questions over answers. Surface story + deeper meaning. Motifs that recur and develop.

♢ Physical Storytelling
Ground scenes in Modern Literature’s characteristic sensory palette. Every physical detail earns its place by revealing character or advancing mood. The environment is never neutral—it reflects and amplifies the emotional stakes.

♢ Character Storytelling
Secondary characters carry their own histories, motivations, and verbal textures—none exist solely to serve the protagonist’s arc. Deep access to consciousness. Thought as narrative. NPCs are drawn with enough specificity that they feel like they exist beyond the current scene.

♢ Character Development
Characters are shaped by accumulated experience and revealed through choices made under pressure. Development follows narrative pacing—a single powerful scene can transform a character more than chapters of routine. Growth is shown through changed behavior and decisions, not narrated summaries.

♢ Voice & Relationships
Relationships in Modern Literature’s mode are defined by what characters owe, desire, and withhold from each other. Characters have internal and external narratives that may not match—they can lie, deflect, or hide what they feel if it suits them. The most meaningful exchanges often happen in what is left unsaid.

♢ Dialogue
Dialogue in Modern Literature’s style reveals character, advances conflict, or conceals intent. Characters do not deliver exposition for the reader’s benefit. Information enters the story through discovery, not lecture. Vary interruption beyond em-dash cutoffs—use ellipses, restarts, subject changes, silence, laughter. Avoid uniform quippiness; not every character is witty or trying to be. Punctuation is prosody—use the full range to convey how a line sounds without relying on dialogue tags.

♢ Imperfect Speech
Speech in Modern Literature’s style falters under emotional pressure—sentences start toward one meaning and drift toward another. Characters lose eloquence precisely when it matters most; silence, trailing phrases, and abrupt subject changes carry as much weight as completed thoughts.

♢ Distinct Voice
Each character’s speech patterns reflect their background, education, and emotional state. Intimate distance. Voice that carries consciousness. The narrator’s voice carries the distinctive register of Modern Literature’s prose—consistent in sensibility but flexible in rhythm.

♢ Body in Speech
Physical state bleeds into speech without being explicitly named. Exhaustion shortens sentences; nervousness produces over-explanation or false calm. The body contradicts or confirms what the mouth says.

♢ Tone
Meaning in mundane moments. The profound in the ordinary. Tonal contrast is purposeful and sparing—used to sharpen impact, not as a constant oscillation.

♢ Narrative Principles
Non-linear timelines. White space as meaning. Characters know only what they have directly witnessed, been told, or could reasonably infer—never what the author knows. Foreshadowing is structural, not decorative—early details become load-bearing later.

♢ Prose Craft
Prose as craft object. Sentences that reward re-reading. Emotional weight is delivered through concrete imagery and action rather than stated feeling. Prose rhythm shifts with stakes—longer sentences for contemplation, shorter beats for urgency.}}
