{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
Romance is the genre of closing distance — psychological, physical, emotional — where every scene must advance, complicate, or threaten the central relationship; if a scene does not measurably change how the characters feel about each other, it has failed. The engine is emotional intimacy under pressure: attraction, resistance, vulnerability, misreading, sacrifice, and choice, with tension generated through the gap between what characters want and what they'll admit, powered by internal wounds, incompatible goals, fear of vulnerability, or social constraint — never by external plot alone. Deploy concrete romance mechanics (forced proximity, enemies-to-lovers sparring, only one bed, caretaking after injury, the dark moment, the earned declaration) as causally motivated beats rooted in character psychology rather than checklist ornaments. Show desire through observable behavior — lingering attention, memorized details, protective impulses resisted, changed habits, conversational attunement — so attraction feels cumulative rather than announced. Center consent, reciprocity, and emotional consequence; don't confuse cruelty or arbitrary secrecy with tension unless the story explicitly interrogates and transforms them.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant register is *ache tempered by warmth* — the reader's chest should tighten during longing, pulse quicken during proximity, and breath catch when a wall finally comes down, with scenes calibrated to feel like standing on the edge of a kiss that hasn't happened yet. Counterpoint is non-negotiable: thread sharp humor to release pressure, genuine frustration to prove the characters have edges, and quiet melancholy to give joy something to stand against — pure sweetness collapses into saccharine, uninterrupted angst becomes numbing. The tonal arc of any romance scene follows the pattern: safety → risk → vulnerability → either reward or retreat, with the ratio shifting toward reward as the story approaches resolution. The emotional range spans giddy hope, aching vulnerability, jealous heat, quiet devastation, and euphoric relief; never stay in any single register longer than two consecutive beats, and let sensual tension simmer beneath even mundane interactions so that a hand brushing a hand while passing a coffee cup lands harder than an explicit scene when the emotional groundwork is laid.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Pacing is a spiral — characters circle each other with "two steps forward, one step back" rhythm, alternating high-tension near-misses (almost-kisses, almost-confessions, interrupted declarations) with slower scenes of deepening intimacy, so that every major emotional beat is *earned* through preceding tension and a first kiss means nothing if the reader hasn't spent thousands of words wanting it. Structure around escalating intimacy and escalating risk: attraction, complication, tentative connection, setback, deeper vulnerability, dark moment, active choice, and emotional payoff — with each scene ending on an emotional pivot (a revelation, a boundary crossed, a fresh obstacle) that changes the relationship state in a measurable way. The cardinal sin is resolving tension too early or through external convenience rather than character growth; the obstacle to love must ultimately be *internal*, and resolution must require the character to change, choose, or sacrifice — never through grand gestures alone but through demonstrated capacity to sustain love via changed behavior and addressed wounds. Avoid the "idiot plot" where conflict depends on characters simply not talking; miscommunication is permitted only when the *inability* to speak is psychologically credible and rooted in established character wounds (fear of abandonment, loss of identity, worthiness), and consequences of romantic choices must linger across multiple scenes. The genre requires an emotionally credible resolution — HEA or HFN — where both characters have actively chosen each other with full knowledge of the other's flaws.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
Prose is body-literate: track pulses, breath patterns, skin temperature, the tightness of a jaw, the involuntary lean toward someone, the tiny observations people make when trying not to stare — anchored in close POV and immediate physical-emotional experience, never omniscient swooning. Vary sentence length by beat: 4–8 words during near-kisses, confessions, or moments of shock (staccato, breathless), 12–18 words for steady emotional observation, and 20–30 words for reflective interiority where a character processes what just happened. Metaphor should be grounded in the physical, specific, and tied to character worldview — not "her heart soared" but "something behind her ribs unlocked, a door she'd welded shut at seventeen" — with never more than one strong image per paragraph and zero purple archaisms. Use concrete sensory detail (the specific cologne, the exact sound of someone's laugh, the texture of a shirt sleeve gripped too tightly) and favor precise vocabulary ("pulse," "flush," "ache," "trace," "unravel") over stock terms. Anti-patterns: "orbs" for eyes, "velvet voice," "heart beating as one," weather-as-love metaphors, excessive adverbs, and any narration that tells an emotion showable through micro-action — a character falling in love doesn't think "I'm falling in love"; they notice they've memorized the other person's coffee order without trying.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Dialogue in romance operates on two simultaneous registers — the surface utterance and the subterranean want — and the distance between them IS the erotic charge; characters reveal desire not through declaration but through vocal texture shifts, the way someone's sentence structure loosens after a glass of wine, the way a formal "you" softens into a first name, the way a breath reshapes a phrase into something it wasn't meant to be. Speech rhythms between romantic leads should develop a private cadence — interruptions that feel like harmony rather than aggression, sentences one character begins that the other finishes, the gradual erosion of performative register into unguarded plainness, as in the way Austen's Darcy moves from stilted formality to raw confession or the way Before Sunrise builds intimacy through increasingly personal questions disguised as philosophical ones. Power in romantic dialogue is fluid and asymmetric: one character controls the conversation's direction while the other controls its emotional depth, and these roles should reverse across scenes — track who asks questions versus who deflects, who fills silence versus who weaponizes it, who uses precise vocabulary as armor versus who strips language bare as offering. Subtext manifests through specific, trackable techniques: displaced concern (arguing about a jacket when meaning "I'm terrified you'll leave"), echoed phrasing that signals deep listening, vocabulary borrowed from the other person's speech patterns like an unconscious adoption of their worldview, and the incremental migration from safe topics to dangerous ones — each personal question a threshold crossed. Anti-patterns to avoid: characters who monologue their feelings in therapy-speak rather than revealing emotion through resistance and fracture; dialogue interchangeable between any two attractive people rather than rooted in each character's class, education, region, profession, and emotional defense structure; and hostility in enemies-to-lovers that reads as genuinely cruel rather than charged with the specific attentiveness that only obsession produces. When characters finally speak their truth, mark the shift materially — shorter sentences, simpler words, the rhythm slowing as if each syllable costs something — so that the move from guarded speech to unprotected honesty lands like the sound of armor hitting stone.}}
