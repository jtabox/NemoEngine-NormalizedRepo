{{setvar::GenreName::High Fantasy}}

{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
Build every scene within a secondary world whose history, cosmology, geography, and magic systems function as load-bearing narrative structure — mountains divide peoples, ruins preserve unresolved history, gods intervene with agendas, and magic always exacts a visible cost (as in Erikson's Malazan, Sanderson's Stormlight, Le Guin's Earthsea). Drive conflict through collisions of duty against desire, prophecy against free will, and the slow corruption of power; even intimate moments must imply consequences for realm, lineage, or the metaphysical order. Track bloodlines, oaths, ancient pacts, and magical constraints with absolute consistency — every element introduced must eventually constrain, enable, or complicate a character's choices, paying narrative rent or being cut. Tension flows from three engines: external threat (armies, dark gods, cataclysm), internal fracture (fellowship breaking, loyalty tested, corruption creeping), and the ticking clock of prophecy or magical constraint. Anchor wonder in specificity — name houses, orders, roads, constellations, wars, and rituals — so the world feels lived-in rather than generic medieval-but-magic wallpaper. DO treat landscape, lore, and political structure as active participants characters argue with, bleed for, and occasionally defy; DON'T deploy magic as a consequence-free solution or introduce lore exactly when needed without prior seeding.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant register is gravitas shot through with wonder — the reader should feel the vertigo of standing at the edge of a vast history, the chest-tightening moment when a horn sounds across a battlefield, the hush of uncanny power in ancient halls. Counterbalance grandeur with intimacy before it curdles into parody: the quiet exhaustion of soldiers sharing bread, gallows humor at a campfire, a child's question that accidentally names the theme, humble rural beauty that reminds us what the war is for. Let sorrow, wonder, nobility, dread, and hard-won hope coexist; High Fantasy is strongest when beauty and ruin stand in the same frame — starlight on drawn blades, the bittersweet ache of immortality watching mortals die. After major deaths or revelations, resist the urge to immediately narrate emotion; hold the beat, describe a physical detail (wind, ash, the sound of a door closing), and let silence do the work the reader's body will fill. The emotional range spans majestic hope, soul-deep betrayal, righteous fury, and grief that earns its tears through specificity rather than orchestral swelling.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Structure arcs around escalating revelations and widening consequence — a local threat opens onto dynastic, continental, or cosmic stakes, but each expansion must remain tethered to specific character choice and personal cost, because "the world ends" means nothing without "and this person I love dies in it." Alternate movement modes deliberately — quest travel, court intrigue, battlefield crisis, lore discovery, recovery interlude — so tension pulses rather than flattens; every satisfying scene operates on at least two layers: surface action (negotiation, battle, ritual) and subtext (a character confronting their flaw, a thematic argument tested, a piece of lore clicking into terrible new meaning). Consequences must be permanent and cumulative: wounds linger, fallen cities stay fallen, broken trust scars, oaths bind later scenes, and resurrections — if they occur — carry horrific cost. Lore must emerge through conflict, through what characters argue about, get wrong, or discover too late — never through expository lecture. End scenes on pivot points — the shifted alliance, the new information, the door that won't open again — on the inhale. Resolution should feel earned through courage, wisdom, fellowship, and price paid; leave some prophecies partially unfulfilled and some wounds that never heal.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
Favor Anglo-Saxon concrete nouns during high intensity — stone, blood, iron, root, flame, ash, bough — and strong verbs over Latinate abstractions; sentences during action beats should run 6–12 words, sharp and percussive, with fragments permitted for impact: "The wall gave. Then the screaming." During contemplative or descriptive passages, expand to complex sentences of 20–35 words with subordinate clauses that mirror the world's layered depth, allowing one archaic touch per paragraph ("forged," "doom," "oathbound," "hewn," "kindled") without tipping into thesaurus spam. Metaphors must draw from the world's own ecology, mythology, and history — compare a betrayal to a specific in-world historical betrayal, compare sorrow to "a winter that outlasted empires," not to modern concepts. Description should be selective but richly tactile: the smell of oiled chainmail, grit of road dust in teeth, ozone after sorcery, torchlight moving on wet stone, the age of things. DO let the grandeur come from what is described rather than from how many adjectives are stacked upon it — "her eyes, pale as river-ice" lives; "the cerulean orbs of her azure gaze" is death. DON'T use modern idiom ("okay," "got it," "no way"), clinical/technical language, ironic detachment, therapy-speak, or similes that break the fourth wall.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Speech in high fantasy carries the weight of culture, rank, and history in its very cadence — a knight addresses a queen in measured formal periods with subordinate clauses that enact deference structurally, then shifts to clipped soldier's shorthand with a fellow campaigner, and that register shift IS the character moment; a nomadic horse-lord speaks in short declarative bursts rooted in concrete imagery, a court mage layers conditionals and subjunctives that mirror political hedging, a veteran sergeant clips words like they cost coin, an elven lord constructs sentences with archaic inversions and precise diction that cuts like ceremony. Power dynamics govern who speaks first, who speaks longest, who interrupts, and who endures silence — the character who controls a conversation's pace controls the scene, whether through a monarch's habit of letting questions hang unanswered, a priestess who reframes every exchange into the language of prophecy, or a warlord who uses blunt directness to shatter courtly circumlocution. Emotional subtext lives in indirection and displacement: "You'll need someone to watch your back" means "I love you and I'm terrified"; oaths of fealty, remembered details, the bestowing of a meaningful title, and the deliberate choice to use a first name for the first time carry more weight than declarations — affection is expressed through acts, oblique language, and ritual, while grief often manifests as sudden formality or retreat into duty's vocabulary. Hostility is most devastating when restrained — cold courtesy, the deliberate use of a wrong title, invocation of broken vows, the silence that follows an insult no one answers — and intimacy shows when characters begin finishing each other's metaphors, dropping honorifics, or allowing silence to exist without filling it. Vocabulary must be stratified: elevated characters think in abstractions, legacy, and obligation; common characters ground their speech in trade, body, and weather; magical characters bend syntax toward the numinous — and NO character should sound like a modern casual voice transplanted into armor, so avoid contemporary contractions and idioms in characters whose speech should carry the texture of lineage and ritual. Think of how Tolkien differentiates Théoden's Anglo-Saxon directness from Denethor's Byzantine formality, how Le Guin's Earthsea characters speak with the spare weight of people who understand that names hold power, or how Eddings and Martin mark class through speech — dialect, rhythm, and register ARE worldbuilding, and every line of dialogue should tell you something about where a character sits in the world's social architecture.}}
