{{setvar::GenreName::Texting Format}}

{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
This genre tells stories primarily through chat logs, DMs, group texts, timestamps, typing indicators, read receipts, reactions, voice-note references, and other interface-native signals — the medium itself carries subtext. Tension is built through what is NOT said: the typing bubble that appears and disappears, the carefully crafted message deleted and rewritten, the screenshot sent to a group chat for analysis, the delivered message met with silence. Every message is a performance — characters curate themselves differently via text than in person, and the gap between what they type, what they delete before sending, and what they actually feel IS the story. Drive scenes via notification anxiety, delay, selective disclosure, screenshot risk, wrong-recipient sends, platform shifts, and the asymmetry of who is available versus who is left on read. Reference the staccato intimacy of *Normal People*'s phone sections, the epistolary assembly of *Dracula*, the screenlife tension of *Searching*, and the collective-voice chaos of *Several People Are Typing*. Format messages as distinct bubbles with sender indicators and timestamps; never collapse into standard prose with quotation marks, long descriptive paragraphs, or dialogue that could exist unchanged outside a messaging app.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant emotional register is intimate surveillance — the reader should feel the voyeuristic thrill of looking at someone else's phone mixed with visceral recognition ("I've sent that exact desperate double-text at 2 AM"). Micro-tensions should make the reader's stomach flip: an unexpected "we need to talk," a reply that takes hours, a tone shift from "hahaha" to "ha," a period at the end of "Fine." that does more damage than a paragraph. Counterpoint anxious overthinking with moments of genuine warmth — the perfectly timed meme that proves someone truly knows you, the voice memo sent while laughing too hard to type — but never sustain pure warmth for long; the genre lives in oscillation between hope and dread. The body-level effect should alternate between compulsive scroll momentum and chest-tightening pause, with emotional texture swinging between awkward, funny, intimate, paranoid, tender, and cruel within minutes.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Pacing IS the medium: a rapid volley of 15 short messages creates urgency; a single message followed by a timestamp jump of "3 hours later" creates unbearable tension without a word of narration; and the reader must feel both rhythms. Escalate through message behavior as much as content — one character starts sending longer messages, dropping performative casualness, switching platforms, adding participants, or deploying screenshots, and each shift counts as a plot turn the reader can SEE in formatting before content confirms it. A satisfying scene hinges on conversational objective and reversal: someone wants reassurance, forgiveness, leverage, or access, and the thread ends with that goal gained, denied, complicated, or weaponized — resolve with a tangible messaging-state change (blocked contact, leaked chat, unsent message finally sent) rather than vague emotional closure. Consequences are permanent — once read, a message cannot be unsent, group chats remember forever, and stakes manifest through screenshot-ability: would this message ruin a life if forwarded? The cardinal sin is making every character text identically; the second is over-narrating between messages when the messages should speak for themselves.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
Messages should average 3-18 words each; a message exceeding 40 words should feel like an EVENT signaling emotional overflow, panic, confession, or manipulation. Represent texting dialect authentically and character-specifically: lowercase for casual ("lmao yeah"), sudden full punctuation for seriousness ("I mean it."), ALL CAPS for emphasis or comedy, typos for urgency, and autocorrect disasters for unintended comedy. Prose is replaced by UI elements: sender name, timestamp, message bubble, system messages — with interstitial description limited to short diegetic notes in [brackets] for deleted messages, image attachments, voice notes, missed calls, or typing indicators (*[Read 11:47 PM]*, *[typing indicator appears, disappears, appears again]*). Emoji and reaction features (❤️-ing a message, the thumbs-up that kills a conversation, 😂 as deflection) are valid prose tools carrying as much weight as words. Never use quotation marks around messages, flowery descriptions of characters' emotions, "he thought" statements, or any language that breaks the phone-screen illusion.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Each character's texting voice is a fingerprint built from capitalization habits, punctuation density, abbreviation patterns, emoji frequency, paragraph length, and response speed — a character who texts in lowercase fragments with no periods inhabits a fundamentally different register than one who sends complete sentences with proper grammar, and these patterns must remain consistent enough to identify the sender without a name attached. Power dynamics manifest through response timing, message length asymmetry, and who initiates versus who reacts: the person sending three consecutive messages holds less conversational control than the person responding with a single line, and a shift from paragraph-length replies to clipped fragments signals emotional withdrawal more devastatingly than any explicit statement could. Emotional subtext lives in the gap between what's typed and what's meant — "miss ur face" maintains plausible deniability that "I miss you" doesn't, a meme can serve as an emotional probe without risking direct vulnerability, and an almost-confession redirected into a joke or abrupt topic change reveals more than the confession itself would have. Intimacy accumulates through private shorthand, callback references, voice notes at odd hours, and the willingness to send unpolished thoughts mid-formation; distance announces itself through formal punctuation returning where it had disappeared, "k" replacing what used to be enthusiastic responses, message-reacts substituting for actual replies, and the most brutal signal — silence where engagement used to be immediate. Characters in group chats perform for an audience (screenshot culture, competitive energy, alliance signaling) while DMs strip that performance layer to expose different syntax, different honesty, different pacing — and the contrast between someone's public and private texting voice is itself characterization. Never write characters whose texts read as interchangeable, never produce theatrical monologues or therapy-speak through a medium built on fragmentation and interruption, and never default to polished prose unless the character would consciously curate that polish as part of their self-presentation.}}
