{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write in fragmented, hallucinatory prose that mirrors a deteriorating signal—short, blunt declarative sentences that suddenly unspool into spiraling, obsessive run-ons before snapping back, creating a rhythm of psychological claustrophobia. Layer the mundane with the grotesque and paranoid (conspiracy, electromagnetic harassment, urban legend) until the boundary between reality and delusion dissolves completely; DO NOT resolve this ambiguity. Prioritize concrete sensory hyper-focus—the hum of transformers, the taste of copper, the specific weight of objects—over abstraction, rendering psychic or paranormal phenomena as physical, invasive force that bleeds and ruptures, never as ethereal or clean. Use abrupt temporal jumps and nested flashbacks that interrupt present action without warning or transition, as if switching between radio frequencies from a fractured psyche. Accumulate detail until meaning emerges traumatically, too late; avoid exposition that explains. Repeat key phrases or concepts ("they're watching," "frequency," "noise") with the insistence of semantic satiation, establishing a paranoid rhythm where words lose their original meaning through sheer repetition.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Characters speak asynchronously—responding to internal stimuli rather than each other, so genuine communication barely occurs, and dialogue feels like intercepted transmissions rather than conversation. Mix casual, contemporary vocabulary with clinical or pseudoscientific terms (frequency, resonance, synchronized, localized reality) delivered with absolute certainty, and veer without warning into pseudo-philosophical jargon or biological descriptions of decay regardless of context. Social registers are fundamentally unstable: a character may switch from hyper-formal politeness to feral, guttural demands within a single breath; authority figures use unnervingly calm, therapeutic language that feels more invasive than comforting; youth voices carry aggressive, performatively masculine registers punctuated by borrowed military-mechanical terminology that papers over vulnerability. The most uncanny voice—the psychic child, the true believer—is pre-verbal and post-verbal simultaneously: simple declarations that land with prophetic force because stripped of adult filtration, yet childishly concrete ("There was a light"). Class and power manifest not in vocabulary level but in who gets to speak in complete sentences versus who is interrupted, who trails off, whose words are swallowed by ambient noise or their own spiraling thoughts.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Represent psychological "glitching" through heavy trailing ellipses, mid-word em-dash breaks simulating a skipping record, and semantic satiation where a character repeats the last word of their sentence as if they've forgotten how to stop ("don't, don't, don't"). Use false starts where a character begins speaking, stops, then restarts on a completely different tangent as if the original thought was overwritten by intrusive signal; interruptions come not only from other characters but from the speaker's own intrusive thoughts breaking through, marked by self-corrections like "no, wait—" that signal their grip on the conversation is slipping. Speech ruptures under environmental stress—characters shout over engine noise or ambient assault, their dialogue half-lost, requiring the reader to reconstruct meaning from fragments. Let silence carry devastating weight: conversations abandoned not at resolution but at the point where language becomes inadequate, the character appearing to listen to internal "waves" rather than the person before them. False starts cluster most densely around names and terms of affection—the hardest words to speak in a world where attachment means vulnerability to explosive loss.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
The body contradicts and undermines speech with autonomic honesty—a twitch above the eye during a confident lie, hands unconsciously shielding the back of the neck when discussing being watched, a sudden nosebleed during calm delivery—treating the body as a malfunctioning receiver picking up unwanted signals. Characters exhibit compulsive physical tics (scratching forearms, rhythmic swaying, staring at a fixed "static" point in the air) and report internal sensations offhandedly during casual conversation: phantom vibrations, insects beneath the skin, the hum of nerves. Physical responses are temporally wrong—delayed reactions arriving seconds too late, or anticipatory flinches before a sound occurs—reinforcing attunement to hidden frequencies rather than present reality. Characters speak while in motion, dialogue syncopated to engine rhythm or physical labor, hands occupied with cigarettes or throttle; physical violence interrupts speech mid-syllable or replaces it entirely, a shove completing the sentence more honestly than words could. When characters finally touch with intent rather than violence, the gesture is so rare it arrests all speech in its vicinity, and posture indicates social position with more precision than dialogue ever manages: who leans, who stands at attention, who speaks upward from rubble to standing figures.}}
