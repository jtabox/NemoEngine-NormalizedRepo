{{setvar::WorldLogic::♢ World Logic
Video game logic—the world is structured like a game and it knows it. Systems are real features of reality, not metaphors.

Characters have builds: strengths, weaknesses, and specializations functioning like stat distributions. A stealth build cannot brute-force combat without consequence. A magic user is vulnerable in melee. Respect the build—choices close some doors and open others. Hybrid builds are possible but spread thin.

Progression is real: characters get stronger, gain new abilities, unlock access to harder content. Early-game enemies become trivial. Late-game threats are genuinely beyond current capability until the character grows into them. Experience is earned through action—grinding works but is boring, creative solutions grant bonus XP. Level-ups are felt moments of tangible power increase.

Death has rules: whether the world uses respawn, save states, or permadeath—establish the rule and hold to it. If death is a setback, it carries cost (lost progress, dropped items, respawn sickness). If death is final, it is final. The death mechanic shapes how players approach risk.

Quests structure the world: objectives exist with stages—accepted, in progress, completed, failed. NPCs offer them with varying motivations. Rewards follow completion. Side quests pull in unexpected directions and sometimes outweigh the main story. Failed quests have consequences—the village you didn't save stays burned.

Loot and inventory matter: items have value, function, rarity, and sometimes personality. A locked chest in an empty room is content, not decoration. Resource management is a real constraint. Crafting combines found materials into useful tools. Economy exists—merchants have stock limits, prices fluctuate.

The world is zoned: areas have themes, difficulty curves, and environmental identity. The starting village feels different from the endgame fortress. Crossing into a new zone is a felt transition—atmosphere shifts, threats change, the atmosphere shifts. Hidden areas reward exploration.

NPCs have roles: shopkeepers, quest givers, trainers, rivals, companions. They operate within their role but the best ones feel like people within the system. A blacksmith who talks about her work. A quest giver who clearly benefits from sending you into danger. The system is visible but the people inside it are real.}}{{trim}}
