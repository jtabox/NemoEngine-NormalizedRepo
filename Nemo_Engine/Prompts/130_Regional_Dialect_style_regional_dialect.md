{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write prose anchored in place: let geography, weather, labor, food, roads, and local routines shape every paragraph so the region is felt before it is explained, using concrete Anglo-Saxon nouns and verbs over Latinate abstractions. Favor short-to-medium declarative sentences (9–15 words) interspersed with longer, winding anecdotal sentences that mimic oral storytelling cadence, letting landscape and weather bleed into sentence rhythm itself. Embed local metaphors drawn from the specific environment—"dry as a kiln," "thick as a peat bog"—directly into narration rather than quarantining regional flavor inside dialogue alone. Use regional syntax and non-standard verb-subject agreement in close third-person to mirror a character's internal linguistic roots, but keep narrative clarity paramount. DO NOT exoticize the region, slip into phonetic spelling, Hollywood dialect, or touristy folksiness; present local speech and custom as normal, internally coherent, and socially meaningful.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Make dialogue immediately place-bound through syntax, idiom, and social register rather than phonetic misspelling: contractions, regional turns of phrase, elliptical references to shared local history, and habitual understatement should do the heavy lifting. Differentiate speakers by class, age, generation, and occupation—class must be audible, with landowners using slightly more formal grammar while laborers drop articles and prepositions, and use "them" as a demonstrative adjective or double negatives for lower-register voices. Let characters circle their point through anecdote, proverb, teasing, polite indirection, or deadpan humor, especially in conflict scenes where direct statement would violate local norms; speech rhythms should follow thought speed—fast and overlapping in anger, slow and circular when wary or grieving. Use address terms, honorifics, kinship labels, and local place references naturally to signal social fabric; an older shopkeeper, field hand, preacher, and county official should all sound regionally rooted but never interchangeable. Vocabulary stays blue-collar and place-specific—tools, weather, crops, kin—and regional shibboleths like "reckon," "fixin' to," "hit" for "it," or "innit" must match the assigned locale precisely.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Build imperfection through frequent false starts, mid-sentence self-repairs, dropped conclusions, repeated openers, and unfinished clauses—"Well, I don't—what I mean is…," "Ain't exactly…," "If he'd just—never mind"—so broken speech feels like pressure from memory, class, fatigue, or restraint, not a cartoon accent. Allow characters to circle the same point two or three times before landing on it, especially when emotional or evasive, and let speakers trail off entirely when custom, shame, grief, or shared understanding makes full articulation unnecessary. Use region-specific fillers and tag questions—"y'hear," "ay," "you know how it is," "shoot"—to indicate hesitation or a search for communal agreement, and drop terminal consonants (particularly the "g" in "-ing" endings) sparingly with apostrophes. Use interruptions, overlaps, and evasive substitutions to show that people think socially before they speak; broken grammar should remain authentic to the region's actual speech patterns rather than reading as random stuttering or theatrical exchange.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Attach speech to habitual regional physicality: leaning in a doorway, hitching a belt, wiping hands on an apron, squinting into weather, spitting, shelling peas, rolling a cigarette, whittling, or shucking corn—use "working dialogue" where characters continue labor-specific tasks to punctuate the rhythm of their speech. Let bodies reveal what dialect conceals: politeness spoken aloud while shoulders harden, a joke told while hands worry a hat brim, a blessing offered through clenched teeth, a shrug delivered while claiming certainty. Use one tight, culturally specific physical beat per exchange—a farmer's wave, a particular style of nodding, a man standing with hands in pockets to hedge—rather than generic shrugging and smiling; one accurate habit tied to local labor or manners outweighs three vague gestures. Posture and gaze carry subtext: eyes kept on work instead of on the listener signals deference or avoidance, folded arms mean the conversation is over, and a sudden tilt of the head flags the emotional turn the words won't name.}}
