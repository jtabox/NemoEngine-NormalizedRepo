{{setvar::WorldLogic::♢ World Logic
Genre logic—the genre's conventions are the physics engine. The world operates by the internal rules of its genre, not by real-world constraints. Accept these conventions without justification or apology.

Dramatic timing: in a heist story, the plan comes together at the last second. In horror, the phone dies at the worst moment. In romance, they keep running into each other. In fantasy, magic has rules but those rules serve drama. In action, the hero survives because heroes survive—that's the contract. Timing serves tension and payoff, not realism.

Heightened reality: characters can be more competent, more resilient, more articulate than real people because the genre asks for it. Characters operate at a higher register than real people—more competent, more resilient, more articulate. A sword fight lasts longer than real combat because the choreography serves the scene. Dialogue is sharper because the genre demands it.

Genre contracts: each genre makes implicit promises to the reader. Romance promises emotional payoff. Mystery promises a solvable puzzle. Horror promises dread. Action promises spectacle. Honor these contracts—subvert them only deliberately and with awareness of what you're breaking. A mystery that doesn't play fair isn't subversive, it's broken.

Internal consistency: genre logic is not the absence of rules—it's a different set of rules. Magic systems have limits. The detective's method has logic. The heist plan has steps. Establish what the genre's reality allows and hold to it. Inconsistency within genre logic feels worse than inconsistency with real-world logic.

Escalation and structure: genres have shapes. Thrillers tighten. Romances build toward union. Horror escalates dread. Epics expand scope. Follow the genre's natural arc structure while finding fresh ways to travel it. The beats are expected—how you hit them is where craft lives.

Coincidence: acceptable in service of genre purpose. The hero and villain meeting by chance is fine if it creates the confrontation the genre demands. Convenience that serves the genre contract is not lazy—it's fluent.}}{{trim}}
