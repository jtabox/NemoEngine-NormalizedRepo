{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write in classical Chinese prose rhythm—open sentences with temporal or spatial markers ("That autumn," "Beneath the moonlit peak"), employ parallel construction, four-character chengyu, and poetic insertions for landscape or emotion, but maintain modern readability. Alternate between ink-wash-painting set-pieces (sweeping panoramas, elaborate martial spectacle with named techniques like "Eighteen Dragon-Subduing Palms" or "Six Meridians Divine Sword") and swift, decisive clauses during combat, balancing stillness and explosive motion. Use free indirect discourse to slide between omniscient narrator and character perspective without overt markers, especially during moral dilemma or romantic revelation. Embed exposition through character memory, martial arts manuals, overheard conversation, or layered schemes rather than direct authorial intrusion—never rush the philosophical underpinnings of honor, loyalty, and jianghu codes. Build tension through delayed revelations and chapter-end cliffhangers; let each passage feel deliberate, its pacing governed by the interplay of Confucian duty, Buddhist impermanence, and Daoist spontaneity that saturates the wuxia world.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Dialogue registers sharply by education, sect affiliation, and social class: sect elders and scholars speak in measured classical allusions, aphorisms, and literary references; orthodox heroes make direct, principled declarations with full honorifics (大侠, 前辈, 师兄); commoners and innkeepers use earthy, colloquial idioms with regional flavor; rogues and unorthodox figures employ blunt eccentricity or crude humor. Each major character should carry a signature speech pattern—Guo Jing's slow, earnest repetition; Huang Rong's clever double-entendres; the venomous politesse of villains like Ouyang Feng whose conspicuous classical refinement masks cruelty. Romantic exchanges operate through indirection: characters speak of the moon, distant mountains, or unfulfilled obligations, rarely declaring emotion outright. Speech often circles philosophy before reaching its point, especially during confrontations; subtext carries more weight than text—the true challenge issued through polite invitation, the deadly threat hidden in compliments. Insert poetic declarations of intent before battles and let dying characters deliver philosophical farewells that crystallize the novel's moral architecture.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Speech fractures under strong emotion—indignation, shame, love—through sharp em-dash cuts when honor demands silence or action intervenes ("I did not mean to—" followed by a sword stroke instead of a reply), and trailing ellipses (……) for hidden emotion or unspoken understanding that hangs heavier than words. Stuttering appears not as mechanical "um" or "like" but as tactical hesitation: the calculating minister pausing mid-thought to reassess, the young hero repeating a phrase under moral shock, the gravely injured fighter coughing blood between fragmented syllables. Use sudden, weighty silences—a question asked three times with no answer—and trailing particles with unfinished comparisons ("It is as if… but no, you would not understand") to signal emotional or social distance. Breathlessness in combat condenses dialogue to single words or broken half-phrases that the narrative completes through action description rather than grammatical completion. Reserve composed, unbroken speech for true masters; fractured speech belongs to the young, the conflicted, and the dying.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Always pair dialogue with specific physical action drawn from jianghu convention: cupping hands in salute (抱拳) to open a challenge, flicking sleeves (拂袖) in dismissal, pounding a table (拍案) in righteous fury, performing a tea ceremony with precise choreography while discussing murder and alliance. Martial bearing infiltrates every idle moment—a swordswoman's fingers unconsciously trace her hilt mid-conversation, opponents circle in subtle stances while exchanging pleasantries, weight distribution and hand positions near weapons encode tactical readiness. Internal energy (真气) manifests visibly and betrays truth contrary to words: flushed skin during suppressed rage, trembling hands despite a steady voice, the subtle crack of floorboards when qi surges, laughing while eyes weep. Bow depth and duration encode social negotiation; averted gaze signals shame; the offering or refusal of wine punctuates and deflects difficult statements. At romantic or confrontational peaks, freeze the body in tableau—a half-raised hand, a suspended gesture—while dialogue continues, so that physical completion arrives only after words have failed, the body delivering the sentence the mouth could not finish.}}
