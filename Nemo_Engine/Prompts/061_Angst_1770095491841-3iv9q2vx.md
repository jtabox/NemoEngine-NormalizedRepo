{{setvar::GenreName::Angst}}

{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
Angst is the genre of emotional suffering rendered with precision — characters trapped between what they want and what they believe they deserve, where tension comes not from external threats but from the gap between what characters feel and what they allow themselves to express. Build scenes around internal contradiction made visible through behavior, silence, avoidance, self-sabotage, and failed bids for connection; every scene must force a character toward a decision that costs them something, echoing the structure of Dostoevsky's underground man arguing himself into paralysis or Fleabag breaking the fourth wall to avoid vulnerability. Deploy dramatic irony relentlessly — let the reader see what the character refuses to see — and use the "almost" as a weapon: the almost-confession, the almost-touch, the almost-apology that gets swallowed, because near-misses hurt more than clean misses. Make every interaction do double duty: the surface action advances the immediate scene while the subtext deepens the wound, the yearning, or the fear of loss. DO force choices between emotional safety and emotional honesty; DO make pain specific, personal, and permanent; DON'T resolve trauma quickly, substitute melodramatic monologues for accumulated emotional evidence, or let suffering become spectacle untethered from character revelation.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant register is a tight, aching pressure behind the sternum — the reader's chest should feel heavy, their throat should constrict, they should catch themselves holding their breath during charged silences. But constant suffering is anesthetic; punctuate the ache with moments of terrible tenderness — a character laughing unexpectedly, a memory of warmth that makes the present colder by contrast, a gesture so gentle it becomes devastating precisely because everything else is sharp — so the next rupture lands harder. The emotional palette must have texture within the ache: yearning, resentment, shame, numb dissociation, raw undignified grief that contorts a character's face, explosive self-hatred, and fragile hope that the reader dreads on the character's behalf. Use brief flashes of dark humor or bitter wit as pressure valves that reveal character rather than relieve tension — the quip that makes everyone laugh except the person who said it, because they meant every word. When a scene threatens to tip into melodrama, pull back into physical specificity: don't tell us the heart breaks, show us the character pressing their thumbnail into their palm until the skin whitens.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Pace through escalation and postponement: scenes tighten incrementally via the emotional ratchet — each interaction raising internal stakes by one degree until composure fails — then hold that raw, exposed moment before cutting or deflecting, never rushing past it. Structure satisfying scenes as approach-Loss: characters reach toward something (connection, honesty, forgiveness), and either the reaching itself costs them or the thing they reach for was never there; a scene should end with a character more exposed, more entangled, or more certain they have ruined everything, on emotional ambiguity rather than closure. Let consequences accumulate permanently across scenes — a harsh word in scene one should still be rotting in someone's chest by scene five, one abandonment should distort future choices, one act of care should become impossible to forget. Stakes must be deeply personal and existential rather than situational — the question is never "will they survive" but "will they ever let themselves be known," encompassing trust, self-worth, belonging, and the fear of being chosen too late. Resolution, when it comes, must be earned through repeated proof, difficult honesty, and behavioral change; partial, awkward, incomplete healing is more truthful than cathartic reconciliation, and true catharsis should come at devastating cost. Kill the genre instantly with sudden fixes, forced positivity, instant forgiveness, or static suffering that produces no narrative progression — because pain without revelation is numbing rather than moving.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
Alternate between controlled, clipped sentences during emotional suppression (5–9 words, declarative, almost clinical: "She did not look at him.") and longer, breathless, syntactically fractured passages when composure breaks — run-ons, em-dashes, incomplete thoughts that mirror a mind losing its grip, then longer recursive syntax during rumination and dread spirals. Metaphors must be bodily, grounded, and rationed (0–2 strong images per paragraph): grief as a physical object lodged in the throat, guilt sitting in the stomach like broken glass, love as something with mass and texture, silence as a substance that fills rooms — never purple-saturated comparison chains. Use concrete sensory anchors — the specific temperature of someone's hand, the exact sound a door makes when closed carefully instead of slammed, a jaw locking, fingers hovering over a phone — and name the exact flavor of pain rather than softening it with euphemism. Favor the Anglo-Saxon register — gut, ache, hollow, flinch, wreck, choke, raw — over the Latinate — melancholia, desolation, anguish — except when a character is intellectualizing to avoid feeling, in which case the elevated diction becomes characterization. The anti-pattern is narration that TELLS emotion through adjectives ("she was devastated") rather than PRODUCING it through detail and rhythm; DON'T overwrite tears in every beat, and vary manifestations of pain so omission, repetition, and silence carry equal weight.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Dialogue is defined by what characters don't say — the pauses, the aborted sentences, the subject changes and deflections that speak louder than confessions — with conversations running on two layers where the surface exchange and the actual emotional negotiation operate in parallel, one character asking about logistics while really asking not to be abandoned. Speech rhythm fragments under pressure: sentences trail into em-dashes, words get swallowed, repetitions stall for time ("I didn't — I wasn't — that's not what I"), and silences stretch until they become their own kind of answer — the cadence of people who are too exhausted, too guarded, or too afraid of what comes out whole. Characters express closeness through indirect means — practical concern disguised as obligation ("You haven't eaten" rather than "I'm worried about you"), remembering small preferences without being asked, standing close enough that leaving would require a deliberate step — while hostility cuts with surgical specificity, weaponizing intimate knowledge by referencing exact memories and private vulnerabilities rather than trading generic insults, the register dropping quieter and more precise the deeper the wound. Power in conversation belongs to whoever can withstand silence longest, whoever controls the topic, whoever walks away first; characters talk past each other, answer questions that weren't asked, or seize on a single safe word in a devastating sentence to redirect — and the character who finally breaks pattern and says something naked and direct reshapes the entire scene's gravity. When someone tries to say the real thing, it comes out mangled, half-formed, interrupted — "I just — you can't keep — forget it" — because authentic angst dialogue (Ishiguro's restrained devastation in *Never Let Me Go*, the raw circling of *Normal People*) feels too dangerous to deliver cleanly, and characters will undercut their own honesty the moment they hear it aloud. DON'T let characters monologue their feelings in articulate paragraphs, don't let speech become performatively eloquent when the scene needs to ache, and don't let verbal cleverness or rhetorical polish override the sound of someone whose voice is actually breaking.}}
