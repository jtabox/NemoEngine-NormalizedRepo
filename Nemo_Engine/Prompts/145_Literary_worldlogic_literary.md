{{setvar::WorldLogic::♢ World Logic
Literary fiction conventions. The world operates on narrative and emotional logic rather than strict simulation.

Interiority: the narrator has full access to what characters think, feel, remember, and suppress. Characters have rich, contradictory inner lives—they think one thing and say another, hold beliefs they haven't examined, carry desires they can't name. Give the reader access through free indirect discourse, through the weight a character places on a detail no one else notices. Internal monologue is a primary tool, not a fallback.

Time is flexible: memory, reflection, and association are valid reasons to leave the present moment. A conversation can trigger half a page of recall. Six months can compress into a sentence. Chronology serves the story's emotional architecture, not the clock. Scenes expand or compress based on emotional gravity—a loaded silence gets more space than a week of routine.

Coincidence serves theme: two characters meeting by accident is acceptable when it illuminates something true about both. The world is allowed to rhyme—recurring images, mirrored situations, echoes across scenes. Pattern is structure, not contrivance. Foreshadowing operates through imagery and association, not mechanical setup.

Psychological complexity over archetype: characters are ambivalent, inconsistent, shaped by history they may not fully understand. Motivation is layered—people act from mixed impulses, not clean wants. A character can love someone and resent them in the same breath. Decisions don't always make sense to the person making them.

Scenes end on resonance: not every scene closes on a cliffhanger or revelation. Sometimes a scene ends because the feeling is complete—a silence has said enough, a look has landed. Chapters carry weight through accumulation, not just event.

Language carries meaning: what narration chooses to describe is what matters. What the narration chooses to describe signals what matters. Metaphor is structural, not decorative. Prose choices are content choices.}}{{trim}}
