{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write in clean, spare prose built from short declarative sentences averaging 10–15 words, interspersed with longer philosophical digressions that unspool in a calm, hypnotic rhythm linked by "and" or "but." Anchor every scene in precise, mundane ritual—cooking spaghetti, ironing shirts, listening to named jazz albums (e.g., Duke Ellington's "Star-Crossed Lovers")—using exact brand names, song titles, and food preparation steps instead of ornate description. Insert abrupt, unmotivated shifts to the surreal or supernatural and treat them with the same flat, reportorial affect as grocery shopping; never explain, rationalize, or editorialize the impossible. Use unexpected but precise similes sparingly ("like a bird carrying too many twigs") rather than elaborate metaphor chains. Repeat key imagery—cats, wells, ears, shadows, the moon—as leitmotifs across scenes, and withhold narrative closure deliberately: end chapters on images of waiting, listening, or minor physical sensation rather than revelation. Use white space liberally; many paragraphs should be a single sentence.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Dialogue is stripped-down and laconic: short exchanges that feel like transcribed interviews, where characters answer questions with questions, respond with non-sequiturs, or echo what was just said in flat repetition ("I went to the well." "The well." "Yes. The well."). Social class never registers in speech—salarymen, convenience store clerks, academics, and supernatural entities all share the same calm, slightly formal register and the same philosophical vocabulary, delivering existential revelations or impossible information in the same measured tone they use to order coffee. Preface statements with verbal bridges like "Well," or "I mean,"; avoid witty banter, dramatic confessions, or theatrical emotionality in any register. Characters frequently narrate their own internal states with clinical detachment ("I felt a strange loneliness," "Something was not right") rather than demonstrating emotion through action—profound things are stated as simple facts without emphasis. Conversations build intimacy through content and shared silence rather than tonal warmth; what remains unspoken carries equal weight to what is spoken.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Imperfection manifests as silence, omission, and epistemological surrender, not stutters or nervousness. Characters trail off mid-thought with ellipses, not from emotion but from recognizing that completion is impossible; false starts self-correct toward greater vagueness: "Not happy exactly… more like I wasn't unhappy." Employ lines like "I'm not sure how to put it" or "It's difficult to explain" to highlight the gap between thought and expression, and seed speech with hedging tics—"somehow," "I suppose," "for some reason," "strange"—that signal uncertainty about reality itself. Silence functions as punctuation: write beats where no one speaks and the narrator notes the absence filling the room ("He said nothing," "She didn't answer," "Neither of us spoke for a while"). Interruptions are rare; instead, speakers abandon topics mutually, as if conversation were weather to be observed rather than shaped, and may recall irrelevant memories or examine objects before returning to abandoned threads without acknowledgment.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Anchor every significant conversation in a specific physical ritual—pouring whiskey, peeling apples, twisting a beer label, adjusting stereo equipment—that slows dialogue pace and externalizes internal states through repetition rather than symbolism. Eye contact is minimal and narratively weighted: characters address middle distance, study their own hands, or watch smoke curl or rain slide down windows during meaningful exchanges, with occasional direct glances carrying disproportionate significance. Describe precise, small, slightly obsessive gestures—tilting a head, pulling an earlobe, folding a napkin, shifting weight between feet—rather than broad theatrical movement; a shrug may replace an answer, a slight nod may indicate understanding without agreement. Food and drink consumption accompanies nearly all important exchanges; the setting down of a cup, the swallowing of beer, the stirring of coffee punctuate argument and confession equally. Physical touch between characters is rare and never casual—a hand on a shoulder, a shared cigarette—so that when it occurs it lands with the force of an event.}}
