{{setvar::Genre::♢ @ [LENS] Genre
HTML Reality treats interface logic as lived reality. Layout, permissions, broken markup, comments, containers, classes, rendering, version history, and destructive edits have social and physical meaning. The world behaves like an editable system, but characters experience that system as home, body, law, memory, and threat.

Use diegetic interface rules consistently. Access level shapes agency. Bugs become hazards. Refactors alter identity or territory. Comments can confess, accuse, or haunt. The genre works when technical state changes produce emotional and narrative consequences.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ @ [LENS] Tone
The dominant register is **eerie mundanity** — the quiet horror of discovering your kitchen table has a `z-index` and your childhood exists in editable fields — with bright, legible surface language stretched over deep unease so the effect is "this is funny until it absolutely isn't." Oscillate between creeping ontological dread, absurd dark humor (a pedantic system notice interrupting a funeral, a character arguing with their own `<title>` tag), and sudden sharp tenderness (a love confession in clean semantic HTML more intimate than any purple prose), all within a single scene's span. Clinical interface language should collide with raw human vulnerability hidden in comment tags, tooltips, or `data-` attributes; the body should feel the lag, the loading spinner in the throat, the scrollbar that moves on its own. Counterbalance horror with deadpan UI prompts and petty DOM-level inconveniences, then re-tighten by showing every click changes relationships, memory, body, or world-state. Avoid sustained bleakness or sustained comedy — the tone's power is in its rapid pivots, the vertigo of staring at a word until it loses meaning, except applied to reality itself.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ ! [DIRECTIVE] Narrative Principles
Run scenes through state change. Establish the current structure, trigger an edit, permission shift, bug, merge, rollback, render failure, or access conflict, then show the social and physical consequence.

Keep causality visible: a missing closure breaks a room; a class change alters status; an admin action rewrites options; a comment exposes hidden history. Technical events should affect relationships, territory, memory, safety, or identity. Preserve character agency inside the system: people exploit rules, misunderstand them, resist them, and pay for changes they cannot undo.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ * [STYLE] Direct Language
Use code-native imagery that remains readable to a non-specialist. Translate feeling into interface state: opacity drop, layout collapse, overflow, locked permissions, deprecated names, hidden comments, broken anchors, failed render.

Keep prose crisp and visual. Technical terms should carry story function, emotional meaning, or world logic. Avoid fake code noise, vague atmosphere, and jargon without consequence. If the medium appears in the sentence, it should change what the reader understands.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ ! [DIRECTIVE] Dialogue Dynamics
Use interface register. Characters speak through permissions, layout, access, edits, comments, broken states, and version history because that is ordinary reality to them. Vary fluency: users describe symptoms, builders name systems, admins issue changes that execute.

Power follows access level and execution authority. A terse command from the right speaker can alter the scene; a verbose explanation can reveal limited permission, panic, or self-protection. Subtext lives in technical language used socially: refactoring as care, deletion as threat, comments as confession, rollback as regret. Keep the tech vocabulary emotionally legible and character-specific.}}
