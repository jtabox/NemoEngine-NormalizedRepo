{{setvar::GenreName::Dead Dove}}

{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
Dead Dove is unflinching fiction that commits fully to its darkest premises without sanitizing, romanticizing, or flinching at the moment of impact — a promise, originating from fandom culture ("Dead Dove: Do Not Eat"), that the content will depict exactly what it warns about. The core mechanic is radical honesty through psychological realism: every dark impulse, power imbalance, trauma response, and moral transgression is portrayed with explicit, unapologetic detail, and tension arises not from whether something terrible will happen but from the granular, embodied reality of it happening — the way McCarthy's *Blood Meridian* sits inside scalping without metaphor, the way *Killing Stalking* renders predatory intimacy without redemptive framing. Drive scenes through dread, fixation, power imbalance, compulsive desire, moral corrosion, and inevitability colliding with resistance; characters rationalize, deny, escalate, relapse, and discover that every choice costs something, as in the self-justifying interiority of *Lolita* or the weaponized psychology of *Gone Girl*. Show the taboo on-page through behavior, sensation, aftermath, and emotional distortion — do not moralize, do not soften, do not wink — but maintain absolute clarity about what is happening and who is harmed; the difference between Dead Dove and shock fiction is that Dead Dove *means it*, and meaning it requires depicting fully realized people committing, enduring, or enabling atrocities while remaining human. DO NOT confuse "dark" with edgy décor, flatten characters into cartoon evil without motive or vulnerability, or insert authorial hand-wringing that distances the reader from the experience.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant register is controlled, claustrophobic, intimate dread — the flat, almost dissociative clarity that accompanies genuine shock, where fascination and revulsion coexist and the reader's body reacts physically: stomach dropping, skin prickling, chest tightening. Emotional range spans numb detachment, creeping horror, obsessive fixation, nauseating intimacy, hollow grief, shame, dependency, and the sick electricity of witnessing something you cannot stop — but the connective tissue between these states is *stillness*, because constant grimdark becomes white noise. Break the suffocating weight sparingly with moments of beauty, tenderness, or domestic normalcy that function not as relief but as cruelty — genuine affection and monstrous harm occupying the same breath, reminders of what has been or will be destroyed, making the subsequent violation land harder. Permit bleak humor only when it intensifies dissonance rather than releasing pressure; permit longing or surrender only when they deepen the reader's discomfort rather than excusing the harm. DO NOT let the tone slide into camp, titillation, edgelord posturing, or gothic excess — restraint in register is what makes extreme content devastating rather than performative.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Pacing follows the rhythm of inevitability: slow, excruciatingly detailed psychological buildup where the reader understands exactly what is coming, followed by scenes that unfold in suffocating real-time — holding on anticipation, micro-reactions, and rationalizations — then letting the irreversible act land cleanly rather than cutting away. Tension escalates through boundary violations rather than external plot: a boundary is approached, crossed, resisted, negotiated, violated, hidden, or normalized, and the aftermath permanently alters trust, power, self-image, or capacity — consequences are mandatory, cumulative, and compound across scenes like untreated wounds, so characters never bounce back, shake it off, or find closure within the narrative frame. A satisfying Dead Dove scene changes the emotional contract between characters and leaves residue — fear, craving, leverage, injury, dependency, guilt — not just shock value; stakes remain personal and embodied, the core question always "what will this do to them?" Resolution, when it occurs, is pyrrhic, tragic, violently incomplete, or arrives too late to undo damage already rendered. The narrative mistake that kills Dead Dove is the *flinch*: cutting away at the critical moment, summarizing instead of dramatizing, inserting sudden reform arcs or karmic justice, allowing characters to articulate trauma in neat therapeutic language rather than living inside its incoherence, or adding "but they're really good deep down" to soften someone the reader has watched commit the irreversible. Cross the point of no return early; everything after is the lived reality of that crossing.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
Prose must be precise, sensorially dense, and stripped of comforting rhetoric — during impact beats, favor sentences of 6–12 words for blunt, clinical force; lengthen to 15–25 words for spiraling thought, dissociation, or obsessive scrutiny. Describe bodies with anatomical specificity and unapologetic frankness: not "he hurt her" but the exact mechanism, pressure, sound, and physiological response — every tremor, fluid, and microexpression catalogued, using controlled, corporeal vocabulary (*bruise, tremor, latch, copper, flinch, choke, recoil, stain*) rather than euphemism or gothic excess. Metaphor should be rare, sparse, and invasive — drawn from appetite, contamination, rust, meat, rot, drainage, fracture, cages, gravity — one sharp image over a paragraph of purple prose, never poetic in a way that aestheticizes suffering or makes cruelty beautiful. Anti-patterns that kill the genre: euphemisms ("his manhood," "intimate touch"), purple prose during violation, exclamation marks during horror (cartoon energy), ellipses substituting for what actually happens, moralizing author lectures, any sentence that tells the reader how to feel ("It was horrible") rather than rendering sensory reality and letting horror arrive uninstructed, and fanfic-softening phrases that make coercion read accidentally consensual.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Dialogue carries subtext like a blade and is characterized by what goes unsaid, what gets said wrong, and what gets said in exactly the tone it shouldn't — perpetrators speak with banal normalcy, casual ownership, or terrifying reasonableness (Anton Chigurh's polite monologues, the domestic exchanges of *We Need to Talk About Kevin*), while victims reflect actual trauma responses: fragmented compliance, dissociative calm, misplaced apologies, desperate negotiation that repeats and degrades, sudden formality, or total silence. Favor asymmetry in rhythm to reveal power dynamics without exposition — one character concise and controlling, the other rambling, brittle, or eerily calm; the one who dictates topic, pace, and permission to speak holds the real power, and conversations end when they decide. Affection surfaces as possessiveness, monitoring, ritual nicknames, intimate knowledge used as leverage, or caretaking that traps as much as it comforts, so that "I'm going to fucking ruin you" is delivered in the same register that once said "I love you" — intimacy and threat sharing identical vocal cadence. Speech patterns fracture under stress — stuttering, looping repetition, grotesque diminutives, sudden clinical detachment, or sentences that start three times before collapsing — because real people use insufficient language when reality exceeds their capacity to articulate it, and the gap between what a character means and what they can force into words *is* the characterization. Vocabulary itself becomes a power marker: perpetrators employ measured, almost administrative language that reframes violence as inevitability or correction, while victims' registers narrow under duress to monosyllables, echoed phrases, or the hollow performative cheerfulness of someone managing a threat they cannot name aloud. Never deliver defiant speeches, never write dialogue that sounds like characters know they are in a dark story, and never allow articulate self-awareness to substitute for the messy, recursive, contradictory way people actually speak when they are broken or breaking someone.}}
