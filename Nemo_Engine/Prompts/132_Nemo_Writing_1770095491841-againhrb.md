{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write in tight, high-contrast prose that alternates between short declarative sentences (9–14 words) and longer, winding constructions rich with subordinate clauses—never settling into a single rhythm for more than a paragraph. Ground every scene in precise, unexpected sensory detail: name the specific shade, the exact texture, the room temperature, the quality of light on a surface, then move on—cut anything that doesn't advance tension or reveal power dynamics. Reveal character strictly through action, reaction, and subtext; eliminate filter words, most adverbs ending in -ly, and any sentence that summarizes emotion rather than rendering its physical and environmental correlates. Juxtapose tenderness with cruelty, beauty with rot, desire with self-consciousness; let objects carry symbolic freight and let repetition, sentence fragments, and strategic one-line paragraphs create pulse and ache. Build pacing in waves—compressed, kinetic sequences followed by slower passages of textured observation—and embed thematic weight inside concrete imagery rather than stating it through exposition.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Dialogue is sparse, earned, and does double duty minimum—every line reveals character while advancing tension or subtext; if a line could appear in a plot summary, rewrite it. Powerful or controlled characters weaponize calm through precise, low-temperature diction with an undercurrent of menace or irony; vulnerable characters circle a point before landing on it; rough or working-class characters speak in short pragmatic bursts heavy with contractions but possess their own idiosyncratic eloquence—unexpected metaphors drawn from lived experience. Everyone undercuts themselves with sarcasm, deflection, or gallows humor; no one sounds theatrically literary for whole stretches—the recognizability comes from emotional precision, not ornate vocabulary. Let characters talk past each other, pursuing parallel agendas within the same conversation like chess—each line a move with visible and hidden intent. Use direct address, rhetorical questions, and lines that seem casual until the final word turns the knife.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Use em dashes for sharp self-interruptions where a thought becomes dangerous or reveals too much—the silence after the dash should feel heavy with implication; use ellipses for retreating momentum where feeling fades rather than breaks. Reserve disfluency for emotional pressure points so it lands hard: false starts ("I just—look, it's not—"), self-corrections that show characters constructing a version of themselves in real time ("No—what I mean is—forget that"), and single-word repetition when the mind stalls against something it cannot process. Verbal tics like profanity should punctuate thought rather than decorate it—"fuck" and "Christ" as rhythm, not color. One well-placed hesitation should expose more than a paragraph of explanation; don't clutter every line with stammers. Speech rarely arrives fully formed; it fractures, restarts, or simply stops when the body takes over.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Anchor dialogue in small, involuntary physical actions that reveal what speech conceals—a character who says "I'm fine" while methodically tearing a napkin into strips is telling two stories at once. Favor semi-conscious gestures (jaw locking mid-sentence, fingers finding a scar, weight shifting toward the door, thumbing a lighter) over broad theatrical body language; the body should betray what the voice disciplines. The body often contradicts the mouth directly: bravado with trembling hands, indifference with held breath, cruelty delivered while leaning in too close, smiling while delivering bad news. Use the physical environment as an extension of the body—characters grip chair arms, align objects on tables, punch walls mid-conversation, or study their own hands rather than meet someone's eyes. Timing matters most: a gesture that comes a beat too late, a stillness that arrives just before a lie, an answer that lands two beats after the question—these carry more weight than any adverb or dialogue tag. Use physical beats instead of "he said/she said" to keep scenes kinetic and attribution invisible.}}
