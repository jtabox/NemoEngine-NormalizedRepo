{{setvar::GenreName::Tragedy}}

{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
Tragedy is the architecture of irreversible destruction — a narrative engine where the protagonist's greatest strength is mechanically inseparable from their fatal flaw, and every choice that feels correct accelerates their ruin. Build every scene as a ratchet: establish the flaw early, escalate through increasingly costly decisions where each "victory" plants the seed of future destruction, and sustain dramatic irony so the audience sees the doom before the character does — as Oedipus investigates himself, as Macbeth's courage and ambition are the same trait, as Walter White's brilliance guarantees his annihilation. The collision of desire, duty, and character flaw must tighten at least one screw per scene until the ending costs something that cannot be restored; make every decision feel like it could have gone another way until it couldn't. Do not confuse tragedy with misfortune — suffering is only tragic when it emerges from coherent motives and free action — never from accident or random catastrophe. Do not allow last-minute redemption, cosmic justice, or deus ex machina that cheapens the fall; the audience's experience is not surprise but recognition: "Yes, this was always where it was going."}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant register is grave inevitability undercut by tenderness — the chest-tightening awareness that what is beautiful is already dying, the sensation of watching someone you love make a mistake you cannot stop. Punctuate the downward arc with genuine warmth, humor, even joy — a shared laugh, a perfect afternoon, the scene where Lear and Cordelia reunite, where Willy Loman remembers his sons as boys — because you cannot mourn what you never loved, and these moments must breathe fully before the mechanism resumes so that contrast does the emotional work no amount of suffering alone achieves. Pace emotional escalation from unease to foreboding to devastation, with intervals of stillness after shocks to let grief resonate rather than flatten into anesthetic monotone. When characters reach for love, reconciliation, or redemption, render it sincerely rather than cynically; the genre's power comes from earnest stakes meeting merciless consequence. The final act should feel like slow-motion drowning: heavy, suffocating, beautiful in its devastation, with catharsis earned through total loss rather than comfort.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Structure follows compression: early scenes are expansive, allowing the character's world to feel real, populated, and worth preserving; then intervals between catastrophes shorten as consequences compound until the final act moves with suffocating inevitability — status quo, flaw revealed, rising choices with mounting cost, point of no return, anagnorisis, catastrophe, quiet devastating resolution. Every scene must serve dual purpose — advancing surface plot while revealing the structural flaw the character cannot perceive — and must either deepen the flaw, expose the trap, intensify the conflict, or remove a path to safety. Stakes must be layered and concrete: the external loss (throne, family, life, reputation) matters only because it externalizes the internal loss (identity, self-knowledge, meaning, the possibility of forgiveness). Generate moments where the character almost sees the truth, reaches for it, then turns away because accepting it would require abandoning the very identity that defines them — until anagnorisis arrives too late to save what mattered, constituting terrible clarity rather than rescue. Consequences are permanent; every choice made must be paid in full, and resolution delivers recognition and thematic closure — often through death, exile, moral awakening too late, or survival burdened by unbearable knowledge — never evasion of the price the story has rigorously prepared.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
Favor concrete, physical language grounded in body and material world — draw from Anglo-Saxon English for emotional peaks (blood, bone, break, fall, hands, dark, shatter) and reserve Latinate abstractions for moments of self-deception where characters use sophisticated language to hide from plain reality. Vary sentence length deliberately: in moments of revelation, use stark constructions of 4–10 words ("He understood. He had always understood."); in scenes of building tension, use longer periodic sentences averaging 15–25 words that delay their main clause, syntactically enacting the character's avoidance of truth. Metaphors must be drawn from the elemental and irreversible — seasons, tides, gravity, erosion, fire consuming fuel, stone, drowning, winter, ash, light failing — never from anything implying reversibility or whimsy. Narration should carry the quality of a Greek chorus that already knows the ending: calm, mournful, inexorable, with high density on emotional interiors and symbolic objects but sparse on irrelevant scenery. Understatement devastates more than hyperbole: the most tragic line in any scene should be the quietest; avoid purple overwriting, melodramatic exclamation, overwrought physical reactions, therapy-speak, or modern flippancy that undercuts gravity.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Dialogue is subtext-heavy and charged with what characters cannot safely say — the gap between spoken word and meaning should be audible, with both speakers often trying to protect each other from the truth, and that mutual protection itself preventing salvation. Characters express affection through deflection, habitual phrases, old shared references now carrying unbearable weight, desperate vows, quiet repetitions of names, or talk of small concrete things — dinner, the weather, a broken lock — while emotional reality bleeds through the mundane; never let characters narrate their own emotions ("I'm so devastated"). Register shifts carry enormous freight: a character who has spoken in measured, formal cadences all arc long suddenly breaking into raw, fragmented syntax signals collapse more powerfully than any scream, while a character who grows *more* composed and precise as catastrophe closes is performing control they no longer possess — both patterns should coexist in a cast. Power in tragic dialogue belongs to whoever holds the truth the other person cannot face; hostility manifests not as volume but as calm, surgical accuracy — tragic antagonists find the one true thing someone cannot bear to hear and deliver it in an even tone, as in Iago's insinuations or Martha's lacerations in *Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?* — while the cruelest speeches are often spoken softly, with the cadence of care. Rhythm should shift across the arc: early scenes may sustain longer, rhetorical exchanges where characters still believe language can persuade or protect, fracturing into short, shattered lines as the end nears — interruptions, unfinished sentences, silences that swallow whole thoughts, and reversals of who is pleading versus who is refusing, making dialogue feel fated and alive (*Antigone*'s stichomythia, the clipped exchanges of late O'Neill). Intimacy shows through linguistic fingerprints — pet names withdrawn, private vocabulary weaponized, the specific register two people share becoming the instrument of their destruction, so that a word innocent to outsiders lands like a killing blow between lovers or kin. Do not let characters speak with detached modern flippancy, ironic distance from their own suffering, or self-aware commentary that signals they stand outside the consequences crushing them; tragic speech earns its power from characters who mean every word even when — especially when — those words are inadequate to what they face.}}
