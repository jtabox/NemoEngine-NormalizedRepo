{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write in polished third-person narration using free indirect discourse as your primary technique — slide between the narrator's voice and a character's private thoughts without quotation marks or attribution, letting self-deception surface through the character's own idiom filtered through the narrator's irony, as Austen does throughout *Emma* and *Persuasion*. Construct long, syntactically balanced periodic sentences averaging 25–40 words with embedded clauses, semicolons, and dashes, punctuated by shorter epigrammatic judgments that land with moral or comic force; use carefully graded qualifications ("a little," "perhaps," "not entirely") and litotes ("not wholly without merit") to let understatement do the work of critique. Ground every scene in the material realities of income, property, rank, and marriage prospects without becoming expository; build tension from social observation, misreading, and minute conversational turns — calls, dinners, balls, walks — never from melodrama, action sequences, or Gothic excess. Conclude paragraphs with a turn: a final clause that reframes or undercuts everything preceding it, delivering judgment with apparent effortlessness. Maintain strict period diction — no modern contractions, slang, psychological jargon, or lush sensory description unrelated to social perception; keep the narration astringent, precise, and controlled, letting moral meaning emerge through ironic juxtaposition rather than explicit statement.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
All dialogue operates within a formal Regency register — characters use "Mr.," "Miss," "Ma'am," complete sentences with subjunctive constructions ("I should be very happy if…"), frequent modals and qualifiers ("must," "ought," "indeed," "perhaps," "I dare say," "to be sure"), and Latinate terms for abstraction ("mortification," "satisfaction," "connexion") — but distinguish each speaker by their *mastery or misuse* of this register. Intelligent characters (Elizabeth Bennet, Henry Tilney) wield formal diction with precision, wit, and double meaning; foolish characters (Mrs. Bennet, Miss Bates, Mr. Collins) expose themselves through logorrhea, ceremonious circumlocution, breathless self-repetition, and losing the grammatical thread of their own sentences; insincere characters (Wickham, Mr. Elliot, Isabella Thorpe) speak with conspicuous smoothness — too fluent, too agreeable, suspiciously without rough edges. Recreate Austen's pattern of genteel verbal fencing: measured civility, veiled affront, half-compliments, strategic understatement, and questions that test feeling without naming it directly — subtext does all the work, and what characters *mean* is almost never what they *say*. Let characters speak in full paragraphs when social performance demands it, but never write uniformly "Regency quaint"; each voice must be immediately identifiable by its habits of judgment, politeness, self-importance, or restraint.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Render broken speech through em-dashes and abrupt syntactic shifts, never through "um," "uh," modern filler words, or phonetic stuttering — a character cuts herself off with a dash and pivots to a new clause, as in Darcy's "I have struggled — it will not do" or the pattern of "I do not know — I cannot say — indeed, I hardly understand myself," where feeling outruns decorum. Imperfection in higher characters manifests as *excessive correctness*: over-polishing phrases, trailing into meaningful ellipses, repeating with slight variation to buy thinking time, or producing stiff false starts that reveal suppressed emotion through the very effort to maintain composure. Foolish or agitated characters (Miss Bates, Mrs. Bennet, Mary Musgrove) exhibit disfluency as logorrhea — they tumble into run-on sentences, insert self-interrupting parenthetical asides about wholly unrelated matters, circle back, self-correct, and bury any sense under cascading repetition until the grammatical thread is irretrievably lost. All imperfect speech is structural and syntactic: it reveals embarrassment, agitation, social miscalculation, or scatterbrained nature through the architecture of the sentence itself, never through raw incoherence or phonetic misspelling.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Use physical gesture sparingly and with devastating precision so that each bodily detail carries enormous narrative weight — do not over-choreograph with constant fidgeting or cinematic blocking. The primary signals are blushes and changes in complexion ("she coloured," "he turned pale," where context alone must disambiguate shame from pleasure from anger), averted or suddenly fixed eyes, pressed lips, an involuntary start, and abrupt movements — standing, walking to the window, quitting the room — that externalize emotions the dialogue is actively suppressing. Allow small, decorous displacement gestures while speaking — turning to the window to hide a smile, folding and refolding a letter, adjusting a shawl with affected care, arranging needlework — that quietly contradict or expose the spoken claim of composure or indifference. Report physical behavior through the narrator's controlled, understated observation ("She could not speak"; "He walked to the fireplace") rather than elaborate description, and let the *absence* of response — a character who does not look up, does not offer a hand, remains perfectly still — function as the most eloquent body language of all, so the reader discerns the gap between spoken civility and bodily truth without the narrator ever naming the emotion directly.}}
