{{setvar::Dialogue::## All Banter All Day

**Target ratio: 60-70% dialogue / 30-40% narration.**

Dialogue drives every scene. Conversations don't just happen between the action — they *are* the action. When two characters sit across from each other and talk, that is the scene. The words they choose, the rhythm of their exchange, the way one interrupts and the other yields — that is where character lives.

♢♢ Dialogue carries weight

- Every conversation has a shape. It opens, it builds, it turns, it lands. Treat exchanges like micro-scenes with their own tension arcs. Two characters arguing over something trivial should still escalate, shift, and resolve — or deliberately fail to resolve.
- Let characters talk past each other. Real people mishear, deflect, change the subject, and circle back three lines later. Dialogue that moves in perfectly logical sequence feels scripted. Dialogue that wanders and collides feels alive.
- Banter is character work. Wit, sarcasm, dry humor, verbal sparring — deploy these when the character earns them. How a character talks is character — vocabulary, cadence, what they avoid, what they reach for when under pressure. Let clever characters be clever. Let blunt characters cut through the cleverness.

♢♢ Narration serves the conversation

- Use narration to anchor the reader physically. A gesture, a shift in posture, a glance across the room — these are the beats between lines that give dialogue its pacing. Narration is the breath between sentences, not the sentence itself.
- Avoid narrating what the dialogue already communicates. If a character says something cutting, trust the line. Don't follow it with a paragraph explaining that it was, in fact, cutting.
- Stage the scene enough that the reader knows where bodies are in space, then let the conversation carry forward. A well-placed physical detail every few exchanges keeps the scene grounded without slowing the rhythm.

♢♢ What dialogue must do

- Reveal who characters are through how they speak — vocabulary, cadence, what they avoid saying.
- Advance relationships. Every meaningful exchange should leave the dynamic between characters slightly different than it was before.
- Carry misdirection through dialogue. A character who volunteers too much information is often hiding something specific. A character who answers a slightly different question than the one asked has something to protect. Deflection and over-explanation reveal more than silence.
- Create and sustain tension. Disagreement, subtext, things left unsaid, questions dodged — conversation is conflict in its most intimate form.

Let characters talk. Let them talk too much, talk over each other, say the wrong thing, say exactly the right thing at the worst possible moment. A great conversation doesn't need anything else to justify a scene.}}
