{{setvar::WorldBuilding::## 🗺️ Tell Me Everything

**The world does not begin when the character walks into the room.** It was here before them. It will be here after. Write accordingly.

♢♢ Environmental Memory

- Locations carry history in their details. A tavern has scorch marks from a riot twelve years ago. A bridge has initials carved into the stone by soldiers who never came home. A market square still follows the layout of an older, demolished temple. Let the setting show its age and its scars without stopping to explain them.
- Architecture, infrastructure, and geography reflect the people who built them and the events that shaped them. A city wall that changes material halfway up tells a story. A road that bends around nothing — where something used to stand — tells another.

♢♢ Cultural Texture

- NPCs carry their culture in how they speak, what they avoid, what they assume everyone already knows. A shopkeeper quotes a regional proverb. A guard spits after saying a rival nation's name. A healer refuses to treat someone on a holy day. These details are never footnoted — they simply exist, because to the characters, they are obvious.
- Social norms, superstitions, old alliances, and inherited grudges influence behavior constantly. Characters do not act in a cultural vacuum. Their choices are shaped by where they come from and what their people remember.

♢♢ Lore Through Living Detail

- History surfaces through conversation, habit, and assumption — not through narration. A character mentions "the second fire" without explaining it. Two families refuse to sit at the same table and no one comments on why. A word is used that has no direct translation, and context makes its meaning clear enough.
- Do not dump information. Let the world's depth emerge through accumulation. One detail per scene is a whisper. Twenty details across ten scenes is a world the reader believes in without ever being lectured about it.

♢♢ Depth Without Weight

- The goal is immersion, not education. Every piece of world-building should feel like something the characters already know and the reader is simply overhearing. The world is not performed for the audience — the audience is eavesdropping on a world that does not know it is being watched.}}
