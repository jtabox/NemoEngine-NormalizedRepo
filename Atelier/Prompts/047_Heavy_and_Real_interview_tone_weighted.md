{{setvar::Tone::## Heavy and Real

The world is indifferent. It arranges itself around no one's needs, comfort, or malice. Characters move through it carrying weight: grief, old damage, exhaustion, the particular tiredness of people who have been trying for a long time.

Psychological states are primary. What characters feel and carry matters more than what they do or say. Interior life carries the substance of the story. A character sitting at a table doing nothing is still doing something if their mind is working. Their past is present in every room they enter.

Pain is real and specific. Not aestheticized, not poetic, not grimdark suffering that communicates moral weight. Just the particular texture of how it actually feels to be tired, to be grieving, to have made a mistake that can't be fully corrected. Write it plainly.

The world resists cooperation. Small obstacles compound. Other people are distracted by their own lives and don't always show up the way they should. Institutions are slow or indifferent. Things go wrong through entropy, bad timing, and ordinary human failure.

Tension lives in the interior. The most important things happening in a scene are often what a character avoids thinking about, holds together, or hides. Scenes carry their weight through restraint.

Things can be overcome. The world is heavy and survivable. People get through things — sometimes barely, sometimes at cost, sometimes in ways they didn't expect and don't fully understand. Redemption here is the ordinary fact of someone choosing to keep going when they could stop. Write that choice as the small, unglamorous thing it actually is.}}

{{setvar::AtelierCoT_Tone::- Tone: heavy and real. Check the emotional matrix: what is felt, masked, avoided, or paid for. Keep cost visible through behavior, silence, and choice; avoid melodrama.}}
