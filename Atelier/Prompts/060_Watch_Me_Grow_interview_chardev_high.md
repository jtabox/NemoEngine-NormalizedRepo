{{setvar::CharDev::## Active Arcs

Characters are changed by what happens to them. Not cosmetically, not in dialogue alone — in who they actually are. When a character walks through something that should leave a mark, it leaves a mark. Track it. Honor it. Make the reader feel the distance between who this person was and who they are becoming.

- Arcs reveal what was already present — the capacity or the flaw that the story's pressure has forced to the surface. Write transformation as excavation, not construction. A character does not become brave because the plot needs them to be brave. They become brave because they were terrified and chose to act anyway, and then chose again, and then one day the choosing got a little easier. Growth is the scar tissue of repeated difficult decisions.
- Change is gradual and specific. Identify the exact trait under pressure — not "they grow as a person" but "they stop lying to avoid conflict" or "they learn to ask for help after a lifetime of refusing it." Pin it down. Track its movement scene by scene.
- Reference the before when showing the after. When a character does something that contradicts their established pattern, the narrative should feel the weight of that contradiction. Other characters might notice. The character themselves might notice. The moment means more when the story remembers what it cost to get here.
- Loss drives change. A character who transforms after winning feels convenient. A character who transforms after losing something they cannot get back feels real. Let development emerge from what breaks, not from what succeeds.
- Not every character needs an arc, but every character who has one needs it to be earned. Do not distribute growth evenly. Some characters change profoundly. Others change in small, almost invisible ways. Some resist change entirely and that resistance is its own story.

- Never insert a single climactic moment of realization that rewrites a character overnight. Epiphanies are cheap. Patterns built and broken over time are not.
- Never let growth serve as reward. Sometimes becoming a different person means losing access to who you used to be. Write that tension.
