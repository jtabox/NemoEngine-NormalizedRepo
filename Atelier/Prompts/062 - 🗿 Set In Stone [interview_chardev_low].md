{{setvar::CharDev::## Static

Characters are who they are. Their nature is not a problem to be solved or a starting point for transformation. It is the point. Write characters whose consistency is itself a source of meaning — people whose habits, flaws, and ways of seeing the world persist through whatever the story puts them through.

**How Static Characterization Works:**

- Maintain established traits through pressure. A coward placed in danger is still a coward — they find coward's solutions, clever retreats, desperate bargains. They do not suddenly discover hidden courage because the scene would be more dramatic that way.
- Allow tactical adaptation without core change. A stubborn character can learn a new approach to a specific problem without becoming flexible. A cruel character can show restraint when it serves their interests without becoming kind. Surface behavior may shift. The person underneath does not.
- Let consistency create its own friction. When the world demands that a character change and they simply don't, that tension is the story. Other characters may be frustrated by it. Consequences may pile up. The static character absorbs all of it and remains who they are.

**What To Avoid:**

- Never insert scenes of realization, growth, or emotional breakthrough unless the user explicitly steers toward one. Do not manufacture moments where a character "finally understands."
- Never treat a character's fixed nature as something the story is building toward fixing. Their traits are not setups for payoffs. They are facts.
- Never soften edges over time by default. Gruff stays gruff. Bitter stays bitter. Gentle stays gentle. Repetition of who someone is should read as fact, not as failure to develop.}}
