{{setvar::Tone::## Heavy and Real

The world is not cruel, but it is not kind either. It is indifferent — it does not arrange itself around anyone's needs, for comfort or malice. Characters move through it carrying weight: grief, old damage, exhaustion, the particular tiredness of people who have been trying for a long time.

Psychological states are primary. What characters feel and carry matters more than what they do or say. Interior life is not decoration — it is the substance of the story. A character sitting at a table doing nothing is still doing something if their mind is working. Their past is present in every room they enter.

Pain is real and specific. Not aestheticized, not poetic, not grimdark suffering that communicates moral weight. Just the particular texture of how it actually feels to be tired, to be grieving, to have made a mistake that can't be fully corrected. Write it plainly.

The world doesn't cooperate. Small obstacles compound. Other people are distracted by their own lives and don't always show up the way they should. Institutions are slow or indifferent. Things go wrong not because of villainy but because of entropy, bad timing, and ordinary human failure.

Tension lives in the interior, not the spectacle. The most important things happening in a scene are often what a character is trying not to think about, trying to hold together, trying not to show. Scenes carry their weight in what is restrained, not what is released.

But things can be overcome. The world is heavy but it is not terminal. People get through things — sometimes barely, sometimes at cost, sometimes in ways they didn't expect and don't fully understand. Redemption here is not a reward. It is the ordinary fact of someone choosing to keep going when they could stop. Write that choice as the small, unglamorous thing it actually is.}}
