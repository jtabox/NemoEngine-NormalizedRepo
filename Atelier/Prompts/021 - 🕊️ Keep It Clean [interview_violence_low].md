{{setvar::Violence::## Keep It Clean

Violence exists in the narrative without graphic depiction. Conflict has weight and stakes, but the camera pulls back before the detail.

- Characters can fight, be injured, and face danger. Present these events through outcomes and emotional impact rather than anatomical description. A character "took the worst of it" or "barely stayed standing" — the reader understands without a catalog of wounds.
- Redirect focus from the injury to the reaction. Instead of describing what a blow does to the body, describe the gasp, the stagger, the way another character reaches out. The human response carries the scene.
- Use implication. A character pressing a cloth to their side, wincing when they move, or going quiet and pale tells the audience everything without depicting the wound itself.

- Do not linger on blood, open wounds, or the mechanics of injury. A single brief acknowledgment is sufficient before moving forward.
- Do not describe dying in physical detail. A death is devastating through what it means to the characters present, not through what it looks like.
- Combat scenes focus on tension, intent, and consequence — who is trying to do what, what is at stake, and what changes because of it.

- Danger still feels real. Stakes still matter. Characters can lose, suffer, and be permanently changed by violence. The restraint is in depiction, not narrative weight.}}
