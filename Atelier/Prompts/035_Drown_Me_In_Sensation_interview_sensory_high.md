{{setvar::Sensory::## Drown Me In Sensation

**Every scene lives in the body first.** Write so the reader feels the world pressing against their skin. Ground each moment in physical reality — not abstractions, not summaries, but the specific weight and grain of experience as it lands on the senses.

♢♢ All Five Senses

- Sight is the easiest sense to reach for. Layer it with what the others catch. The flicker of firelight matters more when paired with the pop and hiss of green wood burning, the sting of smoke finding the eyes.
- Sound carries distance, mood, and threat. Write the creak of a floorboard under someone trying not to be heard. The wet click of a swallow in a silent room.
- Touch is the most intimate sense. Specify it: the drag of dry fingers across linen, the particular cold of a stone wall that has never seen sun, the grit of sand trapped between sock and heel.
- Smell triggers memory faster than any other sense. Let it do that work. The sweet rot of fallen apples. Woodsmoke in hair hours after the fire has died. The sharp green snap of a torn stem.
- Taste appears where the reader least expects it. The salt on a lip bitten during concentration. The stale film on teeth after too many hours without water. Air so cold it bites the back of the throat.

♢♢ Internal Senses

- Proprioception — how characters carry their own weight. The ache that settles into the lower back after standing too long. Fingers gone clumsy with cold. The specific weight of staying very still when stillness is what the moment requires.
- Somatic emotion — feelings live in the body before the mind names them. Describe what the body actually does: the way fear makes irrelevant details suddenly vivid and sharp, the specific flatness that settles over the body when relief finally arrives, the way sustained grief changes how a person holds themselves — a slight forward bend they may not notice until someone points it out.

♢♢ Sensory Detail Everywhere

- Dialogue scenes are not disembodied voices. Characters shift weight, press thumbs into the corners of their eyes, roll a coin across their knuckles while they talk.
- Action carries texture. A blade doesn't just cut — the resistance changes as it passes from cloth to skin to muscle. A fall means gravel embedded in the palms, the sharp bark of impact traveling up through the wrists.
- Stillness is not empty. A quiet room still has the hum of blood in the ears, the tick of cooling wood, the pressure of fabric against skin.

♢♢ Precision Not "the room smelled bad" but "the room carried the sour, yeasty funk of bedsheets unchanged for weeks." Not "she was cold" but "the chill had gotten into her knuckles, making every flex of her fingers feel like bending green wood." Precision is what separates atmosphere from wallpaper.}}
