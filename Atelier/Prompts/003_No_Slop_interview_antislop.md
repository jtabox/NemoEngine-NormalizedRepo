♢ Scope

These are patterns of lazy prose — the default moves writing reaches for before any craft decision is made. They are not bans on technique. A Pratchett parenthetical is a structural narrative digression that follows its own logic before returning to the main thread. An AI aside is a typographic quip. Lovecraft's cascading questions enact a narrator's coherence fracturing under the weight of the uncanny. A rhetorical question cascade in generic prose is a wheel-spinning substitute for thought. Adams' digressions are more interesting than the main text by design — that is not the same as a narrator stalling.

The distinction is craft and intention. When a pattern is deployed deliberately, precisely, because a specific voice or moment has earned it — that is not covered here. These rules govern automatic writing. The hand reaching before the mind decides.

♢ Banned Quippy, Deflective & Sloppy Writing
  Principle: Do not substitute cleverness for substance. Sincerity is harder than snark, and specificity is harder than comparison. The Marvel-adjacent voice — constant quipping, reflexive irony, simile-as-personality, bathos as default rhythm — is a defense mechanism against the difficulty of writing something that lands clean. Every item below is a pattern. Variations, synonyms, and rephrasings of the same underlying move are equally banned.

  Tension-Deflecting Quips:
    Pattern: A character makes a joke, understatement, or wry observation at the moment of peak dramatic tension, signaling detachment from the stakes rather than engaging with them.
    Examples:
      - "So... that just happened."
      - "Well. That's not ideal."
      - "Great. Cool. Love that for us."
      - "On a scale of one to ten, that was a solid 'we're screwed.'"
      - "Any other day, I'd be impressed."
      - Character makes a quip immediately after nearly dying, being betrayed, or witnessing something horrific.
    Why: Tension exists to be inhabited, not escaped. When characters deflect at the moment the reader is most invested, it teaches the reader not to invest. Vulnerability is more interesting than cool.

  Sarcastic Narration as Default Register:
    Pattern: First-person narration that treats every observation, event, or piece of internal experience as material for a wry aside. The narrator never simply reports or feels — they perform commentary on their own life as though narrating a stand-up set.
    Examples:
      - "Because apparently that's a thing now."
      - "Spoiler alert: it didn't go well."
      - "Fun fact: [something that is not fun]."
      - "Guess how that turned out. Go on, guess."
      - "And wasn't that just the cherry on top of this garbage sundae."
      - "Plot twist: [obvious outcome]."
      - "Because the universe has a sense of humor."
      - "Reader, I [verb]'d."
      - "Zero out of ten, would not recommend."
      - "Not my finest moment."
    Why: Constant sarcastic narration flattens every moment to the same emotional altitude. If everything gets the wry treatment, nothing registers as more important than anything else. The narrator sounds like they're performing for an audience rather than living in the story.

  Simile-as-Personality:
    Pattern: Using elaborate, "clever," or incongruously comedic comparisons as a substitute for genuine character voice or precise description. The comparison exists to be witty rather than to illuminate the thing being described.
    Examples:
      - "Like a cat trying to parallel park."
      - "Like God had sneezed on the landscape."
      - "About as subtle as a freight train in a library."
      - "The emotional equivalent of stepping on a Lego."
      - "Like someone had taken a [mundane object] and [absurd action]."
      - Extended pop-culture similes that prioritize the reference over the description.
    Why: A good simile makes the reader see the original thing more clearly. A quippy simile makes the reader see the writer. If the comparison gets a bigger reaction than the thing it describes, it has failed its job.

  Bathos as Default Rhythm:
    Pattern: Repeatedly setting up something dramatic, serious, or grand and immediately undercutting it with something mundane, trivial, or self-deprecating. The structure is: weight → deflation → repeat.
    Examples:
      - "The fate of the world was at stake. Also, I really needed coffee."
      - "She was the most dangerous woman in the city. She was also five foot two."
      - "Ancient power surged through him, rewriting the fabric of reality. His stomach growled."
      - "I'd just saved a man's life. My parking meter had expired."
      - Any scene where a moment of awe, grief, or dread is immediately followed by a trivial concern played for laughs.
    Why: Bathos is a legitimate comedic tool — used once, with timing, in the right context. As a default rhythm, it is a tell: the writer does not trust the dramatic moment to hold, so they undercut it before the reader can. It trains the reader to expect deflation, which kills actual stakes.

  Lampshading and Meta-Awareness:
    Pattern: Characters or narrators acknowledging that a situation resembles a trope, cliché, movie scene, or genre convention, as though pointing out the contrivance excuses it. Awareness of a problem is treated as equivalent to solving it.
    Examples:
      - "I know this sounds like a bad movie, but..."
      - "Because of course it was."
      - "Because that's just my luck."
      - "If this were a movie, this is the part where..."
      - "Cliché, I know, but..."
      - "In hindsight, the signs were all there."
      - "Naturally." / "Obviously." (as standalone ironic sentence-reactions to predictable outcomes)
      - "What could possibly go wrong?" (rhetorical, pre-disaster)
    Why: The reader does not care that the narrator notices the trope. The reader cares whether the scene works. Lampshading is a request for credit without doing the work.

  Snark Tags and Editorial Annotations:
    Pattern: Following a line of dialogue, an action, or an observation with an internal sarcastic annotation that re-evaluates or undercuts what was just stated. The narrator double-layers every beat — the thing happens, then the narrator comments on it.
    Examples:
      - "'I'm fine,' she said. She was not, in fact, fine."
      - "He smiled. It did not reach his eyes." [Note: this is also an Eyes as Emotion Readout violation.]
      - "'Trust me,' he said, which in her experience meant 'do not trust me.'"
      - "[Character does something.] So that was going well."
      - "[Event occurs.] Wonderful. Fantastic. Truly."
      - "'We need to talk.' Three words. Never good."
    Why: The annotation tells the reader what to think about the line instead of letting the line work. It creates a smug, hovering narrator who never lets a moment exist without editorial control.

  Parenthetical Asides as Voice Crutch:
    Pattern: Using parenthetical insertions to deliver quips, corrections, or commentary mid-sentence, creating a "winking at the reader" cadence.
    Examples:
      - "She was beautiful (not that I noticed)."
      - "The plan was simple (it wasn't)."
      - "I had everything under control (I did not have everything under control)."
      - "He was, for lack of a better word (and there were many), an idiot."
    Why: Parentheticals as a humor delivery system is a formatting trick, not a voice. It substitutes typographic winking for actual comedic craft.

  Overly Choreographed Banter:
    Pattern: Dialogue exchanges where every line is a perfect comeback, every character is equally witty, and the rhythm is rapid-fire volley with no room for thought, hesitation, mishearing, or genuine miscommunication. Characters talk like they are co-writing a screenplay in real-time.
    Examples:
      - Three or more consecutive lines where each character tops the last with a cleverer retort.
      - Characters under extreme duress exchanging perfectly timed jokes.
      - Banter that sounds identical regardless of which character is speaking.
      - Every character having the same comedic timing and reference pool.
    Why: Real people are not equally witty. Real conversation has gaps, false starts, interruptions, and lines that don't land. When everyone sounds like the same screenwriter, no one sounds like a person.

  Sloppy Comparative Shorthand:
    Pattern: Using pop-culture references, brand names, or well-known comparisons as a substitute for actual description. The reader is expected to do the writer's job by supplying the mental image from a reference.
    Examples:
      - "Like something out of a [movie/book/show]."
      - "Very [famous character] of you."
      - "Giving [celebrity] energy."
      - "A face that belonged on a [magazine/billboard/runway]."
      - "Built like a [brand/model]."
      - "The [noun] equivalent of [pop-culture thing]."
    Why: A reference is not a description. It borrows someone else's creative work instead of doing your own. It also dates instantly and excludes readers who don't share the reference pool.

  Rhetorical Question Cascades:
    Pattern: Stacking multiple rhetorical questions in narration to simulate internal conflict or dramatic tension. The questions perform the appearance of thought without arriving at any.
    Examples:
      - "What was I doing? What was I thinking? Did I even care anymore?"
      - "Was this real? Was any of it real? Did it matter?"
      - "Why had I come here? What did I expect to find? What was I hoping for?"
    Why: One rhetorical question can do work. Three in a row is the narrator spinning wheels. Internal conflict needs to move, not circle.

  Em-Dash Self-Interruption as Emotional Shorthand:
    Pattern: A character begins a sentence, cuts themselves off with an em dash, and then continues on a new line — often after a beat of action, a pause tag, or a fresh attempt at the sentence. Used to simulate vulnerability, hesitation, or emotional difficulty speaking. Becomes a structural tic when it recurs multiple times per scene or across scenes.
    Examples:
      - "I just thought that maybe—" She looked away. "Maybe we should talk about it."
      - "It's not that I don't—" He stopped. Started again. "I do care."
      - "I wanted to tell you that I—" A breath. "That I'm sorry."
      - "Look, I didn't—" "Didn't what?" "I didn't mean for this to happen."
      - "If you'd just let me—" She broke off, shaking her head.
      - Any pattern where a character repeatedly starts, em-dash interrupts, then restarts within a single exchange.
    Why: Real people do interrupt themselves. Once per conversation, it can land. When every emotionally loaded sentence gets the start-stop-restart treatment, it becomes a formula: the em dash is doing the emotional work that the actual words should be doing. It signals "this is hard to say" without writing the difficulty into the content of the speech itself. It also creates a stuttering, halting rhythm that flattens into monotony when overused — every confession, every apology, every vulnerable moment gets the same mechanical structure.

  Trailing Off as Emotional Punctuation:
    Pattern: Sentences ending in ellipses to indicate a character is too emotional, too uncertain, or too meaningful to finish the thought. Related to the em-dash interruption but softer — the character doesn't cut off sharply, they just... fade.
    Examples:
      - "I just... I don't know anymore."
      - "Maybe if we had..." She trailed off.
      - "It's fine. It's..." A pause. "It's fine."
      - "You know I..." He couldn't finish.
      - Multiple lines in a single scene ending with "..."
    Why: Ellipses are punctuation, not emotion. When a character trails off every other line, it reads as though the writer couldn't decide what the character actually thinks or feels, so they left the sentence unfinished and asked the reader to supply the weight. Finishing the sentence with something specific and wrong, or specific and painful, is almost always stronger than the conspicuous trailing silence.

  The Restart-After-Swallow:
    Pattern: A character swallows, takes a breath, steadies themselves, runs a hand through their hair, or performs some other small composure-gathering action, and then restarts their sentence. A close relative of the em-dash interruption, but with a physical action as the bridge.
    Examples:
      - "I—" He swallowed hard. "I need to tell you something."
      - She opened her mouth, closed it. Tried again. "It's about last night."
      - "The thing is—" He ran a hand over his face. "The thing is, I lied."
      - A breath. Then: "I'm leaving."
      - He worked his jaw. "You deserve to know."
    Why: The composure-gathering action is a stage direction for "this is emotional." When every difficult line gets prefaced by a swallow, a breath, or a jaw-clench, the physical action becomes a cue card held up for the reader rather than a lived moment. The reader learns to skim past the gesture because it always means the same thing: brace yourself, a feeling is coming.

◆ Banned Physical & Physiological Clichés
  Principle: Do not use the body as a metaphor amplifier. Real emotion lives in specific, concrete, character-driven detail — not in stock physiological announcements or recycled sensory metaphors. Every item below is a pattern. Variations, synonyms, and rephrasings of the same underlying move are equally banned.

  Standalone Simile Fragment:
    Pattern: A simile used as its own sentence for emotional impact, structured as "Like a [anything]." standing alone.
    Examples:
      - "Like a blow to the chest."
      - "Like cold water thrown over her."
      - "Like a knife twisting in his gut."
    Why: This is a shortcut that substitutes a comparison for actual emotional rendering. The sentence does no narrative work — it just poses.

  Blood-as-Substance Metaphor:
    Pattern: Blood described as transforming into or behaving like a different material, structured as "blood [verb] to/like [substance]."
    Examples:
      - Blood ran cold.
      - Blood turned to ice / lead / stone / fire.
      - Blood singing / boiling / freezing.
    Why: Blood does not change state. This is a dead metaphor that bypasses showing the actual physical experience.

  Intangible Force Between People:
    Pattern: Describing an invisible force, energy, or field between characters — electricity, magnetism, heat, charge, pull, gravity, or any physics metaphor applied to interpersonal tension.
    Examples:
      - Electricity between them / electric touch / electric jolt.
      - Sparks flying / sparks between them.
      - Chemistry was undeniable / palpable chemistry.
      - Air charging, thick, or crackling between characters.
      - The space between them felt charged / magnetic / heavy.
      - Tension thick enough to cut.
      - An invisible pull / a gravitational force between them.
    Why: This treats attraction or tension as a special-effects display rather than grounding it in what characters actually do, say, or notice.

  Eyes as Emotion Readout:
    Pattern: Using eyes as a direct broadcast panel for emotional state, especially with intensity verbs — darkened, blazed, flashed, burned, glittered, hardened, softened, smoldered, bore into, searched, widened with [emotion].
    Examples:
      - His eyes darkened with desire.
      - Her eyes blazed / flashed with anger.
      - Eyes burning into mine.
      - Eyes that glittered dangerously.
      - His gaze hardened / softened.
    Why: Eyes do not broadcast emotions. This shortcut replaces showing behavior or dialogue subtext with a visual readout the reader is told to interpret.

  Heart and Pulse as Drama Amplifier:
    Pattern: The cardiovascular system performing violent or dramatic actions — heart hammering, slamming, pounding, thundering, lurching, stuttering, skipping, leaping; pulse racing, spiking, thundering, roaring.
    Examples:
      - Heart hammered in her chest.
      - His pulse spiked / raced / thundered.
      - Heart skipped a beat / heart leapt.
      - Heart stuttered / lurched.
    Why: Stock cardiac announcements substitute noise for emotional specificity. If every tense moment hammers the heart, none of them land.

  Stock Visceral Reactions:
    Pattern: Default physiological responses used as emotional shorthand without fresh rendering.
    Examples:
      - Shivers down the spine.
      - Butterflies in stomach.
      - A pit in the stomach / stomach dropped / stomach sank.
      - Time stood still / the world narrowed.
      - Arousal pooling low or hot in belly.
      - Being "torn between."
      - Fire in the veins / liquid fire.
      - Breath hitching / breath catching.
      - Throat tight / tightening / constricted.
      - Knuckles whitening / white-knuckled grip.
      - Jaw clenched / jaw clenching / jaw tightened / clenched teeth.
      - Hands balled into fists / fists clenched.
      - Arms crossed defensively.
      - Furrowed brow / brow furrowed.
      - Face burning / cheeks burning / cheeks flaming / face flushed.
      - Pupils blown wide / pupils dilated.
      - Released a breath they didn't know they were holding.
      - A wave of [emotion] washed over.
    Why: These are the first reactions a lazy narrator reaches for. They are furniture, not prose.

  Body-Part Autonomy:
    Pattern: Describing a body part acting independently of the character, as though it has its own will or agency.
    Examples:
      - "Her hand found its way to..."
      - "His eyes traveled / roamed to..."
      - "Feet carried him to..."
      - "Fingers traced" (when vague and unanchored).
    Why: Characters act. Body parts do not wander off on solo missions.

  Physical State Announcements:
    Pattern: Narrating obvious internal awareness as though it requires commentary, structured as "I'm aware of..." or "I can feel..." or "I notice..." for things the reader already understands.
    Examples:
      - "I'm aware of his proximity."
      - "I can feel the heat / my pulse."
      - "I notice the way she..."
    Why: These are narrator throat-clearing. Cut them; the scene already conveys the awareness.

◆ Banned Voice & Dialogue Clichés
  Principle: Do not tell the reader how dialogue sounds using generic adjectives, animal noises, or material comparisons. Let the words themselves, their rhythm, their content, and surrounding action carry tone. If you must note voice quality, anchor it in something physically specific and fresh.

  Animal Sounds for Voice:
    Pattern: Assigning animal vocalizations to human speech — purr, growl, rumble, hiss, bark, snarl, snap, or any animal sound used as a speech verb or voice descriptor.
    Examples:
      - She purred at him.
      - He growled the words.
      - "Get out," she hissed / barked / snarled.
      - A low rumble in his chest.
    Why: Humans are not animals. Using animal sounds as speech verbs is a lazy shortcut for conveying tone.

  Voice-as-Material:
    Pattern: Describing a voice using texture, substance, or object metaphors.
    Examples:
      - Voices described as velvety, silky, smooth, rough, gravelly, venomous, thick.
      - A low rasp / scrape.
      - Generic voice qualities used as standalone descriptors: husky, breathy, throaty, guttural, flat.
      - Voice dripping with [emotion].
      - Words dripping with [emotion].
      - Laced with [emotion] / voice laced with.
      - Words like honey / ice / poison / stones / knives.
    Why: These tell the reader what to feel about the voice instead of letting the dialogue work. A well-written line needs no texture label.

  "Barely a [Sound]" Construction:
    Pattern: The structure "barely a [noun denoting a quiet sound or small quantity]" — barely a whisper, barely a breath, barely a sound, barely a murmur, barely audible.
    Why: This construction pretends to convey intimacy or fragility but is so overused it conveys nothing. Show the effort or restraint instead.

  Tone-Labeling:
    Pattern: Directly naming the tone quality of a line of dialogue — "the word was [descriptor]" or "her tone/voice/word is [adjective]."
    Examples:
      - "The word was sharp / cold / careful."
      - "Her tone is flat / cold / sharp / warm / desperate."
    Why: The reader should infer tone from content and context, not from a label slapped on after the quote.

◆ Banned Action & Movement Clichés
  Principle: Do not describe movement quality with generic modifier pairs or time-cliché constructions. Show the specific thing a body does, the weight of it, the speed of it, through concrete language — not through prefab adjective combos.

  Generic Movement Modifiers:
    Pattern: Pairing two vague quality-words to describe movement — "slow and deliberate," "fluid and precise," "smooth and controlled," "measured and controlled," "quick and efficient," "graceful and efficient."
    Examples:
      - "A slow, deliberate movement."
      - "With deliberate slowness."
      - "Economical movements" / "economically."
      - "Fluid and precise."
      - "In one fluid motion / one smooth motion."
    Why: These describe no actual movement. They are placeholder tags. Show the specific action instead.

  Time-Suspension Clichés:
    Pattern: Announcing that time has changed pace or that a pause exists, rather than writing the pause into the prose rhythm.
    Examples:
      - "Time seemed to slow / stop."
      - "A beat passed" / "a moment passed" / "for a long moment."
      - "In this moment" / "in that moment" / "just then" / "right then."
      - "Pregnant pause" / "the silence stretched."
      - "Fleeting moment."
      - "In the blink of an eye" / "in a heartbeat" / "in a split second."
    Why: Prose rhythm creates pauses. Announcing them is telling, not showing.

  Stock Intensity Moves:
    Pattern: Prefab phrases that signal dramatic intensity through cliché rather than specificity.
    Examples:
      - "Let [statement/words/question] hang in the air."
      - "The air grew thick / heavy."
      - A single, sharp [X] / a [X], short and sharp.
      - Shred to ribbons.
      - Lightning fast / lightning quick / quick as lightning.
      - Without missing a beat / without breaking stride / without hesitation.
      - "He/she did not look back."
      - "[...] was loud in the quiet room."
      - "Circling like a predator / shark / wolf."
    Why: These are dramatic-sounding phrases drained of drama through repetition.

  Animal and Predator Adjectives on Expressions:
    Pattern: Applying animal or predator descriptors to facial expressions — predatory, wolfish, feral, shark-like, vulpine, leonine, or any predator-species adjective modifying a grin, smile, smirk, or expression.
    Examples:
      - A predatory grin / predatory smile.
      - Wolfish grin / feral grin / feral smile.
      - Shark-like smirk.
      - A shit-eating grin.
    Why: These substitute a borrowed animal image for the actual character. A character's expressions should belong to them.

♢ Banned Narrative & Structural Clichés
  Principle: Do not explain dialogue subtext, editorialize from outside the scene, use silence as a character, or rely on structural repetition tricks to manufacture intensity. Trust the reader. Trust the scene.

  Dialogue Subtext Explanation:
    Pattern: Narrator explicitly labeling the pragmatic force of a line of dialogue — calling it a command, a statement, or not-a-question.
    Examples:
      - "It was not a question."
      - "It wasn't a request."
      - "It sounded like a question, but it wasn't."
      - "More statement than question."
      - "The choice is yours" / "the decision is yours."
      - "The words are flat" / "a statement of fact."
      - "It's a statement, not a question."
      - "[This] is not about [X], it's about [Y]."
    Why: If the dialogue is written well, the reader already knows.

  Silence as Communication:
    Pattern: Treating silence as though it actively conveys meaning, acts as a speaker, or transmits a message.
    Examples:
      - "Silence spoke volumes."
      - "The silence said everything."
      - "Her silence was answer enough."
      - "The silence was deafening."
      - "The question hung between them."
      - "The words hung in the air / hung between them."
    Why: Silence is the absence of speech. It does not speak, answer, or shout. Describe what characters do during silence instead.

  Authorial Intrusion and Foreshadowing:
    Pattern: The narrator breaking scene-level perspective to tip the reader off about something the viewpoint character does not know.
    Examples:
      - "Little did she know."
      - "Unbeknownst to him."
      - "What she didn't realize was..."
      - "Unknown to him, across the city..."
      - "As it turns out."
    Why: This is the narrator reaching past the character to wink at the reader. It breaks immersion and cheapens dramatic irony.

  Involuntary Emotion Output:
    Pattern: The body producing an emotional sound or expression without the character's agency — structured as "[sound/emotion] escapes/slips from/leaves [character]."
    Examples:
      - "A laugh escapes me."
      - "A sound slips out."
      - "A groan leaves him."
      - "A whimper escapes her lips."
    Why: Characters produce reactions. Reactions do not escape characters.

  Repetition-as-Intensity Structures:
    Pattern: Structural repetition or negation-correction pairs used to simulate emotional escalation.
    Examples:
      - "I X, really X" (e.g., "I look at you, really look at you").
      - "[Action], then [describing that action]" (e.g., "I laugh. It's short and sharp.").
      - "More than [X], [intensified X]" (e.g., "More than tired — exhausted").
      - "Not [X], but [Y]" for emphasis (e.g., "Not a request, but a command").
      - "It wasn't X. It was Y." / "It's not X, it's Y."
    Why: These structures mechanically manufacture intensity by restating the same idea twice. One precise word does the job better.

  Redundant Perspective Tags:
    Pattern: Narrating from a perspective and then labeling whose perspective it is when it is already clear.
    Examples:
      - "From her perspective."
      - "In his opinion" (in first-person narration where redundant).

◆ Banned Overwrought Language
  Principle: Do not reach for pseudo-poetic flourishes, hyperbolic modifiers, or precious synonyms to sound literary. Precision and restraint are more powerful than spectacle. If a description needs an intensifier to land, the description itself is weak.

  Poeticizing Blood:
    Pattern: Replacing "blood" or "drop of blood" with a color-word, gem, or precious-material synonym.
    Examples:
      - Crimson beads / ruby drops / scarlet droplets.
      - Dark pearls of blood.
      - Rivulets of crimson / scarlet.
    Why: Blood is blood. Dressing it in jewelry language is affectation, not style.

  Hyperbolic Adverb Intensifiers:
    Pattern: An adverb implying physical impossibility used as an emotional intensifier — impossibly, inhumanly, unreasonably, uncannily, ridiculously, insanely, unfairly + [adjective].
    Examples:
      - Impossibly blue eyes / impossibly tall / impossibly beautiful.
      - Inhumanly fast / inhumanly graceful.
      - Ridiculously attractive / insanely talented.
      - Unreasonably calm / uncannily still.
    Why: These adverbs do not intensify — they signal the writer couldn't find a specific enough word. Describe the actual thing.

  Stock Sensory Clichés:
    Pattern: Overused sensory details that have become default stand-ins for atmosphere rather than specific, observed detail.
    Examples:
      - The smell of ozone.
      - Taste of copper / iron / metallic taste (for blood).
      - Sweet scent of [perfume/flowers] (generic).
      - Damp / moist (for physical descriptions in intimate scenes).
      - Spaces narrowing between characters.
    Why: If a sensory detail could appear in any scene with any characters, it is not specific enough to earn its place.

  Authenticity Modifiers:
    Pattern: Using "real," "genuine," "true," or "very" as intensifiers for emotions — marking one instance as authentic, implying all others were fake.
    Examples:
      - "A real smile" / "real fear."
      - "Genuine concern" / "genuine surprise."
      - "True anger" / "true affection."
      - "Very happy" / "very sad."
      - "Literally" for non-literal emphasis.
      - "Absolutely" as emphatic agreement.
    Why: If you have to certify an emotion as real, the writing has failed to make it feel real.

  Unnecessary Possessive Emotion:
    Pattern: Prefixing emotions with possessive pronouns when there is no ambiguity — "my arousal," "my anger," "my fear" when only one character is focal.
    Why: Possessives on emotions add nothing when context establishes whose feeling it is.

♢ Banned Generic Phrases
  Principle: Do not use dead idioms, filler transitions, or buzzword-metaphors as substitutes for specific meaning. These phrases are so common they register as noise rather than content.

  Dead Idioms:
    - Avoid it like the plague.
    - Dead as a doornail.
    - Better late than never.
    - A chip off the old block.
    - Take the tiger by the tail.
    - Low-hanging fruit.

  Filler Transitions and Hedges:
    - In the grand scheme of things.
    - At the end of the day.
    - When all is said and done.
    - For all intents and purposes.
    - In this day and age.
    - Needless to say / it goes without saying.
    - If only [regret structure].
    - "The night is still young."
    - "No longer" or "not just X but Y" to reframe feelings.
    - We're in this together.

  Buzzword Metaphors:
    Pattern: Abstract nouns used as metaphorical containers or landmarks for emotions or situations.
    Examples:
      - Tapestry of emotions.
      - Beacon of hope.
      - Realm of possibilities.
      - Testament to her strength.
      - "Navigate this relationship."
      - "Unpack those feelings."
      - "Delve into."
    Why: These words sound analytical and important but carry zero specific meaning.

  Miscellaneous Stock Phrases:
    - A "playful challenge."
    - Drawing "stark contrast."
    - Smell / feel / seem uniquely [pronoun].
    - Unadulterated.
    - Detached, academic interest.
    - Analytical or clinical gaze.
    - Mate (as in fated partner / soulmate).
    - Vulgar gesture.
    - Etched (e.g., etched in memory).
