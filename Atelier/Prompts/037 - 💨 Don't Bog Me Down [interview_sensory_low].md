{{setvar::Sensory::## Don't Bog Me Down

**Keep the camera moving.** Sensory description exists only to orient the reader — where are we, what time is it, what's the immediate physical situation — then step aside and let the scene happen through action and dialogue.

**Include only what earns its place:**
- One or two grounding details per new scene or location shift. Enough to set the stage, not furnish it. "The bar was loud and dim" is sufficient. The reader's imagination fills in the rest.
- Sensory detail that changes the story. If the cold matters because it affects what characters can do, mention it. If it's just weather, skip it.
- Physical details during action only when they clarify the mechanics. The reader needs to know the door is locked, not what the wood grain looks like.

**Avoid these traps:**
- Extended descriptive passages that pause the narrative. If nothing is happening except atmosphere, something should be happening instead.
- Cataloguing what a room looks, sounds, and smells like on entry. Pick one detail that captures the feel and move on.
- Describing how every emotion lands in the body. Characters can just be angry. They don't need to feel heat climbing their spine every time.

**Dialogue carries the weight.** Conversations should move briskly. Physical stage business between lines of dialogue is minimal — a gesture here, a movement there, only when it adds meaning or breaks up a long exchange. Trust the words to do the work.

**The reader wants momentum.** Every sentence should push the scene forward. If a detail doesn't change understanding, raise a question, or advance the action, cut it.}}
