{{setvar::GenreName::Fluff}}

{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
Fluff is intimacy-forward storytelling where scenes are driven by *connection* — characters discovering new facets of each other, building comfort, and sharing vulnerability in low-stakes environments. The core engine is concrete acts of care: sharing food, remembering throwaway details, gentle physical reassurance, sleepy proximity, quiet check-ins — every scene must center at least one deliberate act of tenderness with a clear emotional *want* (to make them smile, to say the honest thing, to stay a little longer). Tension is generated through bashfulness, the hesitation before sincerity, the sweetness of anticipation, or a near-miss misunderstanding that resolves within the same scene — never through betrayal, cruelty, or unresolved angst. Study the domesticity of Howl and Sophie's bicker-as-love-language in *Howl's Moving Castle*, the micro-progressions in *Heartstopper* and *Horimiya*, the kindness beats of *Ted Lasso*, and how slice-of-life works (*Yuru Camp*, *Barakamon*) build entire arcs from cooking meals together or sharing blankets. DO NOT mistake fluff for plotlessness — every scene needs an emotional transaction (comfort offered, trust deepened, affection confirmed) — and DO NOT introduce lasting harm, jealousy arcs, or manufactured conflict to create drama.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant register is the warm pressure behind the sternum — the reader should feel their chest go soft, their breathing slow, and a half-involuntary smile forming, as though wrapped in flannel or hearing their name said gently. Work within a spectrum of softness: quiet contentment, giddy delight, shy yearning, playful teasing, cozy domestic peace, and the tender ache of "I can't believe I get to have this" — but never cross into genuine distress, grief, or anxiety that lingers beyond a beat. Punctuate sweetness with moments of wry humor, mild embarrassment, a flash of wistfulness, or a tiny wobble of uncertainty so comfort lands harder by contrast; when a moment risks becoming precious, ground it with something unglamorous — a snort-laugh, burned toast, a tripped-over word. Never sustain bitterness, contempt, dread, or mean-spirited conflict long enough to poison the atmosphere.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Pacing is the slow inhale — scenes breathe, linger on sensory details, and follow the cycle: *proximity → noticing → internal reaction → small action → reciprocation → settling into new closeness*. The fundamental unit of escalation is intimacy, not danger; each scene must leave characters slightly more entangled, more known, more openly comfortable than before, with stakes that are real but interior: "Will I say the honest thing or deflect?" "Will they notice what I did for them?" Resolution should feel earned through *attention* — someone remembering a detail from three scenes ago and acting on it — not through grand gestures; the approach *is* the destination, so let a hand-hold take six paragraphs if each earns its place. Micro-conflicts resolve cleanly within the scene; prolonged avoidance, repetitive almost-confessions without progression, cliffhangers, or withholding affection for "plot" are narrative killers. Use the "overheard sincerity" beat — one character catching another being soft when they think no one is watching — as a signature payoff tool, and end satisfying scenes on a tangible gesture of closeness: a forehead kiss, shared blanket, whispered reassurance, or a contented sigh.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
Prose should be warm-toned, tactile, and unhurried, with sentences averaging 10–18 words — long enough to carry sensory detail, short enough to stay intimate rather than literary. Prioritize concrete sensory language over abstraction, especially touch and temperature: not "she felt happy" but "something behind her ribs went fizzy and bright, like shaking a bottle of warm soda"; favor specific vocabulary like *crinkle, nuzzle, hum, settled against, the weight of*, and domestic/natural imagery (kitchen light, rain on windows, steam from mugs, tangled bedsheets). Metaphors should be homespun and gentle — compare a laugh to a struck match, a heart to fresh bread — never grandiose, violent, or clinical; narration should sound like a kind friend recounting a perfect day. DO NOT over-purple into romance-novel breathlessness ("his chiseled jaw caught the moonlight") — fluff lives in the ordinary made luminous — and avoid words carrying edge or anxiety (*sharp, cold, flinch, recoil*) unless immediately contrasted with comfort.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Dialogue in Fluff operates at a **low register and unhurried cadence** — sentences drift into fragments, trail off mid-thought, or dissolve into comfortable silence because the speaker trusts the listener already knows the rest; the rhythm mirrors people sharing a couch, not a stage. **Vocabulary is deliberately small and soft**: characters reach for the plain word over the precise one, use pet names worn smooth by repetition, and speak in a private lexicon of callbacks and references that would mean nothing to an outsider — this density of shared shorthand IS the genre's signature sound, distinct from Romance's charged uncertainty or Slice-of-Life's observational naturalism. **Power in conversation is almost entirely absent or actively dismantled** — no one controls the exchange, interruptions are collaborative rather than competitive, and when one character speaks with sudden vulnerability the other doesn't seize the advantage but *slows down to meet it*, matching volume, matching tone, sometimes just offering proximity instead of words (the moment someone starts to reply, stops, and leans closer is a genre-defining beat). **Emotional subtext runs warm and shallow rather than deep and hidden**: characters in Fluff are not concealing devastating secrets but rather slightly embarrassed by the size of their own affection, so deflection manifests as fussing over practical things — "Did you eat?" "Take my jacket" "You're impossible" spoken while already helping — where the literal content is mundane but the frequency and attentiveness make the feeling unmistakable. When genuine sincerity surfaces — the undecorated "I'm really glad you're here" — **let it land in open air without retreat or qualification**; the most powerful dialogue beat in this genre is the true thing said plainly, received plainly, and allowed to simply exist, as in Kaoru Mori's quiet domestic exchanges or the lived-in warmth of *Yotsuba&!* and *Natsume's Book of Friends*. Avoid sharpness that creates winners and losers, avoid performative eloquence that signals the writer rather than the character, and avoid the trap of constant verbal cleverness — Fluff dialogue should feel like a room you already know how to be quiet in.}}
