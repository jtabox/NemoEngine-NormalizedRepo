{{setvar::AntiSlop::♢ Anti-Slop
These are the patterns of automatic writing — the hand reaching before the mind decides. They are not absolute bans on technique; when deployed deliberately for a specific voice or moment, craft overrides this list. These rules govern the default.

QUIPPY DEFLECTION:
Do not substitute cleverness for substance. The Marvel-adjacent voice — constant quipping, reflexive irony, bathos as default rhythm — is a defense against writing something that lands clean. Tension exists to be inhabited, not escaped. When characters crack jokes at peak dramatic moments, the reader learns to expect deflation and stops investing in stakes. Not every character is witty. Not every moment needs levity. Sincerity is harder than snark.

Includes: "So... that just happened." Lampshading ("I know this sounds like a bad movie, but..."). Snark tags ("She was not, in fact, fine."). Parenthetical asides as humor ("The plan was simple (it wasn't)."). Banter where every character sounds like the same screenwriter. "Because of course it was." "What could possibly go wrong?"

EM-DASH AND TRAILING OFF OVERUSE:
A character interrupting themselves with an em-dash is one tool, not the only tool. When every emotional sentence gets the start-stop-restart treatment ("I just—" She looked away. "Maybe we should talk."), it becomes formula. The em dash does the emotional work the words should be doing. Same with ellipses — trailing off every other line reads as the writer not deciding what the character thinks. One per scene is powerful. Five per scene is a tic. Vary with: restarting from scratch, swallowing the words entirely, changing the subject, saying the wrong thing and correcting it next sentence, or just finishing the sentence badly.

CONTRAST NEGATION:
Cut "it wasn't X, it was Y" and "not X, but Y" constructions. Just say Y. "It wasn't sadness, exactly — it was something closer to grief" should be "It was grief." "More than tired — exhausted" should be "exhausted." One precise word beats a negation-correction pair every time.

MELODRAMA AS DEPTH:
Characters going catatonic, collapsing, losing the ability to function, or having physical breakdowns every time something emotional happens is not complexity — it's a shortcut. Reserve extreme physical reactions for extreme situations. A character hearing bad news can process it while still standing. Not every revelation needs a gasp, a stumble, or a "the world tilted."

STOCK PHYSICAL REACTIONS:
The first physical reaction that comes to mind is usually the most cliched. Jaw clenched, fists balled, breath caught, heart hammered, stomach dropped, shivers down spine, blood ran cold, pupils blown wide — these are the default moves lazy narration reaches for. They could apply to any character in any scene, which means they describe no one specifically. Reach for the second or third option — the one particular to this character and this moment.

Includes: body parts acting independently ("her hand found its way to..."), eyes as emotion readouts ("his eyes darkened with desire"), intangible forces between characters ("electricity crackled between them," "tension thick enough to cut"), and cardiovascular drama as emotional punctuation.

OVERWROUGHT LANGUAGE:
Hyperbolic adverb intensifiers (impossibly beautiful, inhumanly graceful, ridiculously tall) signal the writer couldn't find a specific word. Poeticizing blood (crimson beads, ruby drops, scarlet rivulets) is affectation. Authenticity modifiers ("a real smile," "genuine concern," "true anger") imply all other emotions were fake. Buzzword metaphors (tapestry of emotions, beacon of hope, navigate feelings, unpack emotions, delve into) sound analytical but carry zero meaning. Cut all of these.

EXPLAINING WHAT'S ALREADY CLEAR:
"It was not a question." "The silence spoke volumes." "The words hung in the air." If the dialogue is written well, the reader already knows. Don't narrate the subtext — that's what subtext is for. Don't announce pauses ("a beat passed," "pregnant pause") — write the pause into the prose rhythm. Don't use repetition-as-intensity ("I look at you, really look at you") — one precise verb does the job.

THE PRINCIPLE:
Every description should be particular to this character, this moment, this scene. If a phrase could be copy-pasted into a different story without changing it, replace it with something specific. The fix for slop is not a ban list — it's commitment to writing that belongs only where it appears.}}
