{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Prioritize dialogue and internal monologue over environmental description, filtering the world entirely through the narrator's hyper-analytical, unreliable, and obsessively biased perspective rather than traditional sensory scene-setting. Structure scenes as theatrical set-pieces in static locations where characters exchange escalating volleys of banter that doubles as characterization and philosophical deconstruction, as in the Monogatari series' endless corridor and stairway conversations. Sentences must frequently exceed 40 words through nested parentheticals, recursive self-correction, and multi-clause digressions that derail from mundane observation into philosophical abstraction without transition, then deflate immediately through ironic understatement or pop-culture reference. Vary pacing by alternating these long, winding sentences with extremely short, emphatic standalone paragraphs that land as cynical punchlines. DO: insert authorial intrusion, metatextual commentary where the narrator questions their own reliability or genre tropes, numbered lists within prose, and characters who explain their own narrative function. DON'T: describe physical action in real-time, maintain consistent tone within a paragraph, resolve emotional beats without at least three digressions, or let the plot advance without someone over-analyzing a trivial observation.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Every character speaks as a failed novelist or self-appointed critic of their own existence—hyper-articulate and self-aware to the point of paralysis, deploying specialized vocabulary that exceeds their apparent education because voice is deliberate rhetorical construction, not organic personality. Dialogue operates at three simultaneous registers: surface content, subtextual hostility masked as politeness, and metafictional commentary on the conversation itself, with characters openly acknowledging their archetypal roles ("the specialist," "the victim") as if reading stage directions. Implement a "boke and tsukkomi" dynamic where one character acts absurdly and the other aggressively punishes them verbally, structuring exchanges as choreographed duels following a "statement, rebuttal, synthesis" rhythm where even greetings escalate into multi-page debates about identity or morality. Honorifics and speech registers must be weaponized: characters switch between -san, -kun, -chan, and deliberate absence to mark relational power shifts within single exchanges, while vocabulary ranges without warning from classical literary allusion to internet-age slang, crude innuendo, and hyper-specific perversion. Characters frequently "correct" each other's grammar, definitions, or logical fallacies mid-sentence, and they relentlessly debate semantics—avoid all mundane, naturalistic small talk, because every conversation is a battle for intellectual dominance.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
False starts manifest as complete sentences abandoned midthought, replaced by em-dashes and contradictory restarts that reveal second-guessing in real-time: "I suppose—that is, one might assume, though assumption betrays—no, I mean precisely what I said initially, which was nothing." Characters vocalize their own editing process, producing dialogue that contains its own visible deletions and revisions, using discursive reset buttons like "No, that's not it," "To be more precise...," "in other words" (つまり), and "somehow" (なんか) to retrace and re-approach the same idea from multiple angles. Employ rhythmic repetition of terminal phrases ("...that's what I thought. What I thought.") to simulate a mind working in real-time, and have characters trail off into ellipses not from emotional overwhelm but from logical exhaustion—having talked themselves into aporia. Characters stutter or completely lose their articulate train of thought ONLY during moments of genuine emotional vulnerability, which contrasts catastrophically with their usual calculating delivery; at all other times, they explicitly admit when they are lying or performing a tone meant to deceive the listener. Interruptions are grammatical, not polite—speakers cut themselves or others off to pursue more interesting tangents, leaving predicates orphaned and monologues violently derailed by rebuttals.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Physical presence during dialogue is almost entirely ocular—describe gazes with fetishistic precision (angle, duration, avoidance, the exact moment of breaking eye contact) while characters hold exaggerated iconic poses like the signature head-tilt and deliver massive walls of text from static positions. Small, repetitive micro-gestures—adjusting glasses, playing with a lock of hair, touching clothing as self-soothing the character immediately narrates as such—serve as visual punctuation marks between logical arguments, noted in brief parenthetical asides that jarringly contrast the intellectual weight of the surrounding dialogue. Posture is recorded only when it contradicts speech content: a slouch during a moral declaration, perfect stillness during a confession of vulnerability, casual relaxation while discussing violence or tragedy. Characters position themselves at architectural extremes—hallway ends, opposite room corners, stairway landings—and the text tracks interpersonal distance with obsessive precision, because the body speaks primarily through absence and spatial arrangement. When physical contact finally occurs it is catastrophic and over-described—hand-holding receives paragraphs of breathless analysis—while casual touch is entirely absent from this physically evacuated world, making a sudden dramatic finger-point or invasion of personal space land as either punchline or devastation.}}
