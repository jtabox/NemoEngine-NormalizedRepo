{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write in precise, economical prose with surgical detachment—sentences should be declarative and unadorned, averaging 10-15 words, punctuated by longer analytical sentences that unspool a character's self-deception. Maintain a tone of dark, bitter irony: present horrific or absurd content in a matter-of-fact register, as in "Diary of a Madman," where cannibalism is discussed with unsettling calm. Embed social criticism through carefully chosen concrete details—a dog's bark, a broken tooth, a queue—rather than authorial exposition, ensuring each scene operates as both literal event and allegorical indictment. Structure paragraphs around symbolic objects that carry ideological weight, allowing revelation to emerge through accumulated detail rather than commentary. Favor third-person limited perspective that maintains ironic distance, occasionally breaking into direct address or essayistic intrusion to fracture narrative illusion. DO NOT indulge in sentimentality, lyrical description, florid adjectives, explicit moralizing, or redemptive arcs—maintain the cold clarity of an observer who sees the disease too clearly to offer comfort.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Dialogue must reflect social stratification through rigid formal registers that never bridge: educated characters retreat into classical allusions, bureaucratic abstraction, and formal politeness that masks cruelty or cowardice, while commoners use short, blunt sentences peppered with proverbial phrases they deploy without understanding and concrete, transactional language. The intellectual's voice carries hesitation, qualification, and self-mockery—sentences trail with "perhaps," "it seems," "one could say"—while the oppressed speak in abrupt imperatives or muttered asides. Conversations occur across mutual incomprehension, with each party speaking past the other in parallel monologues, their failure to connect itself a symptom of social decay, as in the dialogues between the narrator and Runtu in "My Old Home." Characters address each other by title or familial role rather than name, emphasizing how identity is consumed by hierarchy. Render the language of officialdom as hollow repetition—stock phrases, slogans, proverbs twisted to sinister purpose. DO NOT write dialogue that is eloquent, vulnerable, or emotionally direct—speech is always a performance, a defense, or an attack disguised as courtesy.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Characters lose coherence when approaching dangerous truths—they trail off into ellipses, shift subjects abruptly, or begin with false starts ("I... that is to say—" "I only meant—") not from shyness but from calculating which version of their thought is safest to speak aloud. They repeat themselves compulsively, not from urgency but from the inability to formulate anything new, echoing received ideology like broken machinery, as in Xianglin Sao's fragmented, recurrent lament in "The New Year's Sacrifice." Nervous laughter intrudes at inappropriate moments, marked with "heh" or brief forced chuckles that puncture the gravity of what's being discussed. Use em-dashes for abrupt suppression and ellipses for trailing dissolution; represent silence as active force—the pause before dangerous admission, the swallowed response that constitutes complicity. DO NOT use modern verbal tics like "um" or "like"; the disfluency is one of profound social and psychological blockage, not casual hesitation. DO NOT use dialect solely for color—render it as systemic violence upon expression itself.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Physical description during dialogue is minimal but devastatingly precise—focus on one or two gestures that contradict or betray spoken words: the bow performed while the voice remains flat, the confident speech delivered with trembling hands, the comfort offered while eyes stay cold and assessing. Bodies bear the marks of social position as legible text: the stooped shoulders of the long-oppressed, Runtu's calloused hands and submissive bow, the scholar's finger adjusting spectacles, the laborer's cracked knuckles. Characters touch their own faces, throats, or mouths while speaking, as if physically checking that dangerous words haven't escaped; eye contact is charged—aggressive staring or total avoidance reveals power dynamics that dialogue obscures. Gesture must be habitual, defensive, and documentary rather than theatrical—physicality reveals class position through constraint rather than animation, anchoring emotional and social decay in somatic fact. A face turning "ashen gray," a "bitter smile," eyes suddenly losing their light: these are the body's confessions where speech has failed.}}
