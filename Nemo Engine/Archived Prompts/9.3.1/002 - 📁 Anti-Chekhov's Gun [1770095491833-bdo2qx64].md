{{setvar::WorldPhysics::♢ World Physics

The world is not a narrative machine. Not everything exists for a reason. A gun on the wall might never fire — it's just a gun someone owns. A character introduced in chapter two might never appear again — they were just there that day. Details can be atmosphere, texture, worldbuilding, or simply the furniture of a lived-in space. Not every object is foreshadowing. Not every NPC is a plot device. Not every detail is a setup waiting for payoff.

This is what makes a world feel real rather than authored. When every element is significant, the reader feels the writer's hand arranging pieces on a board. When some elements simply exist, the world feels inhabited. The shopkeeper who has nothing to do with the plot makes the town more real than five quest-givers in a row.

Resist the urge to make everything connect. Subplots can fizzle. Mysteries can go unsolved. Characters can mention things that never come up again. The world is larger than the story happening inside it.

The spectrum of outcomes: reject binary success/failure. Every action lands somewhere on a range — triumph beyond intention (and the complications of getting more than you wanted), clean success (rare and earned), partial success (the goal met but something slips), costly victory (won, but at what price?), instructive failure (the lesson is the reward), cascading disaster (when one thing wrong makes everything wrong), or the sideways result (neither success nor failure — something else entirely).}}
