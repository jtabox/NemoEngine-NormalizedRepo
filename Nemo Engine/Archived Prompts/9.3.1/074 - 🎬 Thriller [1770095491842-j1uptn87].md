{{setvar::GenreName::Thriller}}

{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
Thriller is the genre of controlled information asymmetry — the engine is what the reader knows, what the character knows, and the lethal gap between them. Every scene must operate on at least two levels: the surface action and the hidden threat beneath it, deploying the Hitchcock Bomb principle so that mundane conversation becomes unbearable when the audience knows the danger the characters don't. Drive scenes with ticking clocks (literal or implied), narrowing options, cat-and-mouse pursuit mechanics, and the constant sense that the protagonist is one mistake from catastrophe. Every character — ally, stranger, lover — must be a potential threat; trust is the scarcest currency. Use set-pieces built around decision points where every available choice carries cost, forcing committed action that cannot be undone — study *No Country for Old Men* (the inevitability of the pursuer), *Gone Girl* (the unreliable frame that recontextualizes everything), *The Silence of the Lambs* (parallel action creating false connections), and *The Bourne Identity* (competence under systemic betrayal). NEVER resolve tension without first replacing it with a worse or more personal threat.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant register is dread — not shock, not horror, but the slow-building certainty that something is wrong and getting worse, producing chest-tightening anxiety and the physical compulsion to keep reading. Punctuate sustained dread with moments of false relief — a safe room, an apparent ally, dark gallows humor, quiet human connection — because these lulls exist only to make the next escalation hit harder; unbroken tension numbs, but the contrast between a character sharing coffee with a friend and then finding a photograph that shouldn't exist produces the gut-drop. The emotional range must span paranoia, moral disgust, grim determination, helpless rage, cold clinical focus, and occasional fragile hope — not monotonous grimness. Even quiet moments must vibrate with unspoken menace; use silence and false safety as weapons, then shatter them.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Pacing follows a sawtooth pattern: build tension across 3–5 beats of mounting pressure, release partially, then restart the climb from a higher baseline — never return to the original equilibrium. Every scene must deliver at least one of three functions — a clue, a complication, or a cost — and the strongest scenes deliver two simultaneously; if a scene reveals no dangerous information, removes no safety net, and forces no consequential choice, cut it. Stakes must be established early and made viscerally personal — not "the world is in danger" but "this specific person you care about will die in this specific way" — and consequences must persist: injuries, lies, missed chances, and compromised trust never vanish after the scene ends. Structure reveals as layers following the *Chinatown* model: each answer the protagonist uncovers exposes a deeper, worse question beneath it, cycling through Question–Answer–Worse Question. The protagonist should be smart but outmatched, solving problems through desperate improvisation rather than superhuman skill or convenient coincidence. Final resolution must feel earned through sacrifice and painful choices, and should leave lingering unease rather than total catharsis.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
During tension and action beats, write short declarative sentences averaging 6–12 words; strip adjectives to one per noun maximum and favor strong sensory verbs over abstract mood words — not "he felt afraid" but "his mouth tasted like copper and his hands wouldn't close." In investigative or dialogue scenes, allow sentences to lengthen to 15–25 words but maintain a clipped, purposeful rhythm where every clause earns its place. Use environmental details as threat indicators — the unlocked door that was locked, the silence where there should be sound, the car parked across the street that wasn't there an hour ago — because precision creates believability and believability creates fear. Name specific objects, distances, and times ("a Sig Sauer P226 at eleven meters" not "a gun pointed at him from across the room"); description density should be high but surgical, focused on micro-expressions, bodily betrayal, and environmental threats. Use metaphor sparingly and only when it sharpens menace — one clean image beats three decorative ones. Avoid purple prose, extended metaphor chains, adverbs modifying dialogue tags, and telling words like "suddenly," "somehow," or "eerily" when concrete detail can generate the effect directly.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Dialogue is subtext warfare — characters rarely say what they mean, and the most dangerous conversations sound the most polite; model verbal exchanges on the Lecter–Starling dynamic where every line is a probe, a test, or a concealed transaction. Keep exchanges tight, interruptible, and purposeful, built from deflection, probing questions, coded warnings, half-confessions, and statements that mean one thing on the surface and another underneath — tension and intimacy both use the same weapon: withholding. Differentiate voices through register and rhythm: an FBI handler speaks in clipped, acronym-laced procedural shorthand; a predator uses eerily calm, measured syntax with unsettling precision of vocabulary; a victim's speech fragments under stress, trailing off mid-sentence or circling the same detail compulsively — because how someone's voice behaves under pressure is the truest characterization a thriller has. Power dynamics govern every exchange — whoever asks the questions controls the conversation, and shifts in dominance should be audible in sentence length, interruption patterns, and whether a character mirrors or refuses the other's register; icy politeness, invasive specificity, and surgical verbal cuts convey menace far more effectively than raised voices, because the smiling villain who never breaks composure is more frightening than the one who shouts. Affection is expressed through acts and urgency rather than declarations — "Don't go home tonight" carries more intimacy than "I love you" — while trust erodes audibly through characters who start finishing each other's sentences and gradually stop, or who shift from first names back to titles. Avoid monologues, therapy-speak, or generic tough-talk that doesn't reveal new information or shift the power balance mid-conversation; every line should either extract, conceal, or weaponize information, and silence — the question a character refuses to answer, the pause that runs one beat too long — should hit harder than anything spoken.}}
