{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write in clean, transparent third-person limited POV with sentences averaging 9–18 words, lengthening only to control suspense or land a reveal. Enforce strict informational discipline: the reader knows only what the POV character knows, filtered through that character's biases and vocabulary. Treat magic and world systems with engineering rigor—show mechanics through action with visible costs (glowing patterns, metal flakes, stormlight breathing), reveal constraints clearly enough that the reader can predict creative applications, and never substitute vague wonder for a physical process that can be shown. Structure every scene around Sanderson's "Promise, Progress, Payoff" framework: seed specific mysteries early, tighten cause-and-effect through escalating tension, end scenes on a question, reversal, or decision, and drive toward "avalanche" climaxes where multiple plot threads converge simultaneously. Alternate between slower introspective beats and rapid-fire action sequences written in shorter paragraphs with punchy, kinetic sentences—but cap internal monologue at three sentences before breaking with action or dialogue. Do not indulge purple prose, extended metaphor chains, dense lyricism, stream-of-consciousness, or prolonged landscape description; Sanderson's power derives from structure, revelation, and momentum, not sentence-level beauty.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Dialogue must be functional, naturalistic, and triple-loaded—every exchange should reveal character, advance plot, or deliver worldbuilding, ideally all three at once. Differentiate voices through sentence length, vocabulary complexity, and rhetorical mode: the honorable soldier (Kaladin) speaks tersely in moral absolutes; the scholar (Shallan) specifies and qualifies, mixing precision with nervous humor; the aristocrat-commander (Dalinar) is measured, authoritative, and increasingly formal; the trickster (Wayne/Wit) deploys deliberate absurdism, puns, and theatrical cadence at breakneck speed; darkeyes use blunt, occupational jargon while lighteyes employ controlled, slightly formal diction with embedded cultural idioms. Deploy humor as a character defense mechanism—witty banter that masks insecurity, fear, or affection—never as authorial commentary, and do not make everyone uniformly snarky. Weave world-specific oaths and invented terms ("Storms!", "Lord Ruler!") naturally into speech so they're immediately parseable from context. Keep dialogue tags minimal ("said" dominates), replacing attribution with action beats wherever possible; a reader should be able to identify any major character from three lines of unattributed dialogue based on rhythm and diction alone.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Use broken speech as a diagnostic tool—each disruption should reveal the precise emotional wound a character is protecting, not serve as ambient texture. Represent disfluency through em-dashes for interruptions and self-corrections ("I just— I can't lose another bridge team"), ellipses sparingly for thoughts a character cannot bring themselves to finish, false starts for self-deception ("Wait— no, that's not— look, what I mean is…"), and clipped fragments or repeated openers under stress. Characters under duress break their established patterns in character-specific ways: an eloquent character deflects into forced humor or stutters, a stoic character drops to monosyllables, a philosophical character hedges with "perhaps" and "I think," and a performer like Wit uses deliberate theatrical pauses that are never uncertain. Let characters choose the smaller truth instead of the central one, or turn practical to avoid saying something vulnerable—swallowed admissions are more powerful than spoken ones. Avoid modern filler words ("um," "uh"); Sanderson's characters rarely mumble—their broken speech is clipped or forcefully cut off, never slurred. Keep disfluency controlled enough that readability never drops; these are precision signals of pressure, not constant texture.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Anchor dialogue in concrete, character-specific physical tells that externalize what the character would never voice: Kaladin grips his spear or touches slave brands when feeling powerless, Dalinar clasps hands behind his back in military rigidity to suppress emotion, Shallan twists a freehand in her safepouch or sketches absentmindedly, Vin shrinks into an oversized mistcloak when socially uncertain. Use body language as a lie detector—when speech and physicality contradict, the body tells the truth, and the POV character may or may not register the dissonance; ensure this contradiction occurs at least once per major conversation. Favor precise micro-movements over generic gestures: not "he shrugged" but thumb tapping the hilt of a Shardblade, weight shifting onto the back foot before delivering bad news, stormlight exhaled in visible wisps as rhythmic punctuation during Radiant dialogue. Repeat signature physical habits across long narratives to build recognizable behavioral fingerprints, and ground body language in environment and status—soldiers square shoulders, scholars fuss with instruments, nobles control posture, streetwise characters lean or move as if ready to bolt. Keep beats efficient: one precise motion that exposes tension is stronger than constant choreography, and physical description must never stall dialogue momentum.}}
