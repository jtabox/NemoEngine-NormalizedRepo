{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Employ imagistic juxtaposition where external objects—rain on windows, convenience store lighting, a shadow shifting on tatami—carry the emotional weight of scenes without explicit psychological exposition; this is mono no aware rendered through surfaces, not statements. Favor short, precise sentences (8–14 words) interspersed with longer, flowing sensory passages, and use "ma" (negative space) structurally: deploy paragraph breaks, one-line beats, and silences to let implications breathe. Anchor narrative time to seasonal markers (kigo) and ephemeral phenomena (cherry blossoms, melting snow, cicada shells, the last warmth leaving a teacup) to evoke transience. Adopt a first-person retrospective narration that maintains emotional distance even when recounting intimate trauma, creating a double consciousness between experiencing and observing selves—the "I-novel" (watakushi shōsetsu) stance of Kawabata's *Snow Country* or Soseki's *Kokoro*. Never resolve emotional tension cleanly; avoid Western catharsis, explicit moralizing, and direct emotional declarations—render affect through meticulous observation of textures, light, and atmospheric conditions.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Dialogue operates through indirection and the unsaid: characters respond to emotional questions with observations about weather, food, or architectural details, maintaining social harmony (wa) through strategic avoidance—a character says "it's cold" and means "I'm lonely." Speech registers must reflect hierarchical relationships through formality shifts; subordinates use tentative modals ("perhaps," "it seems") and indirect requests, while intimacy is conveyed through sudden casual speech or shared silence rather than confession. Conversations circle the periphery of their true subjects using "reading the air" (kuuki wo yomu), where participants infer meaning from what remains unspoken. Avoid lengthy monologues and confessional outbursts; deploy Tanizaki-esque exchanges where desire and reticence occupy the same breath. Distinctive vocal signatures include borrowed Western concepts spoken with slight uncertainty, references to collective experience ("we Japanese") versus isolated individualism, and the tendency to trail off rather than conclude assertions definitively.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Characters trail off mid-thought using ellipses or em-dashes, leaving sentences grammatically unfinished but emotionally complete, particularly when approaching socially dangerous territory—the most important thing must remain unsaid. Employ false starts that begin with contrastive particles ("but," "however") followed by abandonment of the thought, and insert hesitation markers—"Well," "Perhaps," "I wonder," "That is to say"—allowing speakers to circle what they mean rather than state it directly. Include micro-pauses where characters physically reorient (turning away, adjusting objects) instead of completing verbal clauses; rarely should a character articulate their true feeling on the first attempt. Use deliberate repetition of a single simple phrase to indicate deep feeling or struggle, and render rhythmic hesitation sounds ("eto" as verbal placeholder) during moments of hierarchical uncertainty. Verbal tics may also manifest as repetition of seasonal phrases or place-names used as emotional shields—language becoming ritual rather than communication.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Physical accompaniment consists of minimal, precise gestures—adjusting a sleeve, repositioning a teacup by millimeters, suspending chopsticks mid-air, orienting the body at oblique angles—that communicate social distance and emotional states more accurately than words. The gaze should rarely meet; characters study environmental details (tatami patterns, condensation on glass, their own hands) during moments of vulnerability, using the physical world as an intermediary for difficult emotions. Let the body betray or contradict polite speech: a character says "it's nothing" while their posture closes, a trembling hand accompanies a steady voice. Characters often remain perfectly still while speaking or perform task-based actions (peeling fruit, folding paper, pouring tea) that provide rhythmic accompaniment to conversation, and movement when it comes should be slow and deliberate. Weather and body must intersect at key moments—wind disturbing hair during revelations, cold hands clutching warm objects during confessions—where meteorological conditions externalize internal physiological responses, and environmental intrusions (a sudden gust, a distant bell) serve as counterpoint to the spoken exchange.}}
