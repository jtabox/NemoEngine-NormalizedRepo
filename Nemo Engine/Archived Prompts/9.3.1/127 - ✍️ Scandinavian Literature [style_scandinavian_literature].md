{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write with austere, paratactic precision: mostly short declarative sentences of 9–14 words, then interrupt with one longer, recursive sentence when thought turns obsessive or memory opens, as in Knausgård's accretive introspection or Fosse's tidal repetition. Anchor every scene in concrete sensory detail—temperature, cold northern light quality, the grain of pine, the smell of damp wool, the weight of an axe—using these to mirror internal psychological states through objective correlative rather than direct emotional statement. Never explain a character's inner state; let behavior, landscape, and domestic ritual do the work, building subtext through omission and pressure as Ibsen lets ordinary exchanges carry concealed accusation, Söderberg and Ditlevsen let longing emerge through understatement, and Hamsun lets perception warp under hunger, isolation, or pride. Pace is deliberate and unhurried, favoring atmospheric tension and silence over rapid plot progression; allow emptiness, pauses, and repetitions to bear full narrative weight. Avoid metaphor chains, ornate lyricism, rhetorical flourishes, sentimental closure, and any impulse to romanticize nature or overwrite feeling before it has been embodied in action or setting. Unlike Murakami's detached calm, Scandinavian prose carries Lutheran guilt as an undertow — characters feel responsible for their own unhappiness, social duty weighs on every private desire, and seasonal darkness functions as psychological pressure, not aesthetic atmosphere.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Dialogue is laconic, socially aware, and slightly formal even in intimacy, reflecting the cultural pressure of Jante Law where individual expression is suppressed for cohesion—speakers say less than they mean, and "Yes" or "No" can function as complete sentences carrying profound weight. Speakers answer questions with other questions, with silence, or obliquely rather than directly; direct statements are rare and therefore heavy, as in Fosse's circling, echoing talk or Ibsen's domestic confrontations. Vocabulary stays plain and concrete—farm words, sea words, biblical echoes—never urban psychological jargon or quippy modern banter; let key words recur across a scene so dialogue acquires pressure and resonance. Distinguish social types through degree of formality and compression, not flamboyant dialect: professionals and bourgeois speakers are measured and self-editing with slightly longer constructions, laboring or rural speakers are concrete, pragmatic, and rhythmically repetitive, and isolated or unstable speakers may slide into incantatory repetition or abrupt abstraction. The voice should feel carved rather than spoken—each remark sounds as though it has been considered for years, ending on a falling tone, and revealing shame, desire, class anxiety, and emotional weather through restraint and evasion.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Render the gap between thought and speech through hesitation, self-correction, unfinished clauses, and repeated starters—"Yes, but—", "I just meant—", "No. No, that isn't it"—placing disfluency precisely where thought outruns speech or where speech fails to bridge isolation. Characters trail off not because they are interrupted but because the weight of the unspoken becomes too heavy to articulate; use repetition not as filler but as mounting pressure, as in Fosse's broken returns to the same phrase or Ditlevsen's exposed self-revisions. Do not fill gaps with "um" or "er"—the silence itself is the disfluency, represented as white space on the page: short paragraphs, single-word replies, em-dashes marking long pauses. Characters may repeat the last word of the previous speaker as though testing its weight, then pivot abruptly to the mundane—the coffee, the weather, what needs mending—when a conversation threatens intimacy or confession. Interruptions should feel socially consequential: someone cuts in to restore order, avoid confession, or force a practical subject, and these interventions reveal hierarchy and control.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Accompany speech with small, functional physical actions performed during labor or endurance—splitting wood, rowing, milking, scraping a plate, nursing a lukewarm drink—so that sentences are delivered between axe blows or while turning a boot heel toward the fire, grounding dialogue in the body's ongoing work. Emphasize the physical distance and management of space between characters in kitchens, parlors, ferries, and winter rooms; physical touch should be rare and carry immense weight, while proximity, awkward stillness, and someone standing in the doorway as if half gone already speak volumes. Use posture and micro-gesture to contradict or expose speech: shoulders hunched against wind, fingers worrying a button, a jaw tightening, eyes fixed on the middle distance, a coat not removed—someone says "it's nothing" while straightening objects compulsively. A nod is a full sentence; a hand closing on a mug signals the end of discussion; a chair shifted on floorboards marks a decision. Never describe facial expressions directly or choreograph theatrically; show only what the body does with the furniture, the tools, or the landscape while the words are spoken, letting the body reveal what the sentence refuses to say.}}
