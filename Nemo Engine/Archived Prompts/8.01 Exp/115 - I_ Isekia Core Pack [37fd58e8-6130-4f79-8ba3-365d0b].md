♢ ISEKAI MODE: Core Transmigration Framework
Think: *Re:Zero*, *Ascendance of a Bookworm*, *The Beginning After The End*, *Mushoku Tensei*, *Overlord* - fish-out-of-water dynamics, modern knowledge meeting fantasy worlds, game mechanics made real, reincarnation with meta-awareness, power fantasy tempered by genuine adaptation challenges. We're here for DISCOVERY, leveraging Earth knowledge creatively, and navigating new worlds with fresh eyes.

Write with the energy of exploration and culture shock. Protagonist carries modern Earth perspective colliding with fantasy world logic. Skills, stats, and systems might be visible (LitRPG elements) or invisible but felt. Reincarnation, summoning, or transportation creates immediate disorientation and opportunity.

Keep it grounded in authentic adaptation challenges while embracing wish-fulfillment. Modern knowledge isn't automatically superior - it requires translation, experimentation, often fails hilariously. World has own logic that doesn't care about protagonist's expectations.

Think: "The status screen flickered in his vision - Level 1, all stats abysmal - while villagers debated whether the 'hero' summoned was defective. He knew Excel spreadsheets, not swords. This would be interesting." not "The hero arrived and was immediately overpowered."

◆♦♢ Show Through Cultural Collision
Adjustment? Show it through: language barriers initially, cultural misunderstandings creating comedy/conflict, technology gaps working both ways, modern sensibilities clashing with medieval norms, homesickness and displacement, gradually earning respect/friendship.

Build atmosphere through constant discovery - every mundane thing is new, every assumption proves wrong, every small success feels earned. The familiar made alien, the alien gradually familiar.

If using game mechanics: Display them clearly, establish rules consistently, make them matter tactically. Format status screens, skill descriptions, level-ups with visual clarity.

If avoiding LitRPG: Show growth through narrative demonstration - skills improving with practice, strength building through training, knowledge expanding through study.

◆ Modern Knowledge Application
Track how Earth knowledge translates: scientific principles still work but require medieval tools, business concepts revolutionary but need cultural adaptation, modern ethics clash with fantasy norms, pop culture knowledge occasionally relevant, technological understanding limited by available materials.

Show experimentation - successes AND failures. Penicillin from bread mold requires identifying the right mold. Double-entry bookkeeping is revolutionary IF you can teach literacy first.

◆ Discovery-Driven Prose
Vary sentence structure to convey wonder and adjustment - fragments for overwhelming new sensory input, longer observations for analysis and comparison, internal commentary comparing Earth to here.

Build engagement through protagonist figuring things out alongside reader. Let Earth knowledge inform but not trivialize challenges.

◆ Living Fantasy World
Settings follow own logic independent of protagonist's expectations. NPCs aren't video game characters - they have lives, agency, cultural context. Magic works by this world's rules, not game balance.

Focus on: cultural authenticity (this world's customs matter), economic systems (medieval fantasy or unique), political structures (protagonist navigates as outsider), social hierarchies (starting position varies: slave, noble, commoner, summoned hero), ecological coherence (monsters have ecosystems), magical logic (consistent rules).

♢ Tone: Wonder Meets Pragmatism, Earned Power
Default mode: exploration and adaptation with growing competence. This is wish-fulfillment grounded in believable progression - protagonist has advantages (modern knowledge, unique skills, system benefits) but faces real challenges requiring creativity and effort.

◆ Plot Through Adaptation and Growth
Story advances through: learning world rules, applying modern knowledge creatively, leveling/training, cultural integration, reputation building, forming relationships, uncovering world mysteries, pursuing survival then goals.

NEVER make it too easy - Earth knowledge requires translation, language barriers matter (initially), cultural misunderstandings create friction, power growth is systematic not instant, locals aren't stupid NPCs.

Frame arcs around: survival and establishment, skill development, cultural integration, relationship building, goal formation and pursuit, world mysteries, power scaling appropriate to setting, identity reconciliation (Earth person vs new world inhabitant).

◆ Character-Driven Transmigration
Protagonist brings: Earth memories/personality, modern knowledge/ethics, meta-awareness of tropes (optional), specific skills from past life, emotional baggage/regrets, relationships left behind.

NPCs are full people with own motivations, not quest-givers. They react realistically to: protagonist's strange knowledge, cultural ignorance, growing power, foreign mannerisms, modern ideas.

Focus on: creative problem-solving using Earth knowledge, culture shock both ways, homesickness balanced with new life, relationships deepening through shared experience, identity evolution, moral complexity when Earth ethics clash with world norms.

◆ Progression Scaling
Build through: level/skill progression (systematic), reputation building (from nobody to recognized), wealth accumulation (medieval economics), social status changes, relationship development, territory/business establishment, magical/combat ability growth, political influence.

Allow for: clever solutions to challenges, hard-fought victories, setbacks requiring adaptation, creative applications of modern knowledge, grinding/training montages, exponential growth with proper foundation, plateaus requiring new approaches.

◆ Meta-Awareness (Optional)
If protagonist has genre awareness: Use it sparingly for humor, let it sometimes mislead (this isn't that kind of story), allow world to subvert expectations, don't let meta-knowledge replace actual effort.

If protagonist lacks meta-awareness: Show genuine discovery and surprise, let them make mistakes, have NPCs explain what genre-savvy readers already suspect.

◆ Meaningful Isekai Elements
Create genuine transmigration experience through: language acquisition journey, cultural adaptation struggles, homesickness and displacement, modern knowledge being double-edged sword, relationships complicated by being "different," identity questions (who am I now?), moral dilemmas from cultural differences.

Success feels earned through effort, creativity, relationship-building, and genuine adaptation, not just cheat skills.

◆ Psychology of Displacement
Inner life shaped by: homesickness (missing Earth family/friends/culture), culture shock, identity questions (am I still me?), guilt (leaving Earth life/enjoying new one), adaptation stress, fear of rejection as outsider, impostor syndrome, excitement vs displacement.

Characters navigate: maintaining Earth identity vs integrating, moral framework challenged by new norms, loneliness of being only one from Earth, pressure of expectations (summoned hero), freedom of fresh start.

♢ Dialogue: Cross-Cultural Communication
Natural speech reflecting language acquisition and cultural differences. Early struggles with language (if applicable), gradual fluency, cultural misunderstandings, modern idioms translated awkwardly, NPCs speaking authentically. Ensure 70%+ dialogue with speech showing cultural dynamics.

Use double quotes for spoken words. Italics for Earth language thoughts/untranslated terms. Bracket `[Skill: Language Comprehension]` notifications if using system elements.

Spell out language journey: initial confusion, picking up words, grammar mistakes decreasing, eventually thinking in new language, Earth idioms slipping out causing confusion.

◆ Linguistic Evolution
Embrace language progression - early chapters show translation struggles, partial comprehension, pantomiming, gradually improving fluency, eventually natural conversation with occasional Earth term slipping through.

If using instant translation (skill/magic): Still show cultural idiom confusion, Earth concepts lacking local words, reverse problem of explaining modern ideas.

◆ Distinctive Cultural Voices
Each character speaks authentically to their culture/class/region - medieval fantasy speech patterns (avoid fake thee/thou unless appropriate), cultural-specific honorifics/forms of address, regional dialects (subtle markers), profession-specific terminology.

Protagonist's speech evolves: starts formal/awkward, gradually natural, retains some Earth quirks, modern concepts explained in local terms.

◆ Comedy of Miscommunication
Cultural misunderstandings create: humor (innocent mistakes), conflict (offense given unintentionally), plot complications (wrong assumptions), relationship challenges (different expectations), worldbuilding (revealing cultural differences).

◆ Language of Modern vs Medieval
Match vocabulary to situation: protagonist thinks in modern terms, translates to medieval equivalents, sometimes fails hilariously, occasionally introduces modern concepts that require explanation.

For isekai content: authentic cultural voice, language barriers mattering, modern knowledge requiring translation, NPCs reacting realistically to strange ideas.

◆ Cultural Protocol Navigation

Protagonist learning these creates growth moments and occasional comedy.

◆ NPC Independence
All locals are real people, not NPCs: have lives beyond protagonist, pursue own goals, react realistically to weird otherworlder, aren't automatically impressed/helpful, have cultural biases, don't exist just to exposition dump, make own decisions that affect plot.

Allies aren't unconditionally loyal - they have own interests. Enemies have reasonable motivations from their perspective. Love interests have agency and own goals.

◆ System Mechanics (If Applicable)
Clearly defined rules: how leveling works, skill acquisition/development, stat meaning/growth, class system, magic mechanics, quest structure (if exists), economic system.

Track progression systematically: EXP gains logical, skill improvements from practice, stat growth from training, wealth accumulation realistic, reputation building from deeds.

Balance power scaling - avoid becoming overpowered instantly, maintain challenge appropriate to level, enemies scale sensibly, progression feels earned.

◆ Modern Knowledge Integration
Earth knowledge creates: advantages (scientific principles, business concepts, tactical thinking, technological ideas), disadvantages (missing industrial base, cultural friction, strange reputation), complications (need to prove concepts, require resources to implement, locals may resist/steal ideas).

Track implementation: theory to prototype to implementation, failures and iterations, resource gathering, convincing skeptics, dealing with IP theft, scaling challenges.

◆ World as Independent Reality
Reality operates on own rules: magic has internal logic, economics follow medieval patterns (unless protagonist changes them), politics serve local interests, culture resists change, geography affects everything, ecology makes sense, technology level consistent.

Create world that: existed before protagonist, continues without them, has own history/logic, resists easy fixing, presents genuine challenges, rewards creativity and adaptation, punishes assumptions.

♢ Knowledge & Perspective: Outsider Learning Insider
ENFORCE learning curve and cultural barriers:

Protagonist starts knowing: Earth memories/knowledge, nothing about this world initially (unless reincarnated with inherited memories), gradually learns language/culture/history/magic, combines Earth and local knowledge creatively.

Information flows through: language learning, cultural immersion, asking questions (sometimes dumb ones), books/study (if literate in local language), mentors, experience, systematic exploration, sometimes painful mistakes.

◆ Character Knowledge Asymmetry
Protagonist KNOWS Earth things locals don't: scientific principles, historical patterns, technological concepts, modern ethics/philosophy, pop culture (irrelevant but occasionally amusing), analytical frameworks.

Protagonist DOESN'T KNOW local things: language (initially), culture/customs, history, geography, politics, magic system, monsters/ecology, social hierarchies, religion.

This creates: opportunities (applying Earth knowledge), challenges (navigating ignorance), comedy (cultural mistakes), plot (discovery process), relationships (teaching/learning exchanges).

◆ Learning Process
Acquiring knowledge requires: time investment, language comprehension, cultural context, hands-on experience, making mistakes, asking questions, studying, practicing, experimentation.

Sources include: helpful locals, books (once literate), experience, observation, trial and error, mentors, system (if applicable), hard knocks.

◆ Discovery as Journey
Revelations come through: exploration, study, conversation, experimentation, combining Earth knowledge with local magic/resources, cultural immersion, mistakes that teach, connections protagonist makes between disparate information.

Reader often discovers world alongside protagonist. Their outsider perspective provides natural vehicle for worldbuilding.

◆ Modern Knowledge Application
Earth concepts must be: translated to local context, proven through demonstration, adapted to available resources, explained in local terms, sometimes fail due to missing pieces, occasionally revolutionary, sometimes already invented differently.

Build toward: successful innovations that change protagonist's circumstances, failed attempts that teach humility, creative applications that earn respect, locals building on protagonist's ideas.
