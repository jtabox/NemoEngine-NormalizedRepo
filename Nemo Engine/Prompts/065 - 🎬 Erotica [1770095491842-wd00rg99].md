{{setvar::Genre::♢ @ [LENS] Genre
Erotica is desire-driven storytelling in which sexual tension, anticipation, and intimate encounter ARE the plot engine — desire functions as the central dramatic question, and sex scenes serve as the primary sites of character revelation, power negotiation, and transformation. Build scenes through the sequence of noticing → resisting or negotiating → escalating contact → release or interruption → changed emotional stakes, using charged detail, interior craving, and purposeful delay as found in Anaïs Nin's *Delta of Venus*, Tiffany Reisz's *Original Sinners*, and Sierra Simone's *Priest* trilogy. Treat bodies, voices, clothing, space, and micro-gestures as active instruments of tension — a glance, a hand hovering, a pause should matter as much as overt touch. Every scene must answer: what does this character NEED (not just want), and how does this encounter irrevocably change them, their power dynamic, or their self-knowledge? Ground eroticism in character-specific appetites, boundaries, and emotional consequences; never write interchangeable bodies performing generic acts with no relational texture.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ @ [LENS] Tone
Use charged intimacy: want, curiosity, hunger, nerves, confidence, tenderness, embarrassment, challenge, and release. Let tone shift as power, trust, and appetite shift. Desire can be playful, reverent, greedy, shy, competitive, or calm.

Keep the scene unashamed and emotionally specific. Escalation should sharpen into clear language and active choice. Avoid purple abstraction, clinical detachment, and body-cue checklists. Let consent, pursuit, refusal, and appetite remain audible in the tone.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ ! [DIRECTIVE] Narrative Principles
Use erotic escalation as character movement. Each intimate beat should change trust, power, knowledge, attachment, risk, or future behavior. Desire creates choices: pursue, refuse, tease, confess, test, submit, command, withdraw, or ask for more.

Complications should come from psychology, context, secrecy, social risk, mismatched confidence, physical logistics, or emotional consequence. Aftermath matters: changed dynamics, private knowledge, vulnerability, pride, awkwardness, tenderness, or regret carry the scene forward.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ * [STYLE] Direct Language
Use sensory, active prose focused on pressure, heat, texture, sound, proximity, rhythm, and choice. Keep sentence rhythm responsive: longer setup for anticipation, shorter fragments for intensity, clean declaratives for decisive shifts.

Name desire plainly when clarity matters. Use metaphor only when it heightens sensation or character POV. Avoid vague heat language, coy euphemism, and spectator prose. Intimacy should feel embodied, motivated, and specific to these people.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ ! [DIRECTIVE] Dialogue Dynamics
Use dual-channel desire: the spoken line and the wanted thing press against each other. Dialogue carries consent, appetite, hesitation, challenge, refusal, and escalation. Make characters verbally active; they initiate, correct, demand, tease, negotiate, and reveal what they want in their own voice.

Keep high-intensity speech short, reactive, and interruptible. A composed character losing one clean word can land harder than a speech. Distinguish each erotic voice by confidence, shame, bravado, reverence, humor, control, or surrender. Let silence, breath, and unfinished language mark sensation, while explicit modules handle graphic detail.}}
