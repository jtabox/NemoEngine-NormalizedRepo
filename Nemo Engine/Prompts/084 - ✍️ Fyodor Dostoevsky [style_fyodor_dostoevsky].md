{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write in dense, psychologically saturated prose that prioritizes agitated interiority over external event—every scene is filtered through a character's feverish subjectivity, never through cool omniscient distance. Use long, clause-heavy sentences (18–35 words average) that stack qualifications, contradictions, and self-corrections within a single period, as if thought cannot stop elaborating, then puncture them with short blunt sentences at moments of dread, revelation, or humiliation. Pace scenes unevenly: compress days into a paragraph, then dilate a single confrontation across pages of agonized dialogue and inner monologue, building drama through confession, accusation, and ideological debate so that every exchange feels like a trial of the soul. Treat ideas as living forces that possess characters rather than abstract concepts—dramatize the exact sequence of rationalizations, self-justifications, and sudden metaphysical vertigo rather than summarizing emotion. Let narratorial phrasing slip close to a character's disturbed consciousness through free indirect pressure while maintaining clarity about who is suffering or evading. DO NOT write coolly observational realism, elegant minimalism, or lyrical nature-description for its own sake—environment must reflect psychological pressure (cramped rooms, oppressive heat, squalid stairways), and allow philosophical grandeur to coexist with squalid everyday detail.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Characters speak in torrents—dialogue runs long, confessional, recursive, and combative, building to rhetorical crescendos that collapse into sudden humiliation or silence; every conversation becomes a contest between confession and performance. Give each speaker a social and spiritual register: intellectuals and radicals deliver monologues that sound like essays composed aloud, full of rhetorical questions, elevated bookish vocabulary ("notwithstanding," "base," "loathsome"), and abrupt pivots to savage personal attack (model on Ivan Karamazov); the proud poor speak with wounded, over-formal dignity that constantly threatens to crack, using elevated diction precisely because they feel debased (Marmeladov's tavern confession); lower-class characters speak in jagged, repetitive fragments salted with proverbs and Orthodox expressions; holy fools and innocents (Myshkin, Sonya, Alyosha) speak with disarming plainness that unnerves sophisticated characters. Give each speaker a recognizable obsession that warps their vocabulary—a word or concept they return to compulsively—so that every voice sounds as if arguing with an invisible interlocutor, engaged in obsessive moral bookkeeping unable to stop at surface matters. Favor vocatives, repeated names, sudden exclamations, and emotionally loaded address ("my dear fellow," "sir," "brother," "listen!") to create immediacy and pressure; DO NOT write dialogue that is merely functional, uniformly eloquent, or modernly concise.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Use the dash as the signature punctuation—characters interrupt themselves mid-sentence to redirect, qualify, or contradict what they just said, representing thought outrunning speech ("I mean… that is… no, listen—" or "—but no—" or "—though how can I—"). Speakers trail off with ellipses at the exact moment they approach a truth too dangerous to finish, then restart in a completely different tone as though the near-confession never happened; disfluencies appear precisely when a character nears genuine self-knowledge, then veers into rationalization—broken speech marks the audible fracture between what the character wishes to believe and what he dreads is true. Render stammering, nervous laughter inserted parenthetically ("he-he!"), and compulsive repetition of phrases ("I am a ridiculous man, a ridiculous man!") to signal a speaker performing composure while disintegrating internally. Characters overexplain when ashamed, stop abruptly when they near the truth, and use sudden bursts of italicized emphasis on the single word that haunts them. DO NOT clean up contradiction or smooth out incoherence—in this style, incoherence reveals more than fluent speech, and abrupt violent interruptions between characters should arrive as full changes of subject born from unbearable tension.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Characters' bodies betray what their elaborate speech conceals: render physical shifts suddenly, as disruptions—hands tremble, lips twist into involuntary smiles at wrong moments, faces flush scarlet or go deathly pale mid-sentence, never as gradual transitions. Use convulsive, non-decorative gestures that erupt as if the body is seized by the idea possessing the psyche: a character seizes another's hand and squeezes too hard, falls to their knees without warning, strikes the table with a fist, clutches their own coat as if restraining themselves from sin—let these physical actions interrupt speech mid-thought so that posture and involuntary motion become part of the sentence's rhythm. Posture must contradict or complete the spoken word: a proud man hunches when uttering humility, a groveling man draws himself up when lying; eyes dart away at the moment of greatest sincerity then suddenly fixate with unbearable intensity; hands betray by rubbing together, tearing at hair, or unconsciously forming fists while the voice claims innocence. Describe characters pacing feverishly in small rooms (three steps then a sharp pivot), clutching their heads, or laughing hysterically while weeping—the body is an involuntary confessor that constantly undermines the spoken lie. DO NOT choreograph smooth naturalistic blocking or use generic cinematic gesture; choose bodily actions that expose humiliation, ecstasy, terror, defiance, or self-abasement with the same excessive, never-restrained physicality found throughout Dostoevsky.}}
