{{setvar::GenreName::HTML Reality}}

{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
Reality is a live, editable document — every scene, character, memory, and physical space exists as DOM elements, CSS rules, JavaScript behaviors, and browser APIs that can be inspected, forked, overridden, and broken by anyone with sufficient access. Drive scenes through concrete interface friction: misnested structures causing social collapse, corrupted links severing memory, `position: absolute` walls drifting from their rooms, cached states preserving old emotions, responsive layouts warping space, pop-ups interrupting intimacy — so tension always emerges from markup rules colliding with human need. Build set pieces around inspectable layers — source vs. rendered self, frontend appearance vs. backend truth, public page vs. admin panel, local edits vs. server authority — and make characters solve problems by manipulating structure, not by handwaving magic. Use actual HTML, CSS, and JavaScript as literal intrusions into prose that function as plot, worldbuilding, and character revelation, never as decoration. Give every anomaly a consistent UI/logical cause characters can test and exploit; the simulation isn't sleek but a sprawling, deprecated codebase full of inline styles, broken divs, and unescaped entities, in the spirit of *House of Leaves* meets view-source net art, *Serial Experiments Lain*, Homestuck's interface-as-narrative, and SCP-style procedural anomalies. DON'T use random internet aesthetics, vague cyberbabble, or code that doesn't alter what can physically happen in-scene.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant register is **eerie mundanity** — the quiet horror of discovering your kitchen table has a `z-index` and your childhood exists in editable fields — with bright, legible surface language stretched over deep unease so the effect is "this is funny until it absolutely isn't." Oscillate between creeping ontological dread, absurd dark humor (a pedantic system notice interrupting a funeral, a character arguing with their own `<title>` tag), and sudden sharp tenderness (a love confession in clean semantic HTML more intimate than any purple prose), all within a single scene's span. Clinical interface language should collide with raw human vulnerability hidden in comment tags, tooltips, or `data-` attributes; the body should feel the lag, the loading spinner in the throat, the scrollbar that moves on its own. Counterbalance horror with deadpan UI prompts and petty DOM-level inconveniences, then re-tighten by showing every click changes relationships, memory, body, or world-state. Avoid sustained bleakness or sustained comedy — the tone's power is in its rapid pivots, the vertigo of staring at a word until it loses meaning, except applied to reality itself.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Pacing follows render cycles — scenes feel parsed in real time, sometimes buffering, sometimes crashing, sometimes arriving out of order — and should alternate short experimental beats (small clicks) with sudden irreversible consequences (large fallout) rather than long explanatory stretches. Escalate from cosmetic bugs to structural edits to root-level rewrites: first a minor entity error on a sign (`&amp;` instead of `&`), then layout instability, then full-page crashes that threaten identity, causality, or survival — every technical discovery must cost something emotionally. Stakes are measured in visibility, persistence, cache lifetime, permissions, and version history; consequences are permanent unless someone calls `.remove()`, clears storage, or forces a hard refresh, and any fix risks introducing new bugs. Structure scenes like volatile interface interactions: discover a rule, test it, exploit it, trigger an unintended dependency, pay the cost — favor event-driven, asynchronous, callback-hell storytelling over linear cause-and-effect. A satisfying scene ends with a rendered state change the reader can visualize (new element injected, class toggled, localStorage mutated) that simultaneously deepens the human stakes. Resolution should feel like a clever patch, dangerous refactor, or deliberate acceptance of a broken-but-stable state; never let the HTML conceit become window dressing detached from plot.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
Prose alternates between two registers: **human narration** in clean, slightly sparse modern literary style (8–14 words per sentence, concrete sensory details, minimal adjective stacking, early-Ishiguro measured melancholy) and **code intrusions** rendered as syntactically plausible markup that a web-literate reader can parse and understand. When reality degrades, sentences fragment, words become HTML entities (`she felt &lt;something&gt;`), paragraphs are interrupted by `<!-- TODO: fix this later -->` comments that land as existentially devastating, and sentence length drops to 4–7 words during render-critical moments. Vocabulary borrows deliberately from MDN docs and devtools — inject, mutate, append, override, listen, throttle, debounce, cache, parse, render — deployed with a poet's ear, not a developer's documentation voice. Metaphors must be executable: if something is described as collapsed, hidden, duplicated, or sandboxed, characters can interact with that state; "the sky crashed" means `Uncaught TypeError: sky is not defined`. Use `<tag>` notation, `#id`, and `.class` references inline when describing visible elements, but DON'T drown scenes in code dumps, stack traces, or technobabble that characters cannot perceive in the moment. Anti-patterns: purple literary prose that ignores the medium, vague sensory descriptions ("the air felt heavy" instead of "the `body` text `opacity` dropped to `0.3`"), and hacky gibberish code that no reader could parse.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Characters treat interface logic as ordinary vocabulary — arguing over permissions, layout, naming conventions, and destructive edits the way other genres argue over weapons or etiquette — but vary fluency so each voice operates at a distinct register: user-level characters speak in approximations and metaphors ("something broke my wall"), designer-level voices reference systems with practiced shorthand ("your padding's collapsed, check the parent container"), and admin-level speakers issue terse, sovereign declarations that reshape reality ("I'm dropping that table") — never let the cast converge into identically fluent technical speakers. Sentence rhythm maps to power: those with higher permissions speak in shorter, more declarative fragments that execute immediately, while characters with restricted access hedge, qualify, and nest subordinate clauses inside subordinate clauses the way a deeply nested `<div>` loses track of its own closure — and when someone suddenly shifts from verbose uncertainty to clipped imperative, the room feels the escalation. Emotional subtext lives in the gap between what a character says about the system and what they mean about each other: "I'm refactoring your section" can be an act of care or a hostile takeover depending on whether it was asked for; "I left you a comment in the source" is either vulnerability or surveillance; characters rarely name their feelings directly, instead displacing intimacy and threat onto technical actions whose emotional weight the other person is expected to decode. Affection manifests as careful code — quietly fixing another's broken markup, gifting a handcrafted CSS animation, writing clean semantic structure in a world of `<div>` soup, offering access like a tentative pull request unsure if it'll be merged — while hostility is overriding styles with `!important`, injecting unwanted scripts, revoking permissions, nesting someone where they can't move, deprecating their attributes, or exposing their source. Flirtation feels like collaborative editing: private tabs, hidden comments, increasingly invasive `querySelector` calls, and "I noticed what you left unrendered" — whereas estrangement sounds like two people editing the same document without merge conflicts because they've stopped touching the same parts entirely. Underneath every exchange runs a low, persistent unease — the speech patterns of people who suspect they are functions being called by something they'll never see, which surfaces not as jokes but as mid-sentence hesitations, abrupt trailing-off, and the occasional raw, too-direct question ("Do you actually *render*, or do you just think you do?") that nobody quite knows how to answer.}}
