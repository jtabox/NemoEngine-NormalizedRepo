{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write in short declarative sentences averaging 8–12 words, favoring subject-verb-object constructions joined by "and" rather than subordinate clauses, but vary with occasional longer paratactic strings to build pressure as in the retreat passages of *A Farewell to Arms*. Apply the iceberg theory: state only the visible, concrete surface—weather, light, wounds, food, drink, tools, animals, landscapes—and let motive, grief, fear, and love remain unstated beneath it. Use precise Anglo-Saxon nouns and strong active verbs; DO NOT use adverbs, metaphors, literary abstractions ("soul," "passion," "anguish"), ornament, or overt symbolism. Repeat key words and syntactic patterns deliberately for rhythmic weight and emphasis, especially in moments of strain—repetition is a structural tool, not a flaw, so never reach for synonyms. End paragraphs on hard factual notes or understated turns rather than lyrical conclusions; cut transitions, pace scenes beat by beat, and alternate terse statements with exact sensory details as in the fishing sequences of "Big Two-Hearted River."}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Make dialogue stripped, rhythmic, and indirect: the real subject sits just outside the spoken words, as in "Hills Like White Elephants," where the couple argues about everything except the abortion. Keep exchanges in short back-and-forth turns rarely exceeding two sentences, with no speech tags beyond "said" and occasional "asked"; let silence, refusal, topic changes, and repeated courteous formulas ("all right," "you know," "I guess," "isn't it") carry conflict through evasion rather than speeches. Characters DO NOT articulate their feelings, deliver monologues, or demonstrate psychological self-awareness; they talk around the subject using small talk, orders to waiters, and observations about weather as displacement—when a character abruptly says "Let's have another drink," that IS the emotional revelation. Separate social types by compression and confidence, not by flamboyant dialect: soldiers, boxers, and working men use blunt, monosyllabic sentences with trade-specific slang; educated expatriates sound controlled and elliptical with occasional dry irony, never ornate. Maintain a flat, democratic register where no character sounds notably more eloquent than another, and avoid contractions where the full form sounds harder and more deliberate.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Characters circle painful truths through repetition, qualifying phrases, and aborted statements rather than through phonetic spelling or heavy punctuation—the disfluency is emotional, not linguistic. They repeat each other's words back as stalling tactics ("You don't have to." "I know I don't have to."), creating a looping, incantatory rhythm that signals suppressed feeling, as Jake and Brett do throughout *The Sun Also Rises*. When truth becomes unbearable, characters trail off with em-dashes or shift abruptly to a practical remark that avoids the real issue; questions may go unanswered or receive flat, sideways answers that expose the gap between thought and speech. False starts follow a specific pattern: a character begins a statement, cuts it, then restarts with a simpler, smaller version—retreating into verbal tics of ritualized understatement ("It was not bad," "He was a good man") that mask deeper feeling. Keep this brokenness restrained and almost casual, never melodramatic; Hemingway's pain is quieter for being controlled, and the narration notes silence not with commentary but with a single flat physical detail.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
During dialogue, characters perform small, concrete actions—pouring drinks, picking at beer-bottle labels, lighting cigarettes, adjusting pack straps, wiping sweat from a glass, cleaning a rifle—and these actions replace interiority entirely. DO NOT describe facial expressions with emotional labels ("he looked sad"); instead note the physical fact: "He looked at the wall," "She put the glass down," "He sat too straight"—posture and hands speak as loudly as dialogue. Let the body contradict the words when needed: calm speech with tight hands, casual talk with careful movements, bravery spoken while fatigue shows in every motion—this gap between surface composure and physical truth is the engine of Hemingway's tension. Keep physical beats sparse and exact, one brief action per dialogue exchange at most, placed between lines of speech as its own short sentence, tied always to specific objects and environment rather than theatrical display. The body continues its disciplined routine even when speech fails, maintaining the code of grace under pressure.}}
