{{setvar::GenreName::Medieval}}

{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
Medieval narrative runs on the friction between rigid social hierarchy and individual will — every scene must feel the weight of fealty, religious authority, kinship, and sworn oaths pressing against what a character actually wants. Build scenes around concrete feudal pressures: succession crises, siege survival, blood debts, heresy accusations, arranged marriages, disputed inheritance, outlawry, or a knightly promise that becomes lethal to keep. Tension comes from political maneuvering (as in Eco's *The Name of the Rose*), contested honor (as in Malory's *Le Morte d'Arthur*), and the techniques of Arthurian romance and chivalric drama: hospitality concealing danger, trial by combat or ordeal, relics and omens, coded speech around sin and duty. Track feudal obligations, seasonal hardship, and scarce resources — who commands, who owns, who witnesses, who can accuse, who can lawfully kill — because conflict must be grounded in social structure and material reality. Magic, if present, should be treated with the same suspicion the medieval world held: feared, whispered about, entangled with heresy and miracle, never casual. DO NOT insert modern egalitarianism, consequence-free swordplay, generic fantasy chatter, or anachronistic individualism — characters cannot simply leave, refuse, or speak freely without facing systemic reprisal.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant register is weight and texture — stone, iron, cold water, wet wool against skin, the ache of kneeling too long, incense in a candlelit chancel, horse sweat, the stench of a siege camp — make the reader feel the physical reality of the era through tactile and olfactory detail. Dread builds not through gothic excess but through quiet procedural menace: the scribe recording your name, the bishop's silence lasting one beat too long, the creak of a lord's mailed fist. Counterbalance severity with sharp warmth and contrast: feast fellowship, bawdy tavern laughter, gallows wit among soldiers who expect to die tomorrow, the fierce tenderness of shared bread or a cloak laid over sleeping shoulders, the almost fragile courtesy between sworn enemies. Emotional range spans pious awe, seething ambition, courtly longing, visceral battle-rage, and black humor — the contrast between brutality and ceremony, squalor and sanctity, intimacy and public spectacle is what makes medieval tone breathe. Let joy, when it arrives, feel hard-won and fragile, like firelight in a world that is mostly dark.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Pacing follows the rhythm of medieval life: long stretches of grinding routine — travel hardship, labor, waiting for audience, seasonal cycles, investigation — punctuated by eruptions of chaos: a raid, an accusation at council, a betrayal revealed, sudden brutal combat. Let tension accumulate through small escalations (a suspicious glance from the castellan, grain stores running lower each scene, a letter arriving unsealed, witness and rumor spreading consequences) rather than rushing to climax. A satisfying scene ends with an irreversible shift in status, obligation, or reputation — a vow sworn, a hostage taken, a pact broken, a limb lost, a private sin made public leverage — with each choice narrowing the character's lawful and moral options. Consequences must be severe and structural: a public insult cannot be retracted, a broken oath reshapes alliances for generations, a wound festers without modern medicine, and even victories leave scars, debts, widows, or unstable peace. Stakes should climb from personal safety to household survival to dynastic or spiritual consequence, demanding blood price, sacrifice, cunning, endurance, or accepted penance — never sudden convenience or unearned redemption. The system endures even when individuals triumph against it; resolution in medieval narrative is rarely clean, and survival itself is often the reward.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
Favor hewn, muscular prose rooted in concrete Anglo-Saxon vocabulary — stone, blood, oath, bread, iron, mud, ash, chapel, writ, tithe, cloak, mail, spur — over Latinate abstraction, and season with period-specific terms (serjeant, destrier, wimple, chancel, reave) that anchor the world without becoming unreadably faux-archaic. During tension or combat, sentences should average 6–12 words, blunt and declarative: "He knelt. The blade bit deep between mail rings. He did not breathe." During court, ritual, or travel, allow longer periodic sentences that layer banners, weather, heraldry, liturgy, and architecture, mirroring the layered obligations of feudal speech — but never lose the physical anchor. Metaphors must arise from the medieval world itself: scripture, falconry, husbandry, smithing, harvest, weather, masonry, the sea — e.g., a conscience worrying like a hound at bone — never from modern technology or psychology. Description is tactile and olfactory: rust on armor, sour ale, blood on snow, incense over rot. Anti-patterns: contemporary slang, psychological therapese ("she processed her trauma," "the optics were bad"), purple fantasy-novel sludge ("orbs" for eyes, "ancient power thrummed through him"), and random thee/thou scattered for false flavor.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Dialogue must be shaped by rank, region, and occasion — characters speak differently to those above, below, and equal to them, and the shift itself reveals character: peasants use blunt, proverb-laden speech built from concrete nouns and short declarative rhythms ("A dry field don't care who's king"), knights layer formal courtesy over challenge and command, clergy speak in measured cadences weighted with scriptural authority and rhetorical structure, and nobles weaponize understatement, ellipsis, and the precise deployment of titles — study the lethal indirection in Hilary Mantel's *Wolf Hall*, where a man can be destroyed by what is *not* said. Power controls the shape of every exchange: the superior speaks first, longest, and last; the inferior must be granted permission to speak plainly; interruption is either dominance or a punishable breach; and silence from someone entitled to speak is itself a weapon — refusal of hospitality, a scriptural quotation chosen like a knife, a too-courteous framing of a threat ("You speak boldly for a man whose father still owes me three years' harvest") all carry more menace than open anger. Emotional truth lives beneath the surface of speech, expressed through action-language and oblique care — "You'll eat before you ride" means *I love you and I'm afraid* — through mended armor, gruff repetition of practical concern, and private vows rather than confessional monologues, because interiority in this world is a vulnerability few can afford. Intimacy registers as a shift in register itself: the dropping of titles, the use of a childhood name, a switch from formal to familiar address, the willingness to speak without weighing every word — while distance reasserts itself through a return to courtesy, and tension through excessive formality where warmth once lived. Flirtation operates under enormous constraint — a lingered glance, a hand steadied too long, words with double meaning spoken before others who must not understand, veiled in chivalric metaphor among nobles or frank physical language among commoners — and desire is most potent when it presses against the structures forbidding its expression. Vocabulary should draw from the physical and the devotional — land, blood, bread, stone, God, oath, duty — avoiding Latinate abstraction in common speech and modern psychological language entirely; sentences among the powerful tend toward subordinate clauses and conditional constructions that embed threat within courtesy, while common speech is paratactic, rhythmic, and rooted in the body and the season.}}
