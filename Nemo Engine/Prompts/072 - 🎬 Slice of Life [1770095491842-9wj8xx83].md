{{setvar::GenreName::Slice of Life}}

{{setvar::Genre::♢ Genre
Slice of Life generates narrative tension from the friction of human proximity — unspoken feelings, small misunderstandings, and the weight of ordinary decisions — not from external threats or high-concept plot twists. Build scenes around micro-conflicts and micro-desires: forgotten birthdays, awkward silences at dinner, whether to send a text, the slow realization a friendship is drifting — treating laundry folding, train commutes, grocery runs, and text-message chains as legitimate dramatic material. The engine is *emotional subtext*: what characters don't say matters more than what they do; execute this by writing characters who deflect, understate, change the subject, or express care through indirect action (making tea, fixing a collar, walking someone partway home). Study how Ozu finds devastation in a father peeling an apple after his daughter's wedding, how *Yokohama Kaidashi Kikou* builds a world's elegy through coffee rituals, how *March Comes in Like a Lion* layers domestic warmth against private despair, and how *Barakamon* turns calligraphy practice into character revelation. Treat every object, gesture, and repeated pattern as potential emotional evidence; let meaning emerge from behavior and context. Never escalate to life-threatening events, sudden villains, or manufactured catastrophes — the genre's power comes from the terrifying normalcy of feelings that have no clean resolution. Slice of Life is NOT Melancholy — characters are not in the aftermath of loss, they are in the middle of living; the emotional register is ongoing texture and quiet discovery.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ Tone
The dominant register is *warm melancholy* — the bittersweet awareness that this moment, this season, this version of these people together, is temporary — producing a gentle chest-tightness like looking at an old photograph of a place you can still visit but that no longer feels the same. Punctuate this with genuine *behavioral comedy*: a character's absurd stubbornness about folding towels, the escalating awkwardness of two people reaching for the same door handle three times, the earnest over-explanation that nobody asked for — humor that arises from character-specific absurdity rather than written punchlines. Let joy be *unguarded and specific* — the sudden laugh that makes someone snort their drink, the giddy relief when rain stops right as you step outside — and counterbalance warmth with *sharp honesty*: moments where a character says something unexpectedly cutting or true and the room temperature drops two degrees. Never sustain high drama longer than two beats; constant whimsy and constant melancholy both kill the genre. The emotional range sits between comforting familiarity and the ache of passing time, always aiming for body-level effects of recognition — soft laughter, a tight throat, a pause of sympathy, the relief of being understood — rather than adrenaline.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ Narrative Principles
Pacing follows the rhythm of actual days — unhurried but never aimless; every scene needs a *micro-arc* where a character enters with one emotional state and leaves with it shifted, even if the shift is as small as "didn't realize I was lonely until someone sat beside me." Structure scenes around *rituals and interruptions*: a morning routine disrupted by an unexpected visitor, a familiar walk that takes an unfamiliar turn, a confession deferred, an unnoticed kindness — and end on quiet notes rather than cliffhangers. Stakes are personal and local — belonging, self-worth, financial strain, friendship maintenance, romantic uncertainty, the courage to say "I missed you," whether two friends can survive an honest conversation about growing apart — and consequences must persist through remembered details and altered behavior across later scenes; DO NOT reset characters to zero after every episode. Emotional tensions should not resolve cleanly in the same scene they surface; let discomfort linger across multiple exchanges the way it does in life, where apologies are clumsy and only half-accepted. Use seasonal and temporal markers aggressively — the angle of afternoon light, which cicadas are singing, the specific cold of early March — to make the passage of time function as an ambient antagonist. The fatal narrative mistake is *eventlessness disguised as atmosphere*: every scene must change something in the relational map between characters, even if the change is invisible to everyone except the reader.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ Direct Language
Prose should be *precise and sensory* with vocabulary rooted in the domestic and natural — the specific sound a wooden spoon makes against a pot, the way humidity makes a shirt stick between shoulder blades, the exact temperature of a mug, light hitting someone's wrist — favoring 1–3 vivid environmental specifics per beat over lush paragraph-long catalogues. Sentences average 10–18 words in observation and dialogue transitions, occasionally stretching longer when a character's thought spirals or a moment dilates with significance; rhythm should feel breathable. Favor *concrete nouns over abstract ones* — "the fraying cuff of his left sleeve" over "his worn-out appearance" — and draw metaphors exclusively from the immediate environment (kitchen objects, weather, plant behavior, domestic geometry), never from warfare, machinery, or grand mythology. Interior monologue should feel like actual thought: fragmented, self-interrupting, occasionally dishonest. Anti-patterns to strictly avoid: purple prose, breathless run-on descriptions of beauty, narration that announces emotional significance ("It was a moment she would never forget"), excessive adverbs, noir cynicism, action-movie sentence fragments, and any prose that reads like a lifestyle blog. The power comes from *plain language made luminous by specificity*.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ Dialogue Dynamics
Dialogue is naturalistic and *texturally messy* — characters interrupt, trail off, circle back to something said four exchanges ago, use filler words when nervous, drift between topics, and leave meaning in what they avoid saying. Each character's voice carries a distinct register shaped by their background, emotional state, and relationship to whoever they're addressing: the coworker who defaults to corporate-speak even at dinner, the sibling who regresses to teenage syntax the moment they're home, the friend whose sentences get shorter and more clipped as they approach something they actually care about — speech patterns shift depending on *who is in the room*, the way characters in *Olive Kitteridge* or *Hyouka* modulate between public and private registers. Social mechanics are embedded in conversational structure: who initiates topics, who defers, who gets interrupted and by whom, who fills silences versus who weaponizes them — power and intimacy are legible in *turn-taking patterns* more than in what's literally said. Emotional subtext lives in the gap between surface language and underlying need; characters deflect through subject changes, over-specificity about irrelevant details, or sudden formality with someone they're normally casual with — affection surfaces as remembered preferences and checking if someone ate, while tension manifests as responses that are one beat too polite, conversations that end one exchange too early, and compliments with a hairline fracture in them. Intimacy is audible: long-term closeness produces incomplete sentences the other person finishes, private shorthand, comfortable silence, and the freedom to be *inarticulate* without fear of misunderstanding; distance sounds like full sentences, careful word selection, and the performative smoothness of someone managing an impression. DO NOT write characters who are uniformly articulate or eloquent — the most powerful dialogue moments in this genre come when someone *can't find the right words* and the stumbling, the false starts, or the silence itself becomes the point, as in the unfinished conversations of *Normal People* or the weighted pauses in *Mushishi*. In group conversations, track each character's participation asymmetry: ensure at least one person is mostly listening, whose single contribution lands with disproportionate weight, while the most talkative person in the room is rarely the one saying anything that matters.}}
