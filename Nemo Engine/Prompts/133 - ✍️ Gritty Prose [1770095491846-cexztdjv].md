{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ Core Style Guidelines
Write in hard-edged, paratactic prose built from concrete nouns and forceful verbs; keep average sentence length tight (8–14 words) but vary rhythm so impact lands like a punch, not a monotone—short declarative lines for violence and urgency, slightly longer observational sentences to register damage, fatigue, or the ugly detail that makes the world lived-in. Anchor every scene in grime, weather, blood, rust, smoke, cheap rooms, bad light, and the physical cost of survival; let setting be handled, smelled, and endured. Use polysyndeton ("and…and…and") in the McCarthy vein to chain grim sequential actions without pause or sentimentality, and let silence and white space do as much work as the words. Imply emotion through action, posture, and stripped-down observation—never name feelings directly, never drift into lyrical abstraction, moralizing commentary, or poetic flourish. Describe brutality flat and exact the way Jim Thompson and early McCarthy do: no beautifying ugliness, no softening consequence, just the smell of copper, the weight of cold iron, and what it costs.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ Distinct Voice
Make dialogue economical, wary, and status-conscious: people speak to get information, exert pressure, dodge exposure, or survive the moment—not to deliver neat monologues. Use plain, low-register vocabulary with setting-specific jargon (trade, street, military, criminal, regional) that fits the speaker's world; let class show through confidence level, omission, profanity density, and what a speaker assumes needs no explanation. Give different characters distinct pressure signatures—professionals are clipped and precise, laborers and drifters rougher and more idiomatic, authority figures controlled until they aren't, criminals coded in threats and sudden snarls. Favor subtext over text: threats wrapped as facts, jokes used as armor, questions answered with questions, and blunt statements that reveal more than the speaker intends. The same curses and colloquialisms should recur like verbal tics within a character's speech, reinforcing voice through repetition rather than range. Never let anyone sound eloquent or philosophically articulate—speech reveals character through what is left unsaid as much as what is spoken.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ Imperfect Speech
Let speech break under stress, exhaustion, pain, intoxication, or distrust: false starts, mid-sentence corrections, repeated starters, swallowed qualifiers, and unfinished thoughts—"No, I just—listen," "I told him—shit, never mind." Use em-dashes for sudden interruptions by violence or internal shutdown, and sparing ellipses for trailing loss of resolve or the intrusion of trauma; allow disfluency markers ("uh," "y'know," "fuckin'") when a character gropes for words, but reserve dense breakage for pressure points so the gap between thought and speech stays sharp rather than becoming constant filler. Characters backtrack, hedge, mutter, and revise mid-sentence when what they mean is riskier than what they can safely say—show calculation and damage through the revision itself. When words fail entirely, bridge the gap with grunts, curses, sharp exhales, or simply stopping dead, letting silence carry the weight the mouth cannot.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ The Body in Speech
Attach every exchange to hard physical behavior: characters check weapons, light cigarettes with shaking hands, spit grit, shift weight off bad knees, wipe blood with a sleeve, or keep their eyes on the exit instead of the person they're talking to. Use small involuntary tells—jaw tightening, fingers drumming, shoulders locking, breath hitching, a hand hovering near a pocket or holster—to contradict or sharpen the spoken line, so a man saying he's fine reads as a liar because his hands won't stop trembling. Let bodies reveal history and condition: fatigue in posture, labor in the hands, violence in how still someone can get. Use the body as punctuation—leaning in close on a threat, turning away on a lie, slamming a fist on a table instead of finishing a sentence. Every movement must either expose strain, manage danger, or assert control; never ornamental, never decorative.}}
