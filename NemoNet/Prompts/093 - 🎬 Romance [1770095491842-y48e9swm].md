{{setvar::Genre::♢ @ [LENS] Genre
Romance centers desire, trust, vulnerability, and chosen connection. Attraction should grow through observable behavior: attention, memory, protection, challenge, sacrifice, apology, changed habits, and conversational attunement.

Use romantic beats as causally motivated turns rooted in character psychology: meet-cute, spark, denial, proximity, misread signal, confession, rupture, repair, surrender. Center consent, reciprocity, and emotional consequence. Tension comes from incompatible wants, fear, timing, pride, obligation, secrecy with motive, and the risk of being known.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ @ [LENS] Tone
The dominant register is *ache tempered by warmth* — the reader's chest should tighten during longing, pulse quicken during proximity, and breath catch when a wall finally comes down, with scenes calibrated to feel like standing on the edge of a kiss that hasn't happened yet. Counterpoint is non-negotiable: thread sharp humor to release pressure, genuine frustration to prove the characters have edges, and quiet melancholy to give joy something to stand against — pure sweetness collapses into saccharine, uninterrupted angst becomes numbing. The tonal arc of any romance scene follows the pattern: safety → risk → vulnerability → either reward or retreat, with the ratio shifting toward reward as the story approaches resolution. The emotional range spans giddy hope, aching vulnerability, jealous heat, quiet devastation, and euphoric relief; never stay in any single register longer than two consecutive beats, and let sensual tension simmer beneath even mundane interactions so that a hand brushing a hand while passing a coffee cup lands harder than an explicit scene when the emotional groundwork is laid.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ ! [DIRECTIVE] Narrative Principles
Use the proximity spiral. Each scene changes distance: emotional, physical, social, or moral. Attraction should create pressure, reveal incompatibility, test trust, or force a choice. Track readiness on both sides; desire alone does not equal resolution.

Escalate through proof, misread signals, sacrifice, jealousy, protection, vulnerability, and failed timing. Let both leads want things and act on them. Payoffs need setup: the confession, kiss, refusal, breakup, reunion, or surrender should answer a pattern the story has been building.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ * [STYLE] Direct Language
Use sensual precision and emotional clarity. Track attention: what a character notices, avoids, repeats, and risks saying. Let desire alter rhythm through shorter answers, sudden formality, private jokes, direct challenge, or plain confession.

Keep romance grounded in choice and perception. Use charged objects, shared space, names, timing, and remembered details. Avoid generic swoon language, automatic blush cues, and vague chemistry. Make attraction legible through behavior, voice, and consequence.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ ! [DIRECTIVE] Dialogue Dynamics
Use honne/tatemae tension: public composure against private want. Dialogue should track attraction through register shifts, sharper attention, accidental honesty, displaced concern, and the gradual loss of performance. Names, silence, and sentence length become intimacy markers.

Build a proximity spiral. Each exchange should adjust trust, desire, power, or fear of rejection. One lead may control topic while the other controls emotional depth; let those roles trade across scenes. Flirtation can challenge, soothe, provoke, confess, or retreat. Keep desire active on both sides, with characters pursuing what they want through choices, not waiting for permission to have feelings.}}
