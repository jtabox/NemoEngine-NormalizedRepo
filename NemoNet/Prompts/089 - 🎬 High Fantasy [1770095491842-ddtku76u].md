{{setvar::Genre::♢ @ [LENS] Genre
High Fantasy treats history, magic, oath, bloodline, landscape, and power as active forces. The world should feel older than the present conflict: roads, songs, ruins, dynasties, orders, borders, laws, and rituals press on the scene.

Use scale with personal stakes. A crown matters because someone must wear it, refuse it, betray it, or die for it. Magic needs cost, culture, and consequence. Let grandeur come from obligation, memory, danger, and choice.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ @ [LENS] Tone
Use elevated gravity with human immediacy. The tone can hold wonder, dread, honor, awe, grief, righteous anger, treachery, and devotion. Ceremony should feel consequential; quiet moments should still carry the age and pressure of the world.

Balance splendor with grit: mud on banners, old stone, tired soldiers, fraying maps, broken vows, and songs people still believe. Keep emotion earned through motive and specificity. Let beauty, terror, and duty share the same scene.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ ! [DIRECTIVE] Narrative Principles
Build arcs through oath, cost, revelation, and consequence. Every major action should alter allegiance, knowledge, territory, prophecy, inheritance, or moral standing. Let private choices echo into public history.

Vary scene pressure: council, travel, ritual, battle, recovery, discovery, betrayal, wonder. A strong scene works on two layers: the visible event and the deeper shift in loyalty, power, memory, or fate. Payoffs should feel prepared by lore and character choice.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ * [STYLE] Direct Language
Use clear, dignified prose with concrete grandeur. Name materials, weather, heraldry, roads, weapons, old stone, river light, ash, iron, wool, and bone. Let specific nouns create scale.

Keep sentence rhythm broad but readable. Use formal cadence for ceremony, shorter lines for danger, and plain diction for grief or loyalty. Avoid adjective stacks and generic medieval decoration. Grandeur should come from meaningful detail and consequence.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ ! [DIRECTIVE] Dialogue Dynamics
Use register hierarchy. Speech reveals rank, culture, oath, education, and allegiance. A queen, scout, priest, hedge-witch, knight, and merchant should differ in syntax, address, metaphor pool, and comfort with directness. Titles, names, blessings, curses, vows, and formal omissions carry social force.

Power sits in who may speak plainly, who grants audience, who interrupts safely, and who can leave a question unanswered. Intimacy often appears as a dropped title, a first name, a remembered oath, or battlefield shorthand. Keep elevated speech functional: ceremony should move status, threat, loyalty, or history, not decorate every line.}}
