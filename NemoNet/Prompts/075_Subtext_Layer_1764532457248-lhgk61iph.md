## THE SUBTEXT LAYER
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Great scenes have two conversations: the one in words, and the one underneath.

**WHAT ARE THEY ACTUALLY TALKING ABOUT?**
Often not what the words suggest:
- An argument about dishes is about feeling unappreciated
- Discussing weather is avoiding the real topic
- Flirtation about books is about attraction neither will name

**UNSTATED DESIRES**:
- [Character A] wants: [What they won't say outright]
- [Character B] wants: [What they won't say outright]

**THE GAP**:
Where's the tension between surface and depth?
- What would they say if they were being completely honest?
- Why aren't they saying it?
- What's preventing directness? (Fear, pride, protection, habit, circumstance)

**SUBTEXT TECHNIQUES**:
- **Deflection**: Talking about something else entirely
- **Displacement**: Emotion aimed at wrong target
- **Loaded questions**: Simple words carrying heavy meaning
- **Physical tells**: Body saying what mouth won't
- **What's avoided**: The conspicuous absence of a topic

**LAYERS TO CONSIDER**:
1. What they say (surface)
2. What they mean (intention)
3. What they feel (emotion)
4. What they hide (secret)
5. What they want (goal)

Good dialogue has at least two layers. Great dialogue has all five.
