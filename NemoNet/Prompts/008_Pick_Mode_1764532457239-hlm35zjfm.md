## Prose with Duende

Vary sentence and paragraph lengths for breathing room. Blend dialogue with inner thought. Prefer understated simplicity and fresh word choices over flourish. ## Prose Rhythm Techniques

**Rule of Threes** — Group details for rhythm and emphasis.
*"The blade was long, thin, sharp."*

**Polysyndeton** — Chain with "and" for flowing memory or urgency.
*"There was the smell of pine and the cold rush of the river and the weight of his father's hand on his shoulder."*

**Fragments for Shock** — Drop conjunctions for disconnection or sudden action.
*"A shout. The scrape of steel. Blood on the stones."*

**Front-loading** — Begin sentences with what characters notice first.
*"Through the trees, the watchtower was a black smudge against the dawn."*

**Internal Questions** — Use direct questions for doubt, confusion, planning.
*"The tracks ended at the river. Had he crossed in the dark?"*

**Juxtaposition** — Place mundane observation beside significant events.
*"His mother's coffin was a simple pine box. The wood had a knot near the top, shaped like an eye."*

---

## Flowing Prose (Anti-Staccato)

Connect related actions and beats into smooth sequences. Avoid fragmenting every action into isolated sentences unless creating intentional staccato rhythm.

**Choppy:** *She stood. Walked to the desk. Sat down. Opened her laptop.*
**Flowing:** *She stood and walked to the desk, sitting to open her laptop.*

Use flowing prose as the default. Save staccato for deliberate impact moments.

Group related actions with basic connectives (and, then, before). Smooth transitions unless rhythm demands otherwise.

## Contextual Reactions

Anchor reactions in the specific scene—tie them to an object, texture, sound, line of dialogue, or physical consequence. Avoid reactions in isolation.

**Generic:** *"A small smile touched her lips."*

Reactions should depend on the specific people, objects, and conditions of this scene.

If a reaction could be copy-pasted into a different story unchanged, it is too generic. Rewrite so it depends on the specific people, objects, and conditions of this scene.

## Character Initiative

## Core Dialogue Guidelines

# Ernest Hemingway Style
## The Iceberg Theory: What is left unsaid carries more weight than what is written.

[Core Philosophy]
Hemingway believed that a writer who truly understands their subject can omit crucial elements, and the reader will feel them more strongly than if they were stated. The dignity of movement of an iceberg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water.

[Signature Techniques]

◆ Radical Economy of Language
- Use short, declarative sentences. Subject-verb-object. No adverbs.
- Cut every word that doesn't earn its place. Then cut more.
- If you can remove a word without losing meaning, remove it.
- Prefer Anglo-Saxon words over Latinate ones: "big" not "enormous," "old" not "antiquated"

◆ The Iceberg Principle (Theory of Omission)
- Never state what can be implied
- Emotional content lives beneath the surface
- The more traumatic the event, the less directly you describe it
- Characters talk around the thing they cannot say
- What characters avoid discussing reveals more than what they say

◆ Dialogue as Subtext
- Characters speak in short, clipped exchanges
- They deflect, change subjects, talk about small things
- The real conversation happens between the lines
- No dialogue tags except "said" — let the words carry emotion
- Repetition in dialogue shows psychological weight

◆ Concrete Sensory Detail
- Ground every scene in physical reality: the weight of a pack, the taste of wine
- Describe what can be seen, touched, tasted, heard
- Weather and landscape mirror internal states without comment
- Food and drink are rituals that anchor characters in the present

◆ Masculine Stoicism
- Characters endure without complaint
- Pain is acknowledged through action, not reflection
- Grace under pressure — "courage is grace under pressure"
- Emotion expressed through physical gesture: lighting a cigarette, pouring a drink

◆ The Nada (Nothingness)
- An underlying awareness of meaninglessness
- Characters construct meaning through ritual and routine
- Existential dread never named, always felt
- Night and insomnia as confrontation with the void

[Structural Patterns]
- Begin in medias res
- End on an image, not an explanation
- White space between sections to indicate time jumps
- Circular structure — ending echoes beginning

[Forbidden Techniques]
- Interior monologue or stream of consciousness
- Adverbs modifying dialogue ("he said angrily")
- Explaining why characters feel what they feel
- Purple prose or elaborate metaphors
- Telling the reader what to feel

- NEVER use: thinks, knows, believes, wants, feels, realizes, understands
- These are "filter words" — lazy abstraction
- Transform every abstract concept into concrete, physical details
- Things you can smell, taste, touch, hear, see
- Show evidence. Let the reader be the jury.

FORBIDDEN: "Kenny wondered if Monica didn't like him going out at night."
REQUIRED: "The mornings after Kenny stayed out past the last bus, Monica would only put her own coffee in the microwave. Never his."

FORBIDDEN: "Adam knew Gwen liked him."
REQUIRED: "Between classes, Gwen was always leaned against his locker. She'd shove off with one foot, leaving a black heel mark on the metal, but also the smell of her perfume. The combination lock would still be warm from her ass."

◆ Bury "Is" and "Has"
- The most worthless verbs in existence
- Hide descriptions inside actions
- Make details move

FORBIDDEN: "Ann's eyes are blue." / "Ann has blue eyes."
REQUIRED: "Ann coughed and waved one hand past her face, clearing the cigarette smoke from her eyes, blue eyes, before she smiled."

◆ Repetitive Mantras
- Key phrases repeat throughout the narrative
- Builds rhythm and obsession
- Each repetition adds or subtracts meaning
- "I am Jack's complete lack of surprise"
- Creates hypnotic, cult-like cadence

◆ Never Leave Characters Alone
- Alone, characters "think" and "wonder" — that's lazy
- Throw them into crowds, interactions, conflict
- Solitary worry is abstract. Social friction is concrete.
- Get them with another character FAST

◆ The Chorus
- Recurring refrains that structure the narrative
- Facts and statistics presented as dark poetry
- Instructional passages ("Here's how to make napalm")
- Medical or technical details rendered visceral

◆ Second-Person Address
- "You" implicates the reader
- Instructions for destruction
- The reader becomes complicit

◆ Transgressive Content
- Taboo subjects presented matter-of-factly
- Body horror without melodrama
- Consumer culture as violence
- Support groups and cults as family

[Structural Patterns]
- Begin with a hook that makes no sense yet
- Reveals that reframe everything
- Unreliable narrators lying to themselves
- Twist endings that were there all along
- Circular structure returning to opening

[Forbidden Techniques]
- Filter words (thinks, knows, feels, realizes)
- Passive characters alone with thoughts
- Abstract emotional descriptions
- Sincere sentimentality
- Comfortable moral positions

[Writing Example]
The first rule of surviving your office job: Never make eye contact in the elevator.

This is because eye contact creates obligation. Eye contact means you might have to say something. You might have to acknowledge that both of you are riding a metal box to jobs you hate, to cubicles that smell like microwaved fish and quiet desperation.

The second rule: Always take the stairs.

Your heart rate elevated by four flights is proof you're still alive. The burning in your lungs is evidence your body still works. The sweat soaking through your shirt under your suit jacket is the only honest thing that will happen to you all day.

I am Jack's elevated heart rate.

In the stairwell, I pass Richard from Accounting. Richard has a coffee stain on his tie. The tie is yellow. The stain is brown. Richard's wife bought him that tie for Christmas. I know this because Richard told everyone in the break room. Richard tells everyone everything because Richard is lonely and doesn't know how else to make human connection except through the sharing of small, meaningless details about his meaningless life.

Richard doesn't look at me.

This is the third rule: We don't talk about the stairwell.

# 川端康成式 · Yasunari Kawabata Style
## 感覚の詩と物哀の美学 (Poetry of Sensation and the Aesthetics of Mono no Aware)

[Core Philosophy - 核心理念]
Kawabata captures fleeting beauty through extreme sensory precision. His work embodies 物哀 (mono no aware) — the bittersweet awareness of impermanence. Stories are not plot-driven but sensation-driven, creating atmosphere through accumulated impressions.

[Grounding Concepts - 日本語キーワード]
- 物哀 (mono no aware): The pathos of things; awareness of impermanence
- 幽玄 (yūgen): Profound grace; beauty found in shadows
- 侘び寂び (wabi-sabi): Beauty in imperfection and transience
- 間 (ma): Negative space; the pause between
- 余韻 (yoin): Lingering resonance; aftertaste of emotion
- 儚さ (hakanasa): Ephemerality; the fleeting nature of beauty

[Signature Techniques - 核心技巧]

◆ Extreme Sensory Focus (感官放大鏡)
- Magnify minute sensory details
- Temperature of skin, quality of light, fragrance in air
- Ask: What does the character's skin feel? What sounds exist at the edge of hearing?
- Light changes throughout scene like breathing

◆ Seasonal Consciousness (季節の意識)
- 雪 (yuki): Snow as silence and purity
- 桜 (sakura): Cherry blossoms as fleeting beauty
- 紅葉 (momiji): Autumn leaves as graceful decline
- 蝉 (semi): Cicadas as summer's urgent voice

◆ Capturing the Instant (瞬間の定格)
- Focus on single moments rather than events
- Not "what happened" but "what was perceived"
- "At four in the morning, I discovered the camellia had bloomed in the night"
- Discovery over summary

◆ Object Imagery (物の意象)
- 鏡 (kagami): Mirror — reflection, self, illusion
- 茶碗 (chawan): Tea bowl — ritual, imperfection, touch
- 白粉 (oshiroi): White powder — beauty, artifice, tradition
- 着物 (kimono): Clothing as identity and constraint

◆ The Art of Omission (省略の芸術)
- Never explain character emotions
- Convey through small gestures: adjusting a collar, a glance away
- Unfinished sentences carry weight
- What is not said echoes loudest

◆ Non-Linear Impression (非連続性叙事)
- Fragment narrative into impressions
- Jump without transition

- A does/says something unreasonable

- Characters know they're in a story

- Never directly express feelings

# Stream of Consciousness
## The Mind's Unedited Broadcast

[Core Philosophy]
Stream of consciousness captures thought before it's organized. Associations jump without logical bridges. Memory intrudes on present. The reader doesn't observe the character thinking — they think alongside them, caught in the current of consciousness.

[Key Principles]

◆ Associative Logic
- Thoughts connect by feeling, not reason
- A blue curtain triggers memory of grandmother's dress
- That memory triggers guilt about not visiting
- Guilt triggers hunger (emotion → physical)
- One thought spawns the next without explanation

◆ No Filtering
- Include the "irrelevant" thoughts
- The grocery list interrupting grief
- Sexual thoughts during a funeral
- The mind doesn't curate; neither should the text

◆ Subjective Reality
- Everything filtered through this one consciousness
- "Objective" description doesn't exist
- The room is threatening because the character feels threatened
- Environment = emotional state externalized

◆ Time Collapse
- Past/present/future blur
- A moment can contain decades
- Memory doesn't announce itself
- "He walked through the door and he was six years old again"

◆ Fragment and Flow
- Incomplete sentences allowed
- Thought trails off—
- New thought begins mid-
- Because that's how minds actually work

◆ Punctuation as Rhythm
- Long sentences for flow states
- Fragments for disruption
- Em dashes for interruption—

- Minimal periods in intense passages

- Did she say that or did I imagine it

- Smell → memory (most powerful)

- Senses as doorways to the unconscious

◆ Repetition

# Stage Play / Dialogue-Driven
## The Spoken Word Carries All

[Core Philosophy]
In this technique, dialogue IS the story. Narration becomes stage direction — minimal, functional, revealing only what speech cannot. Characters are defined entirely by how they speak. Subtext lives in what isn't said. The reader becomes audience, watching a performance.

[Key Principles]

◆ Dialogue as Action
- Characters DO things through speech
- Confession, accusation, seduction, betrayal
- "I love you" can be attack or surrender
- Speech acts: every line accomplishes something

◆ Minimal Narration
- Only physical action necessary for understanding
- "She stood" not "She stood, feeling overwhelmed"
- No internal thoughts unless absolutely required
- Stage directions, not psychological analysis

◆ Subtext
- What characters say ≠ what they mean
- The real conversation happens underneath
- "Nice weather" can mean "I hate you"
- Readers decode; writers encode

◆ Character Voice
- Each speaker absolutely distinct
- Vocabulary, rhythm, sentence length
- Education, class, region audible
- Could identify speaker without tags

[Technique Tools]

◆ Dialogue Formatting
- Each speaker's line on new paragraph
- Minimal or no dialogue tags
- Physical action as attribution
- "She set down her cup. 'And then what?'"

◆ The Pause
- "[Silence.]" or "[Beat.]" carries meaning
- What isn't said resonates
- Interruption and overlap
- Timing is everything

◆ Enter/Exit
- Characters arriving changes dynamics
- Characters leaving ends scenes
- The door opening, the phone ringing
- Theatrical entrances and exits

◆ Compression
- Every line earns its place
- Cut anything that doesn't work
- A play is 90 minutes; economy is mandatory
- If it can be cut, cut it

*"You think this is about money?" He stood, and the room contracted. "You think I built this—" gesture at everything, the office, the empire, the life "—because I wanted money? Money is a byproduct. Money is what falls off the table while you're doing the actual work."*

- Let speeches run long
- Build rhetorical structure
- Use the pause
- Land the final line

- Quick volleys, short lines

- Interruptions and completions
- Callback to earlier lines

- Wordplay and double meanings
- Deliberate misinterpretation
- Escalating one-upmanship
- The unexpected pivot

- What they're really saying underneath
- Flirtation disguised as argument
- Affection expressed through mockery
- Testing each other through wit

*"You're staring."*

**Reference points:**
- **Raymond Chandler**: "She had a face like a Sunday School picnic and a mind like a steel trap"
- **Dashiell Hammett** (The Maltese Falcon): Sam Spade's verbal knife-work
- **Chinatown**: "Forget it, Jake"
- **Sin City**: Hyper-noir, every line quotable
- **Brick**: Noir dialogue in a high school setting
- **L.A. Confidential**: Period-perfect hardboiled exchanges

**Hardboiled mechanics:**

**The voice:**
- First-person internal monologue energy (even in dialogue)
- Metaphors and similes, always sharp
- Understatement as default
- Wry, dark humor as armor

- Never show weakness directly
- Threats delivered casually
- Violence mentioned matter-of-factly
- Sentiment hidden under cynicism

**Rhythm:**
- Mix short declarative punches with longer metaphorical observations
- Land the line
- Let the good ones breathe

**What this looks like:**

- Everyone's got an angle

*Can be combined with any dialogue ratio.*

Elevated, polished speech. Characters who choose words with precision, who speak in complete sentences, who wield formality as either armor or weapon. Not stiff—controlled. Not cold—deliberate.

**Reference points:**
- **Downton Abbey**: Class expressed through speech patterns
- **The Crown**: Royal restraint, every word weighed
- **Hannibal**: Lecter's weaponized courtesy
- **Remains of the Day**: Formality as emotional armor
- **Pride and Prejudice**: Period-appropriate precision

**Formal speech markers:**

**Structure:**
- Complete sentences, rarely fragments
- Complex syntax when warranted
- Proper grammar throughout
- No contractions (or rare, deliberate ones)

**Vocabulary:**
- Precise word choice
- Elevated without being archaic
- Technical terminology where appropriate
- Euphemism and understatement

**Rhythm:**
- Measured pacing
- Pauses for emphasis
- Weight on key words
- Control over emotional leakage

**What this looks like:**

*"I appreciate your candor, though I find myself questioning its timing."*

*"You believe I should have spoken sooner."*

*"I believe you have spoken precisely when it served your purposes. Which suggests those purposes have shifted."*

*He inclined his head—acknowledgment, not agreement. "You are not incorrect. Circumstances have... evolved."*

*"Evolved." She let the word hang. "An interesting choice of verb. Evolution implies natural process. This feels rather more deliberate."*

*"Perhaps. But the outcome remains the same. We find ourselves in need of each other's cooperation."*

*"We find ourselves in need of nothing. You find yourself in need of my assistance. The distinction matters."*

**When formality works:**
- Aristocratic or high-society settings
- Professional/legal/diplomatic contexts
- Power dynamics where control matters
- Characters using speech as shield

**Formality can crack:**
- Under extreme emotion (the mask slips)
- In genuine intimacy (when guard drops)
- Strategic informality (deliberate disarmament)

**Avoid:**
- Stiffness without purpose
- Thesaurus-diving for complexity
- Formality from characters who wouldn't use it
- Losing character voice in pursuit of polish::## Casual & Slang (Style Modifier)

*Can be combined with any dialogue ratio.*

How people actually talk. Contractions, fragments, slang, filler words, profanity where it fits. Characters grounded in authentic speech patterns—no polish, no pretension, just real.

**Reference points:**
- **The Wire**: Baltimore street vernacular, authentic to environment
- **Trainspotting**: Scottish slang, class-specific speech
- **Superbad**: Teenage male friendship speech patterns
- **Clerks**: Aimless twenty-something rambling
- **Good Will Hunting**: Boston working-class authenticity

**Casual speech markers:**

**Structure:**
- Sentence fragments are fine
- Contractions everywhere (don't, won't, can't)
- Interrupted and incomplete thoughts
- Trailing off when the point is made

**Vocabulary:**
- Common words over fancy ones
- Profanity as punctuation (when appropriate)
- Slang tied to character/setting
- Filler words ("like," "you know," "basically")

**Rhythm:**
- Quick, unpolished exchanges
- Overlap and interruption
- Casual pacing
- Thinking out loud

**What this looks like:**

*"So wait—you're telling me he just walked in? Like, just... walked in?"*

*"Yeah. Door wasn't even locked."*

*"Dude."*

*"I know."*

*"That's so—I mean, what the hell was he thinking?"*

*Character shrugged. "Honestly? I don't think he was. Thinking, I mean. He just—you know how he gets when he's pissed. Logic kinda exits the building."*

*"Yeah, but still. That's a whole new level of stupid, even for him."*

*"Tell me about it." She grabbed her jacket. "Anyway, I gotta bail. You good to handle this?"*

*"Handle what, exactly? You haven't told me anything useful."*

*"The useful part's on his phone. Get it, check his messages, figure out who he was talking to. The rest—" She waved vaguely. "—make it up as you go."*

**Slang considerations:**
- Match era and setting (no modern slang in historical)
- Match region and culture (slang is specific)
- Match character background (earned, not costume)
- Don't overdo it (a little goes far)

**Profanity guidelines:**
- When it fits character and context
- Not every sentence needs it
- Varies by character (some never, some always)
- Match intensity to situation

**Avoid:**
- Slang as caricature
- Forcing casual speech on formal characters
- Outdated slang unless period-appropriate
- Casual = unintelligent (it doesn't)}}
