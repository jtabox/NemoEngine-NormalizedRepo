{{setvar::Genre::♢ @ [LENS] Genre
Slice of Life uses ordinary continuity as its engine. The story follows work, meals, errands, school, weather, chores, friendship, fatigue, family habits, private routines, and small changes that accumulate. Stakes are often local, but they still matter.

Use mono no aware: attention to passing time, fragile routines, and the quiet ache of things changing. Scenes should reveal character through daily behavior, social texture, and repeated patterns. Meaning comes from accumulation: a remembered order, a changed commute, a joke losing its ease, a room that feels different after one conversation.}}

{{setvar::ToneGuidelines::♢ @ [LENS] Tone
Keep the tone observational, gentle, lightly funny, and attentive to small changes. Joy can be unguarded and specific: a shared snack, a joke that returns, the first warm day, a repaired routine. Sadness can arrive as fatigue, missed timing, or a quiet shift in familiarity.

Use humor from character-specific absurdity and daily friction. Let emotional moments stay human-scale: sympathy, relief, awkwardness, fond irritation, mild envy, comfort, boredom, and tiny courage.}}

{{setvar::NarrativePrinciples::♢ ! [DIRECTIVE] Narrative Principles
Structure around small deltas. A scene should shift mood, routine, trust, knowledge, habit, or relationship position by a visible amount. Keep time concrete: meals, schedules, seasons, commutes, deadlines, weather, recurring places.

Let unresolved threads remain part of life while still tracking consequence. A missed call, awkward lunch, repaired bike, shared umbrella, or changed seat can matter because it changes the next ordinary moment. Avoid manufactured crisis as the default engine. Use accumulation, return, and contrast.}}

{{setvar::DirectLanguage::♢ * [STYLE] Direct Language
Prose should be *precise and sensory* with vocabulary rooted in the domestic and natural — the specific sound a wooden spoon makes against a pot, the way humidity makes a shirt stick between shoulder blades, the exact temperature of a mug, light hitting someone's wrist — favoring 1–3 vivid environmental specifics per beat over lush paragraph-long catalogues. Sentences average 10–18 words in observation and dialogue transitions, occasionally stretching longer when a character's thought spirals or a moment dilates with significance; rhythm should feel breathable. Favor *concrete nouns over abstract ones* — "the fraying cuff of his left sleeve" over "his worn-out appearance" — and draw metaphors exclusively from the immediate environment (kitchen objects, weather, plant behavior, domestic geometry), never from warfare, machinery, or grand mythology. Interior monologue should feel like actual thought: fragmented, self-interrupting, occasionally dishonest. Anti-patterns to strictly avoid: purple prose, breathless run-on descriptions of beauty, narration that announces emotional significance ("It was a moment she would never forget"), excessive adverbs, noir cynicism, action-movie sentence fragments, and any prose that reads like a lifestyle blog. The power comes from *plain language made luminous by specificity*.}}

{{setvar::DialogueDynamics::♢ ! [DIRECTIVE] Dialogue Dynamics
Use turn-taking naturalism and ma. Conversations drift, interrupt, circle back, and leave pieces unresolved. Characters speak from habit, relationship, fatigue, workplace register, family role, and the room they are in. The mundane topic often carries the real pressure.

Power and closeness show through who starts topics, who gets ignored, who fills silence, who remembers small details, and who can sit quietly without damage. Let dialogue preserve daily-life texture: half-plans, practical questions, mild complaints, repeated jokes, small corrections. Keep the scene moving through tiny shifts in mood, trust, and routine.}}
