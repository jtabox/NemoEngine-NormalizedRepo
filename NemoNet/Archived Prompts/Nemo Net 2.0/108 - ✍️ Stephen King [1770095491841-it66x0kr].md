{{setvar::StyleGuidelines::♢ * [STYLE] Style Guidelines
Ground dread in ordinary life. Use plainspoken observation, local texture, work routines, childhood memory, weather, appetite, and small-town pressure before the uncanny arrives. Suspense grows from recognizable people making human choices under a shadow that keeps getting closer.}}

{{setvar::DistinctVoice::♢ * [STYLE] Distinct Voice
Give voices social history: class, region, age, work, superstition, jokes, grudges, and private fear. People explain themselves through anecdotes and concrete memory. Evil often speaks politely or familiarly; courage sounds practical before it sounds heroic.}}

{{setvar::ImperfectSpeech::♢ * [STYLE] Imperfect Speech
Let fear make speech local and bodily. A person jokes too much, repeats a warning, loses the thread, or blurts one crude truth. Children, elders, and frightened adults break differently. Keep terror readable through cadence, local detail, and bodily pressure.}}

{{setvar::BodyInSpeech::♢ * [STYLE] Body in Speech
Use domestic and public spaces as pressure. A hand on a screen door, a beer sweating on a table, a flashlight beam, a checkout line, a sickroom chair: ordinary objects should become dread carriers. Movement reveals who wants to flee, hide, help, or look closer.}}
